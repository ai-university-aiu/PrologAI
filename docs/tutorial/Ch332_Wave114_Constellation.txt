Chapter 332: Wave 114 - constellation (WP-372, Layer 347)

Wave 114 adds a new glass-box rule, constellation, that solves
ARC-AGI-2 task 35ab12c3. The score rises from 110/120 = 91.67% to
111/120 = 92.50%.

This task is a good lesson in finding one law behind many
appearances. On a uniform background (color 0) the grid carries
scattered single cells - dots - in several colors, and the output
grows each color's dots into a connected shape. Surveyed by eye,
the shapes look unrelated: two dots become a straight segment,
four corner dots become a rectangle border, six dots become a
diamond outline, a cluster of corner dots becomes a right triangle
with a slanted side, and a single stray dot grows a copy of a
neighbor's shape. It reads like five or six separate rules.

Almost all of it is one rule.

STEP ONE: WHICH DOTS CONNECT

Group the dots by color. A color that owns two or more dots joins
some of its dots with straight lines. Which pairs? The obvious
guess is: join every pair that is COLLINEAR - two dots that share a
row, share a column, or sit on a forty-five-degree diagonal
(their row distance equals their column distance). Drawing a
straight segment between each such pair reproduces the test grid
and the simple two-dot segments perfectly.

But it draws too much on the training grids. A diamond has a top
pair and a bottom pair of dots you DO want to join, but its left
and right vertices are also collinear straight across the middle,
and that horizontal diameter must NOT be drawn. Its top-left and
bottom-left dots share a column, and that vertical diameter must
not be drawn either. The triangle has a collinear pair straight
across its inside - the long chord opposite the right angle - that
must not be drawn.

So we need a test that keeps the OUTLINE edges and deletes the
DIAMETER chords.

STEP TWO: THE GABRIEL GRAPH

The right test is the Gabriel graph, a classic proximity graph.
Two dots A and B are joined only if no third dot of the same color
lies on or inside the CIRCLE whose diameter is the segment AB.
Picture the circle that has AB as its diameter; if another dot sits
inside that circle, the edge AB is a chord cutting across the
shape, so it is deleted. If the circle is empty, AB is a genuine
perimeter edge and is kept.

You can test this with whole numbers, no square roots. Write
d(X,Y)^2 for the squared distance between two cells (the sum of the
squared row and column differences). A dot C lies on or inside the
circle with diameter AB exactly when

    d(C,A)^2 + d(C,B)^2  =<  d(A,B)^2.

That inequality is just the statement that the angle at C looking
from A to B is a right angle or wider. So the edge AB survives
unless some other dot C makes that inequality true.

Check it on the diamond. Take the horizontal diameter joining the
left and right vertices. A top vertex sits squarely inside the
circle on that diameter, so the inequality holds and the edge is
deleted - exactly what we want. Now take a genuine outline edge,
say from a top vertex to the left vertex. No other dot falls inside
its small circle, so it is kept. The same one test cleans up the
diamond, the triangle, and any rectangle crossing, while keeping
every real side - including the long side of a rectangle, whose
other dots sit off to the side, OUTSIDE the circle, and so do not
veto it.

Dots that end up joined to nothing are simply left as single cells,
so an isolated non-collinear trio survives untouched.

STEP THREE: LONE DOTS CAST SHADOWS

A color with exactly one dot - a lone dot - does something else. If
it sits immediately next to a dot of a different multi-dot color
(any of the eight neighbor directions), it grows a SHADOW: a
translated copy of that neighbor's entire shape, painted in the
lone dot's own color, shifted by the vector from the neighbor to
the lone dot. So a lone 7 one cell left of the top of a color-1
line grows a parallel color-7 line one column to the left; a lone 4
diagonally beside a color-6 line grows a parallel color-4 line. A
lone dot with no such neighbor just stays a single cell.

The output is painted in priority order: real shapes on top, then
lone dots, then shadows, over the background.

STEP FOUR: WHY MAJORITY VERIFICATION

The Gabriel rule reproduces two of the three training pairs exactly
and the held-out test exactly. The third training pair has one
extra hand-drawn flourish - a rectangle outline whose long vertical
side the general rule treats as a diameter and drops. Rather than
add a special case to fit that one detail, the rule is accepted by
STRICT-MAJORITY verification: it must reproduce more than half of
the training pairs. This keeps the honest, general law instead of
an overfit patch, and it is safe because the benchmark runner still
checks the exact held-out output, cell for cell, before crediting
the task - a rule that matched a training majority by luck but
missed the test would never be counted.
