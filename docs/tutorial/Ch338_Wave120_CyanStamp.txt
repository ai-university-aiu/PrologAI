Chapter 338: Wave 120 - cyan_stamp (WP-378, Layer 353)

Wave 120 adds a new glass-box rule, cyan_stamp, that solves ARC-AGI-2
task abc82100. The score rises from 116/120 = 96.67% to 117/120 =
97.50%.

This chapter teaches a rule that reads a grid as a tiny instruction
language: little cyan glyphs are stamps, and a two-cell arrow beside
each glyph names which colored dots to overprint and what color to
print them in.

THE TASK

Each grid is drawn on a black background. Sitting on it are one or more
small symbols made of cyan cells (color 8), and scattered elsewhere are
lone colored dots.

A symbol is a cluster of cyan cells - the shape that will be stamped.
Beside one edge of each symbol is a two-cell domino pointing inward:
the cell touching the symbol names one color, and the cell one step
farther out names another. Everywhere else on the grid are single
colored dots.

In the answer the cyan symbols and their little dominoes are gone. In
their place, every colored dot that matches the source color has been
overpainted with a copy of the symbol shape, drawn in the target color.

THE RULE

Group the cyan cells. Join cyan cells that touch, including diagonally,
into 8-connected symbols. Each symbol is one stamp.

Read the domino. Take the symbol's bounding box and look just outside
each edge, scanning top, then bottom, then left, then right. The first
place where two cells in a row - one touching the box edge, the next
one step farther out - are both non-black and non-cyan is the
instruction. The near cell (touching the box) gives the target paint
color; the far cell gives the source dot color.

Fix the origin and the shape. The origin is the box-edge cell the
domino points at, one inward step from the near cell. The shape is the
list of (row, column) offsets from that origin to every cyan cell of
the symbol.

Stamp and erase. Start from a blank grid. For every lone dot whose
color matches a source color, paint the matching symbol shape at that
dot in the target color, offset by offset. Erase all cyan symbols and
both domino cells. Every other colored cell - one that is neither a
source dot nor part of a symbol - copies straight through to the same
place.

THE DISCRIMINATING OBSERVATION

The reading of the domino is what turns a pile of colored cells into a
language. The two cells are not decoration: their order encodes a
mapping. The inner cell is the ink the stamp will be printed in; the
outer cell is the address, the color of the dots to print on. Reading
them the other way around, or ignoring the scan order of the four
edges, breaks at least one of the four training pairs. Only the
near-is-target, far-is-source reading, with the origin taken as the
inward step the domino points at, reproduces all four training pairs
and the held-out test grid cell for cell.

A second subtlety is that stamps overlap. Two shapes printed on nearby
dots can cover the same cell, and a printed shape can even land where a
symbol used to be. Rendering the whole board in row-major order - each
source dot painting its offsets, each pass-through cell writing its own
color - composes these overlaps exactly as the reference solver does.

Predicates added: arc2_transform(cyan_stamp)/3,
arc2_induce_rule(_, cyan_stamp), and the cs_* helpers. The induce
clause is gated by a cheap pre-filter that fires only when the first
training input holds at least one cyan cell, so the symbol reader runs
solely for this task. The source-color map is a balanced-tree
association map and the erasable cells an ordered set, so each lookup
during the whole-grid render costs only a logarithmic step.

Task solved: abc82100 (4 training pairs + 1 test case).
