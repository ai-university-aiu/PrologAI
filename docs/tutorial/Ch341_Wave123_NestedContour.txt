This chapter teaches a rule that reads a grid as a nest of closed loops,
one inside another, and redraws the same nesting as a clean skeleton of
minimal rectangles - a shrinky-dink that keeps the shape of the
containment and throws away every original size and position.

THE TASK

The board is a two-color grid: a single line color drawn as big closed
loops, and a background color filling the areas between and inside them.
The loops nest - each loop encloses an area, that area may hold a smaller
loop, and so on down to an innermost region. Several separate nests may
share the grid.

In the output the same nesting is redrawn at the smallest possible scale.
Every region becomes a one-cell-thick outline, the innermost region
collapses to a single dot, and where several nests sit side by side they
snap to a shared row or column so their innermost dots line up. All the
original distances and coordinates are discarded; only which region
contains which, and how deep each sits, survives.

THE RULE

Step 1 - Regions. The background is the most common color. Split the grid
into connected regions: each background area as a four-connected
component, and each stretch of the line color as an eight-connected
component. A thin loop line touches diagonally; an area spreads
orthogonally.

Step 2 - Containment tree. Tag each cell with its region and make regions
that share a four-neighbor edge adjacent. Breadth-first search outward
from every background region touching the grid border (the outer sea,
depth zero) gives each region a depth and a parent. The result is a tree:
the sea contains the outer loops, each loop contains an area, each area
contains the next loop, down to the innermost region.

Step 3 - Render. Render each top-level region recursively. A leaf region
(nothing inside it) becomes a single cell of its color - the innermost
dot. An internal region renders its children, arranges them, and wraps
the arranged block in a one-cell-thick frame of its own color. Because
loops and areas alternate down the tree, the frames alternate line color
and background, producing the nested outlines.

Step 4 - Arrange and pad. When a region holds several children, cluster
their innermost-dot anchors into row bands and column bands (two anchors
join a band when they sit within three cells of each other), then align
each child so its anchor lands on the shared band line, filling the gaps
with the parent's color. Finally, if a lone innermost dot ends up
touching a grid edge, pad that edge with one row or column of background.

THE DISCRIMINATING OBSERVATION

The heart of the rule is the separation of nesting topology from absolute
geometry. The output keeps only the containment tree - who is inside
whom, and how deep - and rebuilds the picture at minimal scale. Every
original size, wall thickness, and coordinate is thrown away and
regenerated from the tree alone. Sibling nests are then re-registered to
one another by their innermost dots, not by their old positions, which is
why the anchor-alignment step is essential: it is the only thing that
decides where two shrunk nests sit once their original coordinates are
gone.
