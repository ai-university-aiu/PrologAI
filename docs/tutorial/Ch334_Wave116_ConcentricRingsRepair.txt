Chapter 334: Wave 116 - concentric_rings repair (WP-374, Layer 349)

Wave 116 is a one-line repair of an existing glass-box rule,
concentric_rings, that finally solves ARC-AGI-2 task 13e47133. The
score rises from 112/120 = 93.33% to 113/120 = 94.17%.

This chapter is a lesson in how a rule can pass every training
example and still hide a wrong choice of distance metric.

THE TASK

Each grid is a background field walled by a divider color - the
most frequent non-background color - into regions. Every walled
region is filled with concentric rectangular rings, like the
contour lines of a hill. A cell's ring index is its distance from
the region boundary, where the boundary is the grid edge or any
cell touching a divider wall: the outer ring is index zero and the
index grows inward. Small single-cell markers inside a region name
the color cycle - a marker at ring K colors ring K, empty rings
default to background, and the cycle repeats outward.

In the two training pairs the divider draws one plain rectangle. In
the held-out test it draws several NESTED boxes, one inside another.

THE FAULT

The solver already reproduced both training pairs exactly, yet it
missed the test by twenty-nine cells - every one a single color off,
a uniform shift of exactly one ring. When a rule nails training but
slips on the test by a uniform one-ring shift, the color logic is
not the suspect; the geometry is.

The ring distance was computed by a multi-source breadth-first
search from the boundary, expanding with FOUR-connected steps - up,
down, left, right - which measures Manhattan distance. For a plain
rectangle that is correct: Manhattan distance from the four edges is
exactly min(top, bottom, left, right), the concentric-ring index, so
both training rectangles came out right. But where two nested boxes
meet, the region has a CONCAVE corner, and Manhattan distance cannot
cut across a corner. It walks around and arrives one step late,
over-counting the ring by one along the diagonal - the twenty-nine
cells.

THE REPAIR

Let the distance propagate with EIGHT-connected steps, which
measures Chebyshev distance. Chebyshev distance from the boundary
keeps the ring index constant along a box corner, so the rings
become true concentric rectangles even around concave corners.

The change is a single call: inside arc2_cri_bfs_, the ring-distance
breadth-first search, the neighbour helper moves from
arc2_cri_nbrs_ (four-connected) to arc2_cri_nbrs8_ (eight-connected).
The region flood-fill and the boundary test stay four-connected and
are untouched.

WHY IT IS SAFE

The fault was invisible in training precisely because both training
regions are simple rectangles, where Manhattan and Chebyshev
boundary-distance coincide. Only the test's nested boxes create the
concave corners that separate the two metrics. After the repair, all
twenty-nine miscolored test cells are corrected, both training pairs
still reproduce exactly, and a full run of the 120-task benchmark
shows the other 119 tasks unchanged.

THE LESSON

Concentric rectangles are a Chebyshev object, not a Manhattan one.
When a solver reproduces training exactly yet misses the test by a
uniform one-ring shift, suspect the distance metric before the color
logic, and remember that easy training geometry can make two
different metrics agree.
