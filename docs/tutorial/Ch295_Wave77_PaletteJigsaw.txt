Chapter 295: Wave 77 - palette_jigsaw (WP-335, Layer 310)

palette_jigsaw solves ARC-AGI-2 task f560132c. Each input grid (20x20
in training, 26x26 in the test) scatters four pieces over the
background: three single-color connected shapes and one key piece
whose body embeds a 2-by-2 block of four foreign palette colors in
place of four of its own cells. The total number of piece cells is
always a perfect square: 64 and 100 across the two training grids and
144 in the test grid. The task is to assemble the pieces into the
N-by-N square, rotating each piece by quarter turns so the pieces
interlock with no gaps and no overlaps, with the key piece covering
the top-left corner, and then to repaint every piece with the palette
color of the output corner it covers.

Rule: the palette carrier anchors the corner. The key piece pins the
top-left corner - the same corner where its own palette reading
begins - and under that pin the square has exactly one assembly.

Algorithm:

The background is the most common cell value. All non-background
cells are grouped into 8-connected components over mixed colors, and
exactly four components must emerge; exactly one - the key - carries
more than one distinct color. The key's body color is its most
frequent cell color, and the four cells differing from the body form
a 2-by-2 palette block read as top-left, top-right, bottom-left, and
bottom-right. Each piece is normalized to its bounding-box origin and
converted to a shape mask tagged with a piece index, the key being
index 1 with its palette cells counted as part of its shape; the
summed cell counts must equal N * N, fixing the output side N. The
Wave 76 exact-cover engine then tiles the board with the four
quarter-turn rotation variants of each mask, and the accepted tiling
has the key covering the top-left corner and the four corners covered
by four distinct pieces. Finally each piece is repainted with the
palette color of the corner it covers - the key takes the palette's
top-left color - and the recolored cells are painted row by row into
the output square. The induction pre-filter requires the first output
to be strictly smaller than the first input in both dimensions,
exactly square, with four distinct colors in its four corners, then
verifies all pairs with forall.

The lesson: the corner anchor generalizes from a colored stud to a
role-bearing piece. Without the key-corner pin each grid admits
exactly four distinct-corner tilings - the four global rotations of
one and the same assembly - and only one of them recolors correctly;
with the pin, every grid admits exactly one tiling, confirmed by
exhaustive enumeration. A second lesson: the published human-solver
walkthrough (quadrant roles from centroid offsets with fixed per-role
rotations) does not survive contact with the data - the
piece-to-corner assignment ignores the input quadrants and the
rotations vary freely - so the anchored jigsaw search is the real
rule, and the entire Wave 76 pf_* engine is reused unmodified.

Key predicates:
  pj_multi_/1          - component carries more than one distinct color
  pj_majority_/2       - most frequent color in a color list
  pj_mask_/4           - normalized piece mask tagged with piece index
  pj_color_/9          - piece index mapped to its corner palette color
  pf_comps_/2          - 8-connected mixed-color components (reused)
  pf_variants_/2       - the four quarter-turn rotations (reused)
  pf_solve_/3          - N-by-N exact-cover tiling search (reused)
  pf_render_/3         - placed cells painted as the output square (reused)
  arc2_transform(palette_jigsaw, +Grid, -Out)
