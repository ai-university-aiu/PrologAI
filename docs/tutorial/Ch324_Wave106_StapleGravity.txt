Chapter 324: Wave 106 - staple_gravity (WP-364, Layer 339)

staple_gravity solves ARC-AGI-2 task b6f77b65. The task gives
five training pairs and one test case, all on 12x12 grids. The
corner cell at row zero, column zero carries a lone indicator
color, and the rest of the grid holds a standing scaffold of
colored pieces: vertical poles, horizontal bars, and single
wedged cells, interlocked so tightly that pieces hide cells
inside each other. The indicator names the piece color to
delete. When no piece carries that color the output is the
unchanged input - the first training pair shows exactly this.
Otherwise the named pieces vanish and the survivors settle under
gravity, each rigid piece falling straight down until something
holds it again.

Rule: read the indicator color in the corner; group every other
foreground cell into same-color 4-connected components; split
each component into rigid pieces - maximal vertical runs of at
least two cells are poles, the leftover horizontal runs are
bars, and a bar welds onto a pole as its foot only when one of
its ends touches the pole's bottom cell; extend every pole
whose bottom sits directly above a foreign bar cell one hidden
cell into that bar; delete the pieces of the indicator color;
then settle - a piece is supported when it touches the bottom
row, when a supported piece owns the cell directly below one of
its cells, when it shares a cell with a supported piece, or
when a horizontal segment of it has both outside flanks inside
supported pieces, the supported set grows as a least fixpoint,
and every unsupported piece falls one row per tick until the
whole scaffold is supported; finally redraw the scene with all
vertical cells first and all horizontal cells after, so bars
sit in front of the pole bottoms impaled inside them.

The engine reads the scene with sg_cells_ and floods same-color
components with sg_flood_. sg_vsplit_ and sg_vext_ cut the
maximal vertical runs, sg_hsplit_ and sg_hext_ group the
leftovers into bars, and sg_weld_ with sg_foot_ welds a foot
bar onto the pole whose bottom cell it flanks. sg_impale_ pushes
each pole bottom one hidden cell into a foreign bar beneath it,
which is how a piece can show seven cells in an input and eight
in its output. sg_support_fix_ grows the supported set through
the four conditions in sg_supported_, sg_occ_, and sg_pinched_,
and sg_settle_ ticks sg_drop_ over the unsupported pieces until
the scaffold stands still. sg_render_ paints vertical cells,
then horizontal cells, onto a fresh background with the corner
indicator restored. The induce clause dispatches early, directly
after dot_crawler, requiring equal first-pair heights, a nonzero
corner indicator, an otherwise empty top row, an empty second
row, and the same output corner, then verifying every training
pair.

The lesson of the wave is to treat a stable scene as a statics
problem: every piece in the input already has a reason not to
fall, and cataloguing those reasons - bars pinched between two
poles, poles impaled one cell deep inside bars, single cells
wedged between neighbors - is what reveals the true piece
shapes. A piece that appears to flip upside down between input
and output dissolves into a pole plus a separate one-cell piece
wedged against its top, and cell counts that change between
input and output are the fingerprint of two pieces sharing one
cell. Because support grows as a fixpoint from the floor upward,
a falling piece supports nothing, and a lintel slides past a
still-falling pole to land only when both flanks have settled.
The rule passed the single-task test and the full regression on
the first compile.
