This chapter teaches a rule that reads a scatter of small blue T-shaped
markers, injects each marker's color into the region its stalk points at,
erases the markers back into the background, and finally gives the whole
board a single quarter-turn if a dark-red seven glyph is present.

THE TASK

The board scatters small blue markers shaped like the letter T: a
three-cell straight bar plus one stalk cell attached at the middle of the
bar, so the stalk points away from the bar in one of four directions.
Some Ts are drawn entirely in blue; others keep their two bar ends and
their stalk blue but hold a different color in the bar center. Separately,
the board may carry one isolated dark-red glyph shaped like a seven, or
its mirror image.

In the output each T marks a nearby region for recoloring. The region
just in front of the stalk tip is repainted: an injecting T (center holds
a non-blue color) paints that center color, while a plain all-blue T
paints the color of the field that surrounds it. Every T is then erased,
blending back into its background. Finally, if a dark-red seven glyph is
present, the whole board is given a single quarter-turn: a seven turns it
counter-clockwise, a mirror seven turns it clockwise.

THE RULE

Step 1 - Candidates. For every cell and every one of four orientations
(stalk up, down, left, or right), test for a T: the two bar ends and the
stalk must be blue, and the cell opposite the stalk must not be blue. For
each valid T derive a home color (the dominant color surrounding the four
T cells, ignoring blue) and an injection color (the center color for an
injecting T, or the surrounding color for a plain blue T).

Step 2 - Selection. Consider plain all-blue Ts first, then injecting Ts.
Accept each T only if none of its four cells was already claimed by an
earlier T, giving a disjoint set of injectors.

Step 3 - Seven search. Scan for the first isolated dark-red seven or
mirror-seven glyph - five dark-red cells in the seven shape with no other
dark-red cell orthogonally adjacent.

Step 4 - Erase. Overwrite every selected T with its home color on a
working copy, and overwrite any seven glyph with the color that surrounds
it.

Step 5 - Heal. Repair any blue cell left over after erasing by a
breadth-first search outward to its nearest non-blue neighbor, copying
that neighbor's color. Cells are healed in row-major order, so a repair
can build on repairs already made above and to the left.

Step 6 - Inject. For each T, flood-fill the region directly in front of
the stalk tip on the cleaned grid and paint it with that T's injection
color.

Step 7 - Rotate. If a seven glyph was found, rotate the whole board a
quarter turn: counter-clockwise for a left seven, clockwise for a mirror
seven.

THE DISCRIMINATING OBSERVATION

The heart of the rule is the separation of local injection from the
global rotation. The T injectors and the leftover-blue healing act
cell-locally - each T touches only the small region its stalk points at,
and each stray blue cell copies only its nearest neighbor. The seven
glyph, by contrast, is a single board-wide switch that reorients the
entire result. A solver that folded the rotation into the local painting,
or ignored the glyph, would reproduce training pairs that share one
orientation yet fail the moment the held-out test flips. Reading the
seven as a pure orientation instruction, applied once at the very end, is
what makes one rule serve every pair. A second subtlety is the
tie-breaking in the surround-color count: when two colors border a T
equally, the earliest one seen wins, matching the reference solver.
