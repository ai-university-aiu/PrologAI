Chapter 343: Structural Causal Models - The causal Pack (cf_*, Layer 358)

This chapter begins the AGI Foundations suite (WP-383 through WP-389),
seven packs added after the ARC-AGI-2 climb concluded, each supplying a
capability the AGI research literature names as necessary for general
intelligence. The first is causal reasoning.

There is a famous ladder with three rungs. Seeing: what is. Doing: what
happens if I act. Imagining: what would have happened if things had been
different. Ordinary statistics lives on rung one. Minds live on all three.
The causal pack gives PrologAI the whole ladder as glass-box operations.

A structural causal model (SCM) is a set of variables and the equations
that cause them. Here is the classic firing squad:

    ?- cf_model([
           exo(court, 0),
           eq(captain, court),
           eq(rifle_a, captain),
           eq(rifle_b, captain),
           eq(death, max(rifle_a, rifle_b))
       ], SCM).

The court order is exogenous - it comes from outside the model, with a
default of 0 (no order). Everything else is endogenous - caused by the
model itself. The captain follows the court, both riflemen follow the
captain, and the prisoner dies if either rifleman fires.

RUNG ONE - SEEING

    ?- cf_value(SCM, [court-1], death, V).
    V = 1.

Solve the equations in causal order and read any variable. The model also
answers graph questions: cf_parents, cf_ancestors, cf_descendants,
cf_path, and cf_dsep - d-separation, the test of which variables are
informationally walled off from which, given what you already know.

RUNG TWO - DOING

What if we force rifleman A to fire, with no court order? That is not an
observation - it is an intervention, written do(rifle_a = 1). The pack
performs graph surgery: rifleman A's equation is cut away and replaced by
the constant.

    ?- cf_do(SCM, [rifle_a-1], [court-0], Solution).

The prisoner dies (death is 1), but look at the rest of the solution:
captain is 0 and rifle_b is 0. Forcing a rifleman does not make the
captain retroactively give an order. Intervention flows only forward along
the causal arrows. This is the difference between seeing and doing, and no
amount of rung-one statistics can capture it.

RUNG THREE - IMAGINING

The prisoner is dead. Would he have died anyway if rifleman A had held
fire? Counterfactuals follow a three-step recipe: abduction (work out the
hidden context from the evidence), action (perform the surgery), and
prediction (solve the mutilated model in the recovered context).

    ?- cf_counterfactual(SCM, [court-[0, 1]], [death-1],
                         [rifle_a-0], death, V).
    V = 1.

Yes - he still dies, because the evidence (death-1) tells us the court
ordered, so rifleman B still fires. But had the CAPTAIN stayed silent, the
prisoner would have lived (V = 0). And the pack is honest about
uncertainty: if the evidence is too thin to determine the answer,
cf_counterfactual fails rather than guesses, and cf_counterfactual_all
shows every value that remains possible.

Finally, cf_but_for tests actual causation the way a court of law does:
was X necessary for Y? The captain passes the but-for test of the death.
A single rifleman does not - his colleague would have fired anyway.
