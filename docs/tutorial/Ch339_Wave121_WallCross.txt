This chapter teaches a rule that reads a grid as a physical apparatus:
a stack of walls and gap rows, where orange triplets are cross-painted,
holes and near-border triplets spawn green markers, and some of those
markers reflect to the opposite border.

THE TASK

The grid is a stack of alternating rows. Even rows are horizontal walls
drawn mostly in color 8, with optional 0-holes punched through them.
Odd rows are gap rows drawn in 0, holding 7-7-7 orange triplets.

In the output every triplet is cross-painted, green marker cells appear
at certain positions, and the corner cells of the bottom wall are
determined by a separate rule.

THE RULE

Part A - Cross-paint and vertical markers. For each 7-7-7 triplet: set
the center cell to 6, the two flank cells to 8. Place a single 6 in the
wall row directly above the triplet center and a single 6 in the wall
row directly below it.

Part B - Wall-hole greening. For each interior 0-hole in a wall row:
paint the hole's interior-facing neighbor green (color 3), and paint the
nearer side border green. If the hole is within three columns of a side
wall, also paint the far border green.

Part C - Near-border triplets. A triplet whose left edge lies within
three columns of the left wall paints a two-cell green pair at columns 0
and 1 on the wall row just below it. A triplet near the right wall does
the same at the two rightmost columns.

Part D - Reflection. A green pair placed by Part C reflects to the
opposite border two wall rows above, but only if it is the sole green
pair on that wall row. Two near pairs on the same wall row cancel each
other - neither reflects. If the wall row sits in the lower half of the
grid, an additional reflection appears four rows below.

Part E - Bottom wall corners. When the bottom row is a wall row: green
the left corner if a near triplet sits on the gap row just above, or if
the left half of the bottom wall has no holes. Green the right corner
under the same symmetric condition.

THE DISCRIMINATING OBSERVATION

The reflection cancellation in Part D is the discriminating detail. On
the surface the two training pairs seem to differ only in the positions
of the triplets and holes, but the held-out test exercises a wall row
that receives green pairs from two near triplets simultaneously. When two
near pairs share a wall row, neither reflects. When exactly one near pair
occupies a wall row, it reflects. This one-vs-two count - detected by
grouping near pairs by wall row - produces the correct marker pattern on
the opposite border.
