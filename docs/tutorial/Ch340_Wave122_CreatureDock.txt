This chapter teaches a rule that reads a grid as a scene of living
pieces and their sockets: creatures that must be picked up, turned to
face the right way, and set down onto the socket their body was cut from.

THE TASK

The board is a colored frame of structural bands - whole rows or whole
columns painted a single color - wrapped around a plain background field.
Floating inside are creatures (touching clusters of cyan color-8 body
cells, a maroon color-9 core, and a few colored tips) and locks (isolated
maroon-only patches, each shaped like the core of one creature).

In the output every creature is lifted off the field and re-docked onto
the lock whose maroon shape matches its core, rotated and mirrored so its
colored tips best aim toward the frame.

THE RULE

Step 1 - Find the structural bands. Repeatedly mark any not-yet-marked
row whose remaining columns all carry a single color, and any
not-yet-marked column whose remaining rows all carry a single color,
until neither pass adds anything. This fixpoint peels the frame apart
even when no outer row or column is uniform on its own - a solid top band
can hide the uniformity of a side band until the band above is removed.

Step 2 - Identify field and frame. The background is the most common
non-structural color. Border cells are the structural cells whose color
differs from the background, each tagged with its position and color.
Foreground cells are the non-structural non-background cells.

Step 3 - Group the pieces. Split the foreground into 8-connected
components. A component with any cyan cell is a creature; an all-maroon
component is a lock.

Step 4 - Clear the field. Erase every creature and lock to the background.

Step 5 - Re-dock each creature. For each creature, try every unused lock
across all eight isometries (four rotations by two mirror states). When
the rotated maroon core matches the lock shape, rotate the whole creature
the same way, slide it so the core lands on the lock, and score the
orientation: each colored tip earns a point when it lies on the same side
of the lock's center as the border band that shares its color (columns
for a vertical band, rows for a horizontal band). Keep the highest score,
paint the placement, and mark the lock used.

THE DISCRIMINATING OBSERVATION

The tip-to-border scoring is the discriminating detail. A maroon core is
often symmetric enough to fit a lock in several rotations or mirror
states, so the core shape alone leaves the orientation ambiguous. The
colored tips break the tie: the correct docking aims each tip toward the
border band that shares its color, judged from the lock's center.
