Chapter 299: Wave 81 - wire_relay (WP-339, Layer 314)

wire_relay solves ARC-AGI-2 task a47bf94d. The task gives three
training pairs on 22x22 grids and one test case on a 26x26 grid. Each
grid holds a maze of wires drawn in color 8, crossing marks of color
5 where two wires pass through each other, and solid rectangular
blocks of color 9 that wires enter and leave. Every wire runs from a
tip pointing up or left to a tip pointing down or right, and a
colored figure - a solid three-by-three square, a four-cell diamond
outline, or a five-cell diagonal X - sits centered two cells beyond
one of the two tips. In the output every wire carries its figure
color to both ends: a diamond outline appears two cells beyond the
up-or-left tip and a diagonal X two cells beyond the down-or-right
tip, the input squares are erased, and the wire structure is
untouched.

Rule: find every wire tip, trace each wire through crossings and
blocks to its far tip, read the wire color from whichever end holds
a figure, and draw the diamond at the up-or-left end and the X at
the down-or-right end.

Algorithm:

A wire tip is any color-8 cell with exactly one orthogonal neighbor
among the structure colors 5, 8, and 9; each tip records its inward
direction. From each tip the tracer walks the wire one cell at a
time, always preferring the straight-ahead cell when it is
structural - this carries the walk through 8585 crossing marks
without switching wires - and otherwise turning into the unique
perpendicular structural neighbor, stopping when neither exists; a
fuel bound of twice the cell count guards against cyclic structures
on foreign grids. When the straight-ahead cell is a color-9 block,
the whole block region is flood filled and the walk jumps across it:
straight through to the wire cell directly beyond the region when
one exists, otherwise to the block's only other adjacent wire cell,
resuming in the outward direction of that exit. The two tips of each
wire then pair up: the tip whose outward direction is north or west
takes the four-cell diamond outline and the tip pointing south or
east takes the five-cell diagonal X, both centered two cells beyond
their tips; the wire color is read from whichever slot already holds
a figure, and figures present at both ends must agree. The output
keeps every structural cell, paints the diamond and X cells of every
wire in its relay color, and clears every other cell to background.

The lesson: hidden test grids exercise degrees of freedom the
training pairs leave dormant. The first prototype crossed blocks by
walking straight through their cells and passed all three training
pairs, but the test grid hides a corner turn inside a three-by-four
block - a wire enters heading west and leaves heading south, which
the straight-walk shortcut silently truncated. Modeling the block by
its function - a connector of its perimeter wires with a
straight-through preference and a unique-other-exit fallback - fixed
the test while leaving every training trace unchanged. A second
lesson: straight-preference tracing is the entire crossing logic; no
special case for the crossing marks is needed because the marks are
structural cells like any other.
