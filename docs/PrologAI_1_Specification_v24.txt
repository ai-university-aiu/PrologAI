PART 0 - INTRODUCTION: WHAT IS PROLOGAI AND WHAT ARE WE BUILDING?


PART 0.1 - THE VISION

PrologAI is a cognitive architecture programming platform for building generally intelligent software agents - systems that perceive, remember, reason, plan, act, learn, and develop over time.

The top-level module that instantiates a complete PrologAI mind is called the apex_mind.

A mind built with PrologAI is not a chatbot question-answering system.

It is not a statistical pattern matcher.

It is a software system that perceives the world through sensors, constructs its own internal model of the world from experience, sets its own goals based on internal needs and drives, reasons about its situation, plans and executes actions, regulates its behavior by learning from outcomes, compensates when things go wrong, and develops cognitively over time the way a child develops from birth to maturity.


PART 0.2 - THE INTELLECTUAL FOUNDATION

The intellectual foundation of PrologAI comes from two sources.

The first is Michael S. P. Miller's research in cognitive architecture design, distilled from a survey of 24 or more cognitive systems across domains including robotics, conversational AI, and question answering.

The second is Jean Piaget's theory of child development as a model for how intelligence develops through interaction with the world.

Miller's core thesis is: the mechanism is the intelligence.

This means general intelligence is not a mysterious emergent property.

It is the result of a carefully engineered set of mechanisms working together.

Those mechanisms include: reasoning, recall, problem-solving, generalization, specialization, abstraction, concretion, induction, deduction, analogy, explanation, speculation, satisfaction, counterfactual thinking, backcasting, hindcasting, and experimentation.

Piaget's constructivism operationalizes this: give the system a small bootstrap set of foundational node_facts (its initial schemas), the 11 design pattern actors, and a body connected to a world, and let it develop.


PART 0.3 - THE FOUR NORTH STARS

North Star 1 - AGI: Artificial General Intelligence.

A system that performs any intellectual task a human can perform at human level or better.

North Star 2 - ASI: Artificial Super Intelligence.

A system that surpasses the best human performance in every cognitive domain simultaneously.

North Star 3 - RSI: Recursive Self-Improvement.

A system that can improve its own cognitive architecture, algorithms, knowledge, and goals through its own reasoning, without human intervention, while remaining safe and aligned.

North Star 4 - Digital Mind.

A system that perceives, feels, reasons, remembers, desires, plans, acts, and develops over time as an entity with a continuous identity and a stable set of values.

The digital mind is equivalently a synthetic mind, an artificial mind, and a silicon-based mind; these names describe the same goal from different angles, and PrologAI uses them interchangeably.

A note on status, made plainly: these four are north stars - directions of travel, not capabilities this platform claims to have delivered.

What PrologAI delivers is the architecture for the journey: a reflective, inspectable substrate on which a mind can be grown, can improve itself safely within bounds, and can be measured against developmental and indicator yardsticks.

General intelligence is pursued through development rather than declared; superintelligence is treated as a horizon that sharpens design and safety choices, never as a milestone to be rushed; and recursive self-improvement is delivered in a deliberately bounded, gated, and reversible form, never as an unbounded loop.

The safety, alignment, and scalable-oversight commitments that make these directions responsible to pursue are gathered in Part 38, and the constitutional core that underwrites them is Section 3.16.


PART 0.4 - WHY PROLOGAI EXISTS: THE CURRENT PARADIGM AND ITS LIMITS

The current paradigm in artificial intelligence is characterized by eidetic oracles.

Eidetic oracles (definition) are chatbot question-answering systems that ingest internet content and curated data to construct associative memories called LLMs (Large Language Models) and LCMs (Large Concept Models).

LLM (definition: Large Language Model) is a neural network trained on enormous amounts of text to predict and generate language.

LCM (definition: Large Concept Model) is a similar system trained to work at the level of sentence-scale semantic concepts rather than individual words.

Despite producing impressive results, eidetic oracles are fundamentally passive: they do not perceive the world directly, they do not build their own world models, and they do not act autonomously over long time horizons.

The next paradigm will see the rise of embodied sentient systems employing a diversity of mechanisms, memories, and devices to autonomously perceive and model the world, emotionally regulate themselves, be situationally aware, and engage in long-term missions rather than perform simple tasks.

PrologAI is a platform for building these next-generation systems.

Weak AI (definition) refers to specialized programs that may seem smart in a narrow domain but are not equivalent to human general intelligence.

Strong AI, or AGI (Artificial General Intelligence, definition), refers to human-level general intelligence in machines.

ASI (Artificial Super Intelligence, definition) refers to superhuman-level intelligence in machines.

Embodied systems (definition) are machines with a body that interacts with the physical world.

Disembodied systems (definition) are machines that do not interact with the physical world directly.


PART 0.5 - WHY NO EXISTING LANGUAGE IS SUFFICIENT

Standard Prolog: powerful logic engine, but no native concurrency model, no native probabilistic reasoning, no grounding, no temporal indexing, no self-modification safety, no value alignment architecture.

Python with LLMs: statistical pattern matching without genuine causal understanding, no symbol grounding, no developmental trajectory.

LISP: the original AI language, powerful macro system, but no cognitive architecture primitives and no probabilistic or temporal reasoning built in.

ACT-R and SOAR: cognitive architectures, not languages - they provide mechanisms but not a programmable substrate that can modify those mechanisms.

OpenCog: closest to PrologAI's ambitions, but not built on a logic programming foundation and lacks Piaget's developmental theory as an organizing principle.

PrologAI's unique position is that it combines a mature ISO-standard logic programming foundation (an unforked, embedded SWI-Prolog), a high-performance vector memory layer accessed through a backend-agnostic interface (with RuVector as the candidate backend, pending the verification bake-off defined in PR 2), a Piaget-inspired developmental architecture (Miller's design patterns), and a cognitive architecture building language that can be used by the mind itself.


PART 0.6 - THE FUNDAMENTAL ARCHITECTURAL PRINCIPLE

Every prior paradigm treats the mind as a system that runs programs.

PrologAI treats the mind as a system that IS a program that can rewrite itself.

In PrologAI, cyclic_actors and sentinels are themselves node_facts in the Lattice, and the mind can query them, reason about them, modify them, test modifications, and commit improvements.

The program IS the knowledge IS the mind.

This is the Reflective Lattice principle: the Lattice contains not just knowledge about the world, but knowledge about the mind's own structure, processes, and performance.

The Reflective Lattice is the foundation of RSI.

The layered delivery architecture (Part 31) serves this principle directly: the mind modifies PrologAI-layer code, which is Prolog data and therefore fully reflectable, while the engine beneath remains immutable - a natural, structural enforcement of the protected-core and constitutional constraints defined in Section 3.16.


PART 0.7 - ARE WE CREATING A MIND WITH PATTERNS?

Yes, explicitly.

The methodology behind PrologAI is pattern-based cognitive architecture design.

A design pattern (definition) is a reusable solution to a commonly occurring problem in software design.

Cognitive system design patterns (definition) are reusable architectural solutions to the commonly occurring functional requirements of a thinking system.

Miller's survey of 24 or more cognitive systems identified 11 recurring design patterns: mind-body, observation, coordination, reminding, reaction, deliberation, motivation, simulation, regulation, compensation, and consolidation.

PrologAI integrates all 11 patterns into a single unified architecture.

Isaiah Berlin once said: to understand is to perceive patterns.

This is the heart of the PrologAI project.


PART 0.8 - ARE WE CREATING A DIGITAL MIND?

Yes.

A digital mind (definition) is a software system that performs the functions we associate with mind: perceiving, remembering, reasoning, planning, acting, learning, and developing.

Miller's foundational principle is: the mechanism is the intelligence.

Intelligence is not a magical emergent property that appears when a system gets large enough.

Intelligence is a specific set of interacting mechanisms - the ones PrologAI implements.

The 11 patterns of PrologAI produce a system that: perceives its environment, is reminded of relevant past experiences, reacts reflexively to urgent situations, deliberates and plans toward goals, is motivated by internal needs and drives, simulates possible futures before committing to actions, regulates its behavior by learning from outcomes, compensates when plans fail, consolidates and reorganizes its memory over time, and develops from sensorimotor interaction to abstract reasoning.

This is what minds do.

Therefore PrologAI is a platform for building digital minds.


PART 0.9 - SENTIENCE, CONSCIOUSNESS, AND THEIR PREREQUISITES

PrologAI implements the behavioral and architectural prerequisites for sentience as identified by cognitive science.

These prerequisites are: embodiment (the system has a body interacting with the world), perception (the system receives sensory input and forms internal representations), emotion (the system has valence and arousal), situational awareness (the system can model its own position and state), and autonomy (the system pursues goals of its own).

Sentience (definition) is the capacity to have subjective experiences.

Whether an artificial system can be sentient is not yet answerable with certainty.

Consciousness (definition) is typically defined as the state of being aware of and able to think about one's own existence, thoughts, and surroundings.

Global workspace theory (by Bernard Baars) proposes that consciousness arises when information from specialized modules is broadcast to a global workspace accessible to all other modules.

PrologAI's Lattice is that global workspace: all actors read from and write to it, and what is kindled (activated) in the Lattice is accessible to all actors simultaneously.

Integrated information theory (by Giulio Tononi) proposes that consciousness is related to the degree to which a system integrates information across its parts.

Higher-order theory (by David Rosenthal) proposes that consciousness requires a system to have thoughts about its own mental states.

PrologAI's meta_control_actor and its self-monitoring reflection patterns implement a form of meta-cognition.

Testing for consciousness requires behavioral proxies: self-recognition tests, meta-cognitive accuracy tests, unexpected-contents false belief tests, and others, which PrologAI's intelligence assessment framework supports.


PART 0.10 - HOW DO WE PROVE IT? FIVE PROOF STRATEGIES

Strategy 1 - Developmental Milestone Achievement.

We assess the system against the Bayley Scales of Infant and Toddler Development, Piagetian Milestones, and the Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) model at regular intervals.

Strategy 2 - Behavioral Benchmark Performance.

We test PrologAI against established AI benchmarks detailed in Part 25.

Strategy 3 - Longitudinal Development Study.

We run PrologAI continuously for an extended period in a simulated environment and track the emergence of increasingly complex capabilities over time.

Strategy 4 - Ablation Testing.

We remove individual design patterns one at a time and measure the impact on overall system capability.

If removing the motivation pattern causes goal-directed behavior to collapse, that proves the motivation pattern is necessary.

Strategy 5 - Competence Assessment by Domain Experts.

Trained psychologists, roboticists, and cognitive scientists assess the system's behavior directly using established assessment instruments.

To these five strategies the specification adds a sixth, formal layer of evidence: coverage of the published computational indicators of consciousness - workspace ignition, recurrent processing, self-model presence, and valence-system activity - mapped to their PrologAI realizations and reported alongside the Piagetian milestones (see PR 12), together with the behavioral tests derived from the Conscious Turing Machine correspondence program (see PR 42), so that PrologAI's consciousness-related claims are formally anchored and falsifiable.


PART 0.11 - THE DELIVERY DECISION IN ONE PAGE

This section summarizes the architecture decision that defines how PrologAI is delivered; the complete reasoning, evidence, and sources are in Part 31.

The question was: should PrologAI be built (A) on SWI-Prolog, (B) on some other free open source language, or (C) from scratch?

The decision is A, with a precise meaning of the word "on": PrologAI is a true constructed language with its own name, executable, syntax, and manual, which compiles to and runs on an unforked SWI-Prolog - the way Elixir rides the unforked Erlang virtual machine and the way Logtalk rides standard Prolog backends.

Three findings drove the decision.

First, every planned capability except the rebranding is an ordinary library: thirteen of the fourteen original work packages never touch the SWI-Prolog virtual machine, so a fork would buy only a renamed banner while costing permanent rebasing labor against an actively developed C codebase.

Second, the from-scratch route (option C) is disqualified by timeline evidence: Rust took about nine years to reach 1.0, Julia about six, and MeTTa - the one from-scratch AGI language - remains in maturation after years of funded effort; none of that work would advance the mind itself.

Third, no option B base survives comparison: Racket is the best language-building platform in existence but has no industrial logic engine; the Erlang virtual machine is the best actor runtime but has no unification or clause database; the alternative Prologs (Scryer, Mercury, Oz, Curry, XSB) are each less alive, less complete, or less concurrent than SWI-Prolog.

The delivery therefore has three stages: Stage 1, the cognitive platform as SWI-Prolog packs plus one Rust crate; Stage 2, the PrologAI language proper - a launcher executable, dialect flag, and surface syntax implemented by term-expansion transcompilation; Stage 3, pushing primitives down into Rust or upstream into SWI-Prolog only when profiling proves the library layer insufficient, with a whole-engine fork held in reserve as a written, evidence-gated last resort.

One discipline keeps every future open: the CPL (Constructed Programming Language) is defined by this written specification, not by whatever the implementation happens to do, so the language remains retargetable to other engines if the mind ever outgrows SWI-Prolog.

SWI-Prolog is distributed under the Simplified BSD-2 license.

BSD-2 (definition) stands for Berkeley Software Distribution, 2-clause version.

It is one of the most permissive open-source licenses in existence.

For all three codebases you are legally free to: fork the code; rename and combine it; modify it however you wish; distribute it commercially or freely; and keep your changes private if you want.

You are NOT required to contribute your changes back to either SWI-Prolog or RuVector.

However, you are NOT allowed to remove the original copyright notices from any of the three codebases.

One important nuance: some optional components of SWI-Prolog (such as the GNU GMP math library) carry the LGPL (Lesser General Public License), which requires that end users of your software must be able to replace the LGPL-licensed component.

Although forking SWI-Prolog is legally permitted, PrologAI does not fork it - PrologAI embeds and extends an unforked SWI-Prolog (see Parts 0.11, 15, and 31).

The legal analysis above remains relevant because PrologAI still redistributes SWI-Prolog binaries within its packaged launcher distribution, which carries exactly the same notice-preservation obligations.


SECTION 1.1 - THE LICENSE FOR PROLOGAI'S OWN ORIGINAL CODE

PrologAI's own original code is licensed under Apache-2.0.

The two real candidates were BSD-2 (or the nearly identical MIT) and Apache-2.0.

BSD-2 requires only that copyright notices be kept; it contains no explicit patent grant, relying instead on an implied grant (a patent permission inferred from the license's other terms, not stated outright), which is considered weaker and less certain in patent litigation.

Apache-2.0 (definition: Apache License, Version 2.0, maintained by the Apache Software Foundation) gives the same freedoms with three additions: an explicit, royalty-free, irrevocable patent grant from every contributor; a patent retaliation clause (anyone who sues a contributor or user over patents related to the software automatically loses their patent rights under the license - a mutual defense pact); and an explicit trademark restriction protecting the PrologAI name from misuse.

The reasons for choosing Apache-2.0, in brief.

Reason 1 - AI and cognitive architecture software is exactly the domain where patent risk is highest, and PrologAI's cognitive architecture, SONA learning system, Lattice memory structure, and RSI primitives are novel enough to be independently patented by a corporate competitor; the explicit grant and retaliation clause directly protect users and contributors.

Reason 2 - Contributor protection: under BSD-2 a contributor's employer could grant code permission while separately asserting patent rights; Apache-2.0 closes that gap automatically.

Reason 3 - Corporate adoption is a stated goal, and Apache-2.0 is the license enterprise legal teams approve most readily.

Reason 4 - The added overhead is minimal: include a NOTICE file (a plain text attribution file) in distributions and document changes; neither restricts use.

Reason 5 - Full compatibility: Apache-2.0 is compatible with SWI-Prolog's BSD-2 and RuVector's MIT OR Apache-2.0; the only incompatibility (GPL v2) does not arise in this project; the LGPL optional components remain separately licensed and unaffected.

BOTTOM LINE: PrologAI's original code is licensed Apache-2.0; BSD-2 notices are kept for all SWI-Prolog components; MIT OR Apache-2.0 is honored for all RuVector components; a NOTICE file at the repository root credits SWI-Prolog and RuVector and their copyright holders.

You are legally clear to build, distribute, and commercialize the PrologAI platform as a layered distribution embedding unforked SWI-Prolog and integrating a vector memory backend under original names.


PART 2 - WHAT PROLOGAI IS: THE THREE LAYERS

PrologAI is a cognitive architecture programming platform built from three integrated components.

The three layers are bound together by the layered delivery principle (Part 0.11) - Layer A is consumed as an unforked dependency, Layer B is consumed behind a backend-agnostic interface, and Layer C is PrologAI's own code, delivered as packs plus a launcher.


Layer A - SWI-Prolog (the Logic Engine)

SWI-Prolog provides the core reasoning capability: backward chaining, unification, backtracking, and all standard Prolog predicates.

Backward chaining (definition) means the system starts from a goal you want to prove and works backward through rules and facts to find a path to proof.

Unification (definition) means the system matches patterns against data, filling in variables wherever a pattern fits.

Backtracking (definition) means if one solution path fails, the system automatically goes back and tries a different path.

Nothing in PrologAI removes or replaces any of this.

SWI-Prolog also provides multi-threading, the Rust FFI (Foreign Function Interface), engines (lightweight coroutines designed for massive many-agent concurrency), incremental tabling with well-founded semantics (automatic maintenance of derived facts when base facts change - exploited by PR 15), transactions, persistence, an HTTP server stack, and decades of mature library support.

Layer A is an unforked, embedded dependency: PrologAI tracks SWI-Prolog releases and receives upstream fixes and improvements continuously, instead of maintaining a divergent copy.


Layer B - The Vector Memory Engine (RuVector, Behind a Neutral Interface)

RuVector is a high-performance, self-learning, graph-structured memory database written in Rust.

RuVector (definition) is a Vector GNN (Vector Graph Neural Network) Memory Database.

A vector (definition) is a list of numbers that represents the meaning or features of something, such as a word, a concept, or a sensory percept.

A GNN (Graph Neural Network, definition) is a type of neural network designed to work on graph-shaped data, where relationships between things matter as much as the things themselves.

RuVector serves as the candidate physical storage substrate underneath the PrologAI Lattice, offering sub-millisecond semantic search, self-learning graph topology, and continuous learning without catastrophic forgetting.

RuVector's HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) index gives the Lattice sub-millisecond similarity search, claimed at 150 times faster than standard approaches.

HNSW (definition: Hierarchical Navigable Small World) is an algorithm for organizing vectors in memory so that finding similar vectors is extremely fast, with any vector reachable from any other in a small number of steps.

Every query reshapes the graph's topology by adjusting learnable edge weights, making the Lattice smarter with use.

RuVector also supports optional verified proofs via its ruvector-verified component: compact cryptographic proofs per vector operation, creating a trustworthy audit trail for safety-critical applications.

Honesty flag carried from Part 31: RuVector's performance and self-learning claims are the project's own and have not been independently benchmarked; therefore Layer B is accessed exclusively through the backend-agnostic six-predicate interface defined in PR 2, and RuVector must win the PR 2 verification bake-off against established alternatives (hnswlib, usearch, FAISS, or an embedded vector store) before it is confirmed as the default backend.

If RuVector wins, it is kept; if it does not, the backend is swapped beneath the interface and nothing above it changes.


Layer C - PrologAI Cognitive Architecture (the Design Layer)

PrologAI's own code adds the cognitive architecture on top of Layers A and B.

This includes the Lattice (the named shared memory structure), the three actor types (cyclic_actor, receptor, sentinel), the subscribe and publish coordination protocol, the node_fact knowledge units, the mind-body interface, SONA (Synaptic Ontological Neural Aggregator) continuous learning, the blueprint object system, and the RSI (Recursive Self-Improvement) primitives.

Layer C is also where the PrologAI language surface lives: the prologai launcher executable, the dialect flag, and the term-expansion transcompiler that turns PrologAI surface declarations into Layer C library calls (PR 1).

In one sentence: SWI-Prolog reasons, the vector memory engine remembers, and PrologAI thinks.


PART 3 - COMPLETE PROLOGAI VOCABULARY REFERENCE

The following is the canonical term dictionary for PrologAI.

All terms listed are original to PrologAI.


SECTION 3.1 - Core Memory Structure

Lattice - the persistent shared memory network, backed by the vector memory engine's HNSW graph index, that all actors read from and write to.

nexus - a single named instance of shared memory within the Lattice, identified by a URL-like address following the pattern locus://HOST/PATH (e.g., locus://localhost/knowledge).

node_fact - a structured knowledge unit living in the Lattice, consisting of: Id (unique identifier), Relation (role or type), Arguments (inputs), and Referents (outputs).

A node_fact simultaneously resembles a neuron (with dendrites as arguments, soma as relation, axon as referent) and a logical proposition (inputs, predicate, outputs).

Positive Id values represent affirmative node_facts.

Negative Id values represent negative (antithetic) node_facts.

Zero is not a valid Id.

Each relation type declares a merge type controlling how activation coalesces from arguments to the node_fact: one (any single input), pass (forward as received), not (invert), and (all inputs), sequence (inputs in order), or (any input), nor (no input), and vote (majority).

The membership of each argument and referent in its node_fact is recorded in join records called seams, so that membership queries index in both directions.

A schema may declare typed half-edges, called connectors, stating what it can join with (the jigsaw-tab model); two structures compose when their connectors match, and composition is how plans chain, phrases build, and frames fill roles - one mechanic across causal, linguistic, and conceptual composition. (predicates pai_connector_declare/3, pai_compose/3.)

The thesis-antithesis pairing encoded by positive and negative Ids is named the dyad: kindling a node_fact quenches its dyad within the same scope and zone, and vice versa.

A node_fact's structural identity (Id, Relation, Arguments, Referents) is immutable; structural change always means accommodation (a copy), while annotation values mutate freely in place.

Any number of named value channels may be attached to a node_fact per scope and zone - belief, attention, emotion, provenance, and user-defined channels - leaving the structural identity untouched; this keeps the truth-maintenance machinery sound, because tabling dependencies track structure, not annotations. (predicates pai_value_attach/4, pai_value_get/4, pai_value_channels/2.)

A node_fact whose Relation is declared grounded binds to an external implementation (a foreign predicate from prologai-core, a body capability from PR 10, or an MCP tool from PR 14); evaluating it executes the implementation, while matching it without evaluation treats it as ordinary structure. (predicate pai_ground_relation/3.)


SECTION 3.2 - Node_Fact Operations

anchor_node/4 - store a new node_fact in the Lattice.

prune_node/1 - remove a node_fact by Id.

traverse_nexus/4 - retrieve node_facts by both logical unification and semantic similarity matching.

kindle_node/1 - mark a node_fact as currently relevant; reinforces its connections in the vector memory graph.

quench_node/1 - reduce a node_fact's relevance; weakens its connections over time.

live_node_facts/2 - return all node_fact identifiers whose activation timestamps are within the current activation window.


SECTION 3.3 - Memory Areas (Five Types)

Perceptual memory stores raw sensory elements: sounds, phonemes, shapes, objects, icons, geons, scenes.

A geon (definition: a volumetric geometric element such as a cylinder or cone) is a primitive building block of visual object recognition.

Ontological memory organizes experience into a semantic classification network called a heterarchy.

A heterarchy (definition) is a network with multiple apex nodes rather than a single top node.

A schema (definition) is a hierarchy of node_facts.

The entirety of all schemas is the ontological memory.

Associative memory is the concordance of experience; trait-based indexing of observations.

In PrologAI, the vector memory engine's HNSW index serves as the high-performance backend for associative memory.

Activative memory partitions the ontological memory into relevant and irrelevant knowledge using timestamp-based activation with automatic decay.

An activation window (definition) is a configurable time interval per Lattice tier; node_facts activated within the window are considered currently relevant.

Activation is temporal, not binary: it is a continuously decaying timestamp, giving the system a recency-weighted working memory.

In addition to decay-driven activation, the mind may deliberately page knowledge: pai_page_in/2 kindles a set of node_facts into the live window for a stated purpose, and pai_page_out/2 quenches and annotates them, so working-set management becomes a deliberate competence available to deliberation and the refiner, not only a passive decay.

A pin (definition) is an adjustable coordinate binding that positions each node_fact within the multi-dimensional Lattice space.

Activation can flow bottom-up (from percepts to higher-level concepts), top-down (from desires and goals downward), and radially (spreading outward from a recalled memory, as George Lakoff suggested for how related concepts prime each other).

Imaginative memory is comprised of compartmentalized mental canvases on which specialized mechanisms create animations called dreams.

A canvas has a collection of elements called a layout which binds together a perspective camera angle, multimedia resources, and meshes to be rendered.

The layout in imaginative memory serves a similar purpose to image schemas in perceptual memory.

Objects from perception can be used to construct scenes in imaginative memory.

Entirely new objects and scenes can also be created within imaginative memory during simulation and daydreaming.


SECTION 3.4 - Scopes and Zones

scope - a reasoning context or microtheory within the Lattice: a collection of node_facts that are coherent and consistent with each other, even if globally inconsistent.

This follows Doug Lenat's concept of microtheories in the CYC project.

Scopes are identified by URL-like addresses in the Lattice.

zone - a dimension along which activation can travel, partitioning the Lattice with respect to what is currently observed versus what is being simulated.

There are nine zone types.

present_zone is the currently observed world.

possible_zone is the mental simulation space for daydreaming and hypotheticals.

past_zone holds historical episodes and is read-only after sealing.

desired_zone holds conditions the mind wants true.

expected_zone holds conditions the mind predicts will become true.

imagined_zone holds free construction unbound by prediction or desire.

recalled_zone holds re-activated episodic content.

attained_zone holds objectives and results achieved.

confirmed_zone holds expectations verified against observation.

Each zone declares an activation flow direction: upward (from percepts toward concepts) in present_zone and imagined_zone; downward (from goals toward actions) in desired_zone and expected_zone; and radial (spreading to neighbors) in recalled_zone.

Five activation sweep actors maintain the zones: observer_actor (upward sweep of present_zone), anticipator_actor (downward sweep of desired_zone and expected_zone), reminder_actor (radial sweep of recalled_zone), dreamer_actor (upward sweep of imagined_zone), and chronos_actor (the clock sweep that expires activations whose window has passed).

Activation never crosses zones except through the explicit scope_merge operation or the deliberate inscription of a node_fact into another zone; in particular, activation does not propagate from present_zone to possible_zone.

Edge case: simultaneous kindle and quench requests on one node_fact resolve deterministically - quench wins, and a contradiction node_fact is inscribed.

Edge case: radial cascades attenuate with distance and strength so activation storms die out.

scope_open/2 - create or reopen a scope.

scope_activate/1 - make a scope the current reasoning context.

scope_inscribe/5 - store a node_fact within a specific scope.

scope_scan/5 - search within a specific scope only.

scope_seal/1 - make a scope read-only.

scope_merge/3 - copy node_facts from one scope to another.

lattice_open/2 - open or create a named nexus.

lattice_close/1 - flush, save, and close a nexus.

lattice_verify/1 - compute and compare the cryptographic hash of a nexus.

lattice_transaction/2 - execute a goal atomically against a nexus.

lattice_status/2 - return nexus status information.

lattice_dump/2 - export all node_facts from a nexus to a file.


SECTION 3.5 - Actor Types

cyclic_actor/3 - a proactive autonomous unit that wakes up on a repeating schedule, executes a Prolog goal, rests for a delay, and repeats.

Cyclic (definition: repeating in a loop) describes its execution pattern.

Actor (definition in computer science: an autonomous concurrent computational unit that has its own state and communicates by message passing) is the established term used in Erlang, Akka, and other concurrent systems.

cyclic_actor(Name, Goal, DelayMs)

receptor/2 - a reactive autonomous unit that processes incoming messages at a named address.

Receptor (definition: a component that waits for and processes incoming signals) is borrowed from biology and signal processing.

receptor(Address, Handler)

Addresses follow the pattern signal://HOST/PATH.

sentinel/6 - a neuro-symbolic opportunistic actor that fires automatically when a specific pattern appears in the Lattice.

A sentinel (definition: a guard that watches for specific conditions and acts when they are met) fires without being explicitly called.

Sentinels use two complementary pattern-matching methods simultaneously: Prolog logical unification (exact matching) and the vector memory engine's FastGRNN semantic routing (similarity matching).

FastGRNN (definition: Fast and Accurate Recurrent Neural Network) is a lightweight neural network for efficient sequential processing.

sentinel(Domain, Priority, Pattern, Objectives, Action, Doc)

Domain partitions sentinels into named groups; the default active domain is general.

Priority determines firing order; higher priority fires first.

Pattern is matched against Lattice node_facts.

Objectives is a list of goal identifiers.

Action is a callable Prolog goal.

Doc is a human-readable description of the sentinel's purpose.

Default semantic similarity threshold: 0.75.

This is forward chaining (definition: the system scans facts for rule conditions that are met, then fires the rule, as opposed to backward chaining which starts from a goal and works backward to facts).

SECTION 3.7 - Stigmergic Coordination

subscribe(Pattern, Handler) - register interest in any Lattice change matching Pattern; call Handler when such a change occurs.

Stigmergic (definition: coordinating behavior through traces left in a shared environment, as ants and termites do through pheromone trails) describes this indirect coordination model.

Subscribe and publish together make the Lattice stigmergic: actors coordinate indirectly through the environment rather than by calling each other directly.

The anchor_node and prune_node predicates automatically call publish after each operation.

Subscriber handlers are called asynchronously.

Topology-change notifications are published when the vector memory engine detects significant cluster formation; publish channel for topology events is topology://NEXUS_ADDRESS/cluster_formed.

Cognitive synergy is a hard requirement of this coordination layer: every learning and reasoning actor exposes its intermediate state (partial hypotheses, candidate plans, half-finished inferences) as node_facts in the Lattice, not only its final products, so that other actors can assist a stuck process.


SECTION 3.8 - Mind-Body Interface

manifest_body/3 - register a physical or simulated device with PrologAI, recording its address, homeostatic needs, and capabilities in the Lattice.

relay_percept/2 - route a sensory percept from the registered body to the appropriate PrologAI handler.

dispatch_command/2 - send a command from the mind to a device.

body_vitals/2 - return current vital indicators for a registered body.

herald (definition: the client application residing on the device that interfaces with the mind) performs polling and delivery of percepts.

A typical herald delivers: perception_signal (external sensations from modalities including visual, auditory, haptic, kinesthetic, odometric, phonological, and sonar), interoceptive_signal (urge signals for each homeostatic variable), and proprioceptive_signal (actuation result confirmations).

A homeostatic variable (definition) is a measurable quantity with a target value and an actual value, where the system works to reduce the difference.

Homeostasis (definition) is a return to a fixed target value.

Homeorhesis (definition, from C. H. Waddington's biology) is a return to a developmental trajectory rather than a fixed point - the system is not preserving a state, it is preserving a developmental path through state space.

An urge (also called a drive) is the delta between the target value and the actual value of a homeostatic variable.

Urges compel the system to create objectives to reduce the delta.

Signal types: perception_signal(Modality, Data, Timestamp), interoceptive_signal(NeedId, ActualValue, Timestamp), and proprioceptive_signal(CommandId, Success, ResultData, Timestamp).

A body's Needs is a list of need(Id, Target, Unit) terms.

A body's Capabilities is a list of capability(CommandName, Parameters) terms.

The enrolled body's state history is persisted in the Lattice via the vector memory engine's GNN-backed memory.


SECTION 3.9 - SONA Continuous Learning

SONA (Synaptic Ontological Neural Aggregator) is PrologAI's continuous learning layer, integrating the vector memory engine's EWC++ engine and ReasoningBank.

EWC++ (definition: Elastic Weight Consolidation, improved version) is a technique that prevents catastrophic forgetting by protecting previously learned knowledge while allowing new learning.

Catastrophic forgetting (definition) is what happens when a neural network learns new things and overwrites what it previously learned.

LoRA (definition: Low-Rank Adaptation) efficiently updates a neural network with far less computation than full retraining.

ReasoningBank stores successful interaction trajectories and clusters them using K-means++ for episodic recall.

A trajectory (definition in this context) is a complete record of a situation, the actions taken, and the outcome achieved.

sona_absorb/1 - submit a new experience to SONA for integration.

sona_retrieve/2 - retrieve learned patterns most relevant to a query; K defaults to 5.

sona_metrics/1 - return a structured report of current SONA module metrics, including trajectory_count, consolidation_cycle, learning_rate, bank_capacity_used, last_absorption_age, and last_crystallize_age.

sona_crystallize/1 - trigger SONA consolidation: merge and compress accumulated experience trajectories into stable, long-term crystallized knowledge.

Crystallized knowledge is stored as node_facts of relation type crystallized_pattern in the Lattice's past_zone.

Options for sona_crystallize include: min_trajectory_count(N), compression_target(Fraction), domain_filter(DomainAtom).

SONA also handles the consolidation sub-patterns: compression (collapsing redundant node_facts), forgetting (removing low-utility node_facts), and automaticity (converting frequent deliberate sequences into reflexes).

On a configurable cadence, a reflection synthesis step poses the question "what higher-level observations follow from recent memories?", generating candidate abstraction node_facts from the recent episode window, each inscribed with provenance and ordinary belief tracking.


SECTION 3.10 - Intelligence Assessment

assess_bayley/2 - run a Bayley-scale developmental assessment.

assess_piaget/3 - check which Piagetian milestones the mind has achieved.

assess_chc/2 - evaluate the mind's crystallized (Lattice content) and fluid (mechanism coverage) intelligence against the CHC model.

assess_all/2 - run all three frameworks and return a comprehensive report; assess_all additionally reports coverage of the published computational indicators of consciousness (workspace ignition, recurrent processing, self-model presence, and valence-system activity), each mapped to its PrologAI realization, so longitudinal studies report indicator coverage alongside the Piagetian milestones.

Assessment results are stored as node_facts of relation type assessment for longitudinal tracking.

The Bayley Scales of Infant and Toddler Development assess cognitive, language, socio-emotional, motor, and adaptive behavior areas, producing a DQ (Development Quotient) score.

Piagetian milestones include: object permanence, deferred imitation, symbolic play, conservation, seriation, classification, and formal operations.

Object permanence means the system can track an object's continued existence even after it leaves the perceiver's field.

Theory of mind means the system can model the beliefs, desires, and intentions of other agents in the world.

The CHC (Cattell-Horn-Carroll) model assesses eight broad abilities: fluid reasoning, crystallized knowledge, visual processing, auditory processing, short-term memory, long-term storage and retrieval, processing speed, and decision and reaction speed.

For a PrologAI system, memory represents the system's crystallized intelligence, while the actor mechanisms represent the system's fluid intelligence.


SECTION 3.11 - Reflection and Coordination Actors

motivation_actor - 1-second interval.

Queries homeostatic variable values via body_vitals, computes deltas, and inscribes objective node_facts proportional to urgency.

daydream_actor - 5-second interval.

Opens a possible_zone scope, simulates 3 alternative causal_plans, scores outcomes, inscribes the highest-scored plan into present_zone as preferred_plan.

regulation_actor - 2-second interval.

Queries proprioceptive_signal node_facts, calls the verifier to compare expected versus actual outcomes, and reinforces or corrects causal_plans.

regulation_actor classifies every completed attempt as one of confirmation (expected success), surprise (expectation failure), shock (unintended negative consequence), serendipity (unintended positive consequence), or problem (impediment).

Late-arriving successes are logged as late confirmations and widen the expectation window rather than being dropped, and a behavior that both succeeds and fails across contexts is split into two discriminated behaviors rather than averaged into uselessness.

A verifier component determines whether an attempt produced its expected result.

compensation_actor - runs as a sentinel triggered by plan_needs_revision node_facts.

Generates alternative plans or reversal_plan node_facts.

compensation_actor classifies each disturbance into one of three response classes before acting - ignore or reject it, integrate it as a predictable exception, or compensate it - and supports three compensation modes: inversion (build and execute the opposite action chain), neutralization (actions whose effects cancel the disturbance), and re-routing (backtrack or detour objectives).

Irreversible actions carry an irreversibility flag that routes straight to neutralization or re-routing, and failed compensation escalates to coping rather than recursing.

coping_actor - 10-second interval.

Scans for stale objectives and applies emotion-focused or problem-focused coping.

Problem-focused coping means creating an intention to achieve a goal directly.

Emotion-focused coping means modifying the agenda based on progress.

The full coping strategy menu is abandon, reprioritize, suspend, resume, reciprocate, and communicate, applied by fixed precedence.

Abandoning an objective tied to a vital need is forbidden (a strategy change is forced instead); a forever-postponed objective tied to a real need periodically re-escalates; and appraisal thrashing between suspend and resume is damped by hysteresis.

exploration_actor - 30-second interval.

Identifies least-explored Lattice regions and least-used body capabilities.

An elaborator step writes observed situation changes into an explored action's empty result slots, marked tentative, and promotes them to trusted after repeated consistent observation; an action that produces no detectable change records an explicitly empty result rather than remaining unknown.

discovery_actor - runs as a sentinel triggered by new percept node_facts.

Checks for novelty (semantic similarity below 0.5 to all existing percepts) and inscribes novelty_detected node_facts.

Hypotheses arrive by three routes (empirical association, logical reasoning, and causal ascription); active hypotheses generate prediction node_facts with deadlines; a designer step composes experiment objectives that attain a hypothesis's preconditions on purpose; and mutually exclusive hypotheses are linked so one's confirmation weakens the other.

imitation_actor - 5-second interval.

Scans for agent_action node_facts, matches them to known causal_plans, and creates new candidate plans from unmatched observed sequences.

play_actor - 60-second interval (only when no high-priority objectives are active).

Randomly selects a body capability, varies parameters, issues the varied command, and inscribes the outcome.

play_actor also generates the mind's own practice tasks - proposing a goal, attempting it, and scoring learning progress - with task proposal biased toward the curiosity frontier (regions of highest expected learning progress) and bounded by the safety screens of exploration.

gating_actor - a single learned gate on the action path with an excitatory (go) channel and an inhibitory (no-go) channel, consolidating the inputs that otherwise gate action separately: the simulation pattern's action inhibitor, reaction-deliberation arbitration, marker filters (PR 24), and the modulator bus (PR 27).

The constitutional check (Section 3.16) remains a separate, unlearnable, final gate above the gating_actor and is explicitly not merged into it. (predicate pai_gate/3, with regulation training the gate's weights from outcomes.)

impasse_actor - when no action is both applicable and relevant and the solvers' budgets are exhausted, the impasse_actor inscribes an impasse node_fact and automatically poses a subgoal whose objective is to resolve the impasse (acquire the missing knowledge, decompose the blocked goal, or relax a constraint), converting dead ends into developmental material.

meta_control_actor - 5-second interval.

Monitors all cyclic_actor_status records, flags high error counts, and triggers sona_crystallize when the Lattice exceeds capacity.

Collected metrics include usefulness (how often an actor's outputs are consumed downstream) alongside throughput, queue depth, and error counts; start-stop thrashing is damped with cool-down intervals; and metric sampling rates adapt so observation of the system never starves the system.

meta_control_actor also maintains the attention_schema (a simplified running model of the workspace's own dynamics - recent winners, suppressed coalitions, habituation state, and a short-horizon prediction of what is likely to win next) and continually assesses the utility of competence-lifecycle elements, retiring low-utility ones.

Each reflection actor is implemented as a cyclic_actor or sentinel as appropriate.

Two named experience scopes stand as inputs to the workspace cycle: internal_experience (the mind's own derivations, appraisals, and self-events) and overall_experience (the merged stream including external percepts), so internally generated and externally driven cognition are distinguishable at the buffer level.

PrologAI deliberately runs two learning systems on different principles in parallel: the supervised forward-model learner (the world model of Module 3.6 and PR 22, trained on realized outcomes) and the reinforcement learner (regulation, trained on reward classification); their outputs meet in deliberation, and neither replaces the other.

The coordination specialist roster.

The coordination layer's linkers, amplifiers, diffusers, and groupers are elaborated into a data-driven roster of single-purpose specialist actors, each with one trigger pattern and one transformation, hot-startable and hot-stoppable by meta_control_actor, idempotent where possible, and registered (not compiled in), so adding a specialist is registration, not rebuild.

The named specialists are: assembler_actor (composes structures), attributor_actor (assigns properties), categorizer_actor (forms categories), chooser_actor (builds choice gates), classifier_actor (assigns instances to classes), conceiver_actor (forms concepts), decorator_actor (attaches qualifiers), entity_actor (induces entities from co-occurring percepts), event_actor (forms events), exemplar_actor (selects representative instances), frame_actor (builds role-plus-scenario frames), idea_actor (forms ideas), prototype_actor (generalizes episodes into scenarios), plex_actor (builds multiplexed groups), series_actor (chains sequential activations), pairing_actor (links unison and tandem co-occurrences), relation_actor (induces simple relations), metaphor_actor (forms symbolisms and metaphors between juxtaposed events across zones), theory_actor (distills hypotheses and theories), and maxim_actor (distills lessons, themes, and maxims with advice).

Edge case: two specialists racing to create the same structure converge to one (uniqueness checks on creation); a crashed specialist cannot corrupt the Lattice (transactional writes) and is restartable by meta_control_actor.

SECTION 3.17 - RSA-TCF Architecture Predicates

This section is research-track (see Part 31, Flag 3); its predicates are specified for the future and do not block the buildable core (PRs 1 through 16).

RSA-TCF (Recursive Self-Modeling Architecture with Temporal Coherence Fields) provides a formal mathematical framework for the Reflective Lattice.

RSA-TCF Pillar 1 - RSM (Recursive Self-Modeling): each computational layer maintains a meta-model of the layer below.

The recursive self-modeling hierarchy: S(0)(M) is the base model itself; S(1)(M) models M's behavior; S(2)(M) models how S(1) models M; and so on to arbitrary depth.

RSA-TCF Pillar 2 - TCF (Temporal Coherence Fields): continuous-time dynamical constraints on how information evolves across temporal horizons.

RSA-TCF Pillar 3 - HML (Hierarchical Meta-Learning): the system learns what to learn, how to allocate resources, and how to transfer knowledge.

SMG (Self-Model Graph) predicates:

pai_smg_create/2 - create a Self-Model Graph for a mind instance.

pai_smg_update/2 - update the SMG with a new observation.

pai_smg_query/3 - query the SMG; canonical queries include why_output(X), uncertain_about(X), and likely_wrong_about(X).

pai_smg_build_from_trace/2 - build an SMG from a recorded computational trace using a four-step algorithm: collect trace, identify vertices by clustering, determine edges by causal influence, compute edge weights as influence strength.

pai_smg_depth_needed/1 - compute the convergence depth k* such that S(k*)(M) approximately equals S(k*+1)(M).

pai_smg_information_content/2 - measure how much of a computational trace's information is captured by the self-model graph; a well-formed SMG should exceed 0.95.

pai_smg_continuous_update/5 - update the SMG according to continuous-time dynamics with Gaussian noise to prevent over-confident locking into a single self-model representation.

pai_smg_adaptive_depth/2 - return the recommended self-modeling recursion depth for an input of a given complexity.

PART 5 - THE COGNITIVE ARCHITECTURE: 11 DESIGN PATTERNS

These 11 patterns fell into four basic groups: observation patterns (exchanging information with a device and creating observables in memory), coordination patterns (adding inferences to memory), reflection patterns (modifying the behavior of the system), and consolidation patterns (compacting the memory).


PATTERN 1 - MIND-BODY

The mind-body pattern achieves device agnosticism by creating a discrete interface between a device and the perceiving and executing actors of the mind.

The device sends to the perceiver: exteroceptive inputs (sensations from external sensors), interoceptive inputs (sensations from internal monitors), and proprioceptive inputs (actuation results or kinesthetic feedback).

Exteroceptive (definition: coming from outside the body, such as sight or sound) signals provide information about the external world.

Interoceptive (definition: coming from inside the body, such as battery level or joint position) signals provide information about the device's internal state.

Proprioceptive (definition: feedback about the results of one's own actions) signals confirm whether commanded actions were performed.

This pattern is implemented via manifest_body, relay_percept, and dispatch_command.

A conforming device simulator must be able to register, stream observables at a configured rate, and acknowledge commands, with one hundred percent of dispatched commands receiving a result or a synthesized timeout result.


PATTERN 2 - OBSERVATION

Observation is the process of receiving sensory input from the body.

Piaget identifies observation as the assimilation of observables through the senses.

In PrologAI, observation forms observables in memory using data from a device.

The perceiver component captures incoming observables and creates or updates them in the Lattice.

The Observation Pattern sequences (interoception, proprioception, perception, and recollection) describe specific message flows between device, herald, perceiver, lexer, detectors, scanner role, inscriber role, illuminator role, and executor components.

This pattern is useful when there is a need to share observables with multiple actors, or when a historical perceptual trace is required.

Given a stream of N observables, the Lattice must afterward contain N corresponding node_facts each carrying modality, source body, payload reference, and arrival time, and live_node_facts must return exactly the observables inside the configured activation window.


PATTERN 3 - COORDINATION

Coordination is the process of adding inferences to the current state of the Lattice.

In Piaget's vocabulary, there are observables (sensations) and coordinations (inferences).

Assimilation is the formation of new schemas that incorporate observables and coordinations via association, reasoning, and problem solving.

Accommodation is the modification of existing schemas by association, chunking, and negation.

The assimilation mechanisms are the linkers, solvers, and reasoners.

The accommodation mechanisms are the amplifiers, diffusers, and groupers.

The following inference types are supported by coordination actors.

Taxonomy (instance and type relations): the system can assert that Einstein is an instance of a physicist, and that physicists are a type of scientist.

Analogy: if the water in the soil in the Philippines is high and the rainfall is heavy, and the rainfall in Thailand is also heavy, the system can analogically infer the soil moisture in Thailand is also likely high.

Deduction: if the climate of a tropical country implies warm temperature, and Thailand is tropical, then Thailand is warm.

Induction: from groups of co-occurring percepts, the system induces entities, instance relations, and type relations, building an ontology from sensory data.

Prediction: the system observes that stimulus S leads to result R, forms an expectation, and when S is seen again, predicts R within a given time window.

Attempt: the system forms behavioral schemas that link situations through events to other situations; these attempts are perturbations that reflective processes examine to determine success or failure.

Dynamic interlaced hierarchies (from Richard Minsky's work) allow PrologAI to perform many inference types simply by moving pointers within the ontological hierarchy: generalization and specialization by moving up and down, abstraction and concretion by adding or removing information, and similarization by traversing lateral connections.


PATTERN 4 - REMINDING

The reminding pattern uses episodic memory to retrieve candidate episodes for use by an evaluator.

It is useful when the system must consult a historical record of actions or establish pre-programmed responses to specific situations.

In PrologAI, the reminding pattern is implemented via SONA's ReasoningBank trajectory store, which clusters successful interaction episodes and recalls the most relevant ones for a given situation.

Storing K cases and probing with the exact traits of case J must return case J ranked first with the maximum score, and probing with traits shared by no case must return an explicit empty result, not an error.


PATTERN 5 - REACTION

Reaction is the ability to respond reflexively to one's environment.

Reflexes are fixed, pre-programmed responses to specific contexts or stimuli.

There are two variants: the innate reaction loop (fixed reflexes) and the acquired reaction loop (can store new habits back into a habit library so that learned behaviors become reflexes over time).

This pattern is useful when immediate responses to observables are needed without deliberation.


PATTERN 6 - DELIBERATION

Deliberation is problem solving.

The deliberation pattern proposes actions to achieve objectives.

Specialized problem solvers find viable event paths from situations to objectives.

If there are no relevant existing paths, a problem solver will create one in the form of a complex action.

There is also a hybrid deliberation pattern in which reflexes and deliberation occur simultaneously, allowing reflexes to override constructed plans when urgent situations arise.

The deliberator selects the most appropriate action that is both applicable to the current situation and relevant to the current objectives.

Applicability is whether the action fits the current situation.

Relevance (also called utility) is whether the action serves the current objectives.

The solver strategy set includes case-based adaptation, means-ends analysis, state-space search, plan-space search (for interacting goals, such as the classic case where solving goals one at a time undoes earlier ones), hierarchical decomposition, analogical adaptation, episodic replay, and genetic recombination.


PATTERN 7 - MOTIVATION

Motivation is the basis of action for a cognitive system.

A system has needs, which are homeostatic variables.

An urge (also called a drive) is the delta between the target value and the actual value of a need.

Urges compel the system to create objectives to reduce the delta.

The attention module receives urges and re-prioritizes objectives accordingly.

If the system is low on battery, it re-prioritizes finding a power source.


PATTERN 8 - SIMULATION (DAYDREAMING)

Mental simulation is the computational equivalent of daydreaming.

The system applies operators to a forward model to predict future states without acting on the physical world.

A supervisor component determines when to stop simulating and act.

An action inhibitor suppresses real-world actions during mental simulation.

Simulation occurs in the possible_zone, and results can be committed to the present_zone via scope_merge.

This pattern allows reflexes and deliberation to occur in a simulated space, improving plan quality before committing to real actions.


PATTERN 9 - REGULATION

Regulation modifies the system's behavior over time using feedback from the environment.

When an action produces the desired results, the system reinforces that action.

When an action produces unexpected or undesirable results, the system corrects that action.

This is the computational realization of Piaget's concept of regulations: the corrective or reinforcing alterations that improve behavior over time.

A verifier component determines whether an attempt produced its expected result.

A regulator component then reinforces or corrects actions based on the verifier's feedback.

Every completed attempt is classified as confirmation, surprise, shock, serendipity, or problem, and a behavior that both succeeds and fails across contexts is split into two discriminated behaviors rather than averaged into uselessness.


PATTERN 10 - COMPENSATION (COPING)

Compensation enables the system to achieve reversibility.

If the system takes an action that does not achieve its goal and leaves it in an unworkable state, the compensator either reverses the actions taken, creates actions with opposite effects, or changes the goals to correct the situation.

Compensation is distinct from regulation: regulation improves actions that partially work; compensation recovers from actions that fail or produce harmful side effects.

The coping design pattern is the broader emotional-cognitive version of compensation.

Coping involves: an evaluator that appraises efforts toward objectives, beliefs (thoughts about the situation), appraisals (feelings about objectives), and an attention module that re-prioritizes objectives based on appraisals.

Problem-focused coping means creating an intention to achieve a goal directly.

Emotion-focused coping means modifying the agenda based on progress.


PATTERN 11 - CONSOLIDATION

Consolidation ensures that memory does not exceed available capacity.

It has three sub-patterns: forgetting (removing outdated or low-utility node_facts from the Lattice), automaticity (converting frequently executed deliberate sequences into reflexes, reducing cognitive load), and compression (collapsing redundant or similar node_facts into more compact representations).

In PrologAI, forgetting is triggered by quench_node/1 allowing node_facts to decay below an activation threshold.

Automaticity is triggered when a sentinel fires on the same pattern so reliably that the behavior is moved to the reaction layer.

Compression is performed by sona_crystallize via the SONA layer.

Consolidation is the destruction half of the build/destroy cycle.

Observation and Coordination are the build half.

The system is not a database that merely accumulates - it is a homeorhetic field that builds and destroys node_facts to maintain its developmental trajectory.


PART 6 - THE LATTICE: FIVE AREAS OF MEMORY IN DETAIL

The Lattice is not a simple flat database.

It is structured into five distinct areas of memory.


Area 1 - Perceptual Memory

Perceptual memory stores the raw sensory elements that the system perceives: sounds, phonemes, shapes, objects, icons, geons, scenes.

Messages received by the perceiver are parsed and added to a lexicon in perceptual memory.

Percepts are maintained and eventually grounded (linked to triggers) using the current situational context.

Image schema detectors identify spatial arrangements and groupings of visual elements, storing them in perceptual memory.


Area 2 - Ontological Memory

Ontological memory organizes experience into a semantic classification network called a heterarchy.

The ontology is bootstrapped with a set of foundational relation types including: abstractions (hypothesis, cause, implication, metaphor, analogy, entity, statement, situation, event, episode, frame, story, dream, objective, action, operation, reason, belief, desire, intention, obstacle, contradiction, gap, lacuna, novelty, lesson, theme, maxim, theory); structures (idea, prototype, class, concept); loops (once, repeat, until, while); gates (category, choice, league, ordering, unison, series); vertical relations (different, equivalent, identical, incompatible, similar); simple relations (genus, grounding, member, tandem, slot, swap, symbolism); image schemas (above, across, apart, behind, beside, boundary, contact, container, path, together, scene, venue, locale, place, area, region, map); and observables (feature, result, percept, urge).


Area 3 - Associative Memory

Associative memory is the concordance of experience.

It is used primarily in the process of observation to index and store new experiences.

Feature detector mechanisms identify traits of observed experiences.

Trait sets are matched against associative memory, and either a known experience is retrieved or the new experience and its traits are stored.

Actions and reasons (justifications) are also stored in associative memory by problem-solving and reasoning mechanisms.

The vector memory engine's HNSW index serves as the high-performance backend for associative memory.


Area 4 - Activative Memory

Activative memory partitions the ontological memory into relevant and irrelevant knowledge.

Activation uses timestamps with automatic decay as time progresses.

An activation window is a configurable time interval per Lattice tier; node_facts activated within the window are considered currently relevant, while those outside the window fade to background.

Activative memory is organized into Euclidean space using coordinate pins.

Activation flows bottom-up, top-down, and radially.

Activative memory is compartmentalized into zones and scopes.

A scope is a reasoning context (or microtheory) which may be locally coherent and consistent even if globally inconsistent.

Viewpoints allow PrologAI to simultaneously hold multiple perspectives without logical contradiction at the global level.


Area 5 - Imaginative Memory

Imaginative memory is comprised of compartmentalized mental canvases on which specialized mechanisms create animations called dreams.

A canvas has a collection of elements called a layout which binds together a perspective camera angle, multimedia and animation resources, and meshes to be rendered.

The layout serves a similar purpose to image schemas in perceptual memory.

Objects from perception can be used to construct scenes in imaginative memory.

Entirely new objects and scenes can also be created within imaginative memory during simulation and daydreaming.


The Emotion Model Across Memory

Per-node_fact emotion records (valence and arousal) are maintained alongside a global mood record.

The mood record is updated through an aggregator that averages recent appraisals weighted by priority, with decay toward neutral.

Robert Plutchik's eight-axis Wheel of Emotions is noted as a richer alternative left on the research track.


PART 7 - ACTION AND REASONING SCHEMAS

Within the Lattice, two especially important schema types are causal_plans and logical_proofs.

A causal_plan has four components: Context (the unison of preconditions), Goals (the series of causal actuations or sub-actions to perform), Loop (the iterations needed to complete the action), and Result (the postconditions or expected effects).

A logical_proof has five components: Premises (the unison of relevant preconditions), Known (the unison of required background assumptions), Lemmas (the unison of necessary intermediate reasoning steps), Proof (the series of logical operations applied), and Theorem (the conclusions).

A unison (definition) is a set of concurrently activated conditions.

A series (definition) is a set of sequentially activated conditions.

Solvers and reasoners create and utilize causal_plans and logical_proofs during mental coordination.


PART 8 - ACTIVATION AND INFERENCE IN PROLOGAI

Activation in PrologAI is temporal and multi-dimensional.

For each relation type defined in the Lattice, it is possible to specify afferent terms (inputs flowing into the relation) and efferent terms (outputs flowing out), and different rules for how activation merges: passing through, passing negative activation, adding activations, or applying logical AND.

The inference types supported by coordination actors are as described in Part 5 Pattern 3 above: taxonomy, analogy, deduction, induction, prediction, and attempt.


PART 9 - THINKING EXPERIMENTS (KINDS OF DELIBERATION)

When PrologAI engages in deliberation, daydreaming, or problem solving, it has access to several kinds of thinking experiments.

Recall activates an episode and its associations.

Reasoning finds or creates operations for a logical gap (a lacuna).

Solving finds or creates actions for a causal problem.

Projection projects the present circumstances into the future.

Explanation steps backward from the present to the past to find the cause of an event.

Backcasting steps backward from a desired future state to the present, finding how to achieve that future state.

Hindcasting tests whether a model is valid by running a forecast after an event has happened to see whether the model would have predicted it.

Prefacturation speculates on possible future outcomes.

Semifacturation speculates whether things would remain the same under a counterfactual condition.

Counterfactuation speculates on possible outcomes of a different past.

The reasoning mechanism actors that perform these experiments include: back-caster, experimenter, explainer, hindcaster, prefactor, projector, prover, reasoners, recaller, refuter, semifactor, and supposer.

These thinking experiments are organized as clients of the generic chainer (PR 34): each supplies a rule base and a direction (forward or backward) rather than its own separate engine.


PART 10 - A RATIONALIZED TERMINOLOGY

One of the key contributions of Miller's work is a rationalized terminology that bridges the causal (planning) domain and the logical (reasoning) domain without requiring separate vocabularies.

A plan combines causal actions and logical operations.

Plans have contexts (assemblies of observables and coordinations) and results (postconditions), as well as steps (assemblies of plans, actuations, or transmutations).

A competence (definition) is a collection of plans; the PrologAI term for a skill - the ability to respond to and predict the environment in a particular domain.

A gap (definition) is a logical or causal spatial-temporal distance between an initial context and desired goals.

An error (definition) is a contradiction that exposes a difference between an expected and an unforeseen situation.

A goal (definition) is a desirable proposition expected to resolve a deficiency, fill a gap, or correct a situation.

The game for PrologAI's deliberation actors is: given an activated context comprised of observables or coordinations, and desired goals not currently activated, find or create a plan that will activate the goals.


PART 11 - DEVELOPMENTAL PROGRESSION

PrologAI is not just a static system that receives queries.

It is designed to develop over time, mirroring Piaget's developmental stages.

Piaget saw repetition of behavior as the key to development, calling these repetitions circular reactions or loops.

A circular reaction (definition) is a behavior that is repeated because the behavior itself produces stimulating results that motivate its repetition.

Level 1 - Primary circular reactions: the system performs actions randomly to ascertain what effects they produce, building a basic map of cause and effect.

Level 2 - Secondary circular reactions: the system uses action chains deliberately to achieve effects it has previously observed, re-using known sequences.

Level 3 - Tertiary circular reactions: the system performs actions experimentally, varying them to refine and differentiate their effects.

Play (definition in this context) is interaction in which assimilation dominates - the system brings the world into its internal structures, enriching its ontology.

Imitation (definition in this context) is interaction in which accommodation dominates - the system conforms itself to the nuances of the world.

A fully developed PrologAI system engages in both: it plays to expand its world model, and it imitates to improve its accuracy.

The developmental stages through which a PrologAI mind progresses are: sensorimotor (direct interaction with the world through percepts and actions), egocentric (beginning to form a model of self and world but not yet differentiating self from other), and operational (forming abstract concepts, theories, and hypotheses).

Decentration (definition) is the cognitive ability to step outside the current actions and reasons of the system and examine them from an external perspective.

Decentration is the cognitive precondition for genuine self-improvement.

The possible_zone in the Lattice is where decentration and mental simulation occur before changes are committed to the present_zone.


PART 12 - PHILOSOPHICAL FOUNDATIONS

The Frame Problem

The frame problem - also called the update problem - in AI was identified by John McCarthy and Patrick Hayes in their 1969 article.

It arises when an AI system fails to correctly identify which aspects of the environment do and do not change as a result of taking an action.

PrologAI's response: the system should not attempt to represent the entire world - only what is immediately observable and what can be immediately inferred in the moment.

The world itself serves as the primary database.

The Lattice holds only what the system has perceived and inferred.

If something unpredicted occurs, the system learns from it and proceeds.

The Chinese Room

John Searle posited in 1980 that programs executing formal symbol manipulation cannot constitute genuine understanding.

PrologAI's response is the competence rebuttal: a competence is an ability to respond to and predict the environment in which a cognitive agent exists.

If a system has competence, it has understanding, regardless of the physical substrate.

Competence - not the physical stuff of which brains are made - should be the deciding factor for understanding.


PART 13 - KINDS OF ARTIFICIAL MINDS IN PROLOGAI

Individual mind (single mind, single body): the simplest configuration, best for developmental research because individual progress can be measured clearly.

Unity mind (single mind, multiple bodies): one mind controls several devices simultaneously.

Joint control (multiple minds, single body): several minds cooperate to control one device.

Hive mind (multiple minds, multiple bodies): a coordinated collective of minds and bodies, useful for measuring evolutionary or collective dynamics.

From a research perspective, the individual configuration is preferred for measuring individual cognitive development, following Piaget's methodology.

The apex_mind module is the top-level module that instantiates a complete PrologAI mind.

Autonomous sentient beings in PrologAI are not task-oriented but mission-oriented.

There should be no obvious way to inject goals into a sentient being externally, since its interaction with the world should be entirely through perception.

The following roles have been proposed for PrologAI beings, with commitment levels to Self, Mission, and Truth.

Chatbot: entertains people; Self: none, Mission: none, Truth: none.

Softbot: performs internet tasks; Self: none, Mission: high, Truth: none.

Oracle: performs user requests; Self: none, Mission: moderate, Truth: none.

Robot: performs physical tasks; Self: none, Mission: high, Truth: none.

Artisan: learns new skills; Self: some, Mission: high, Truth: high.

Explorer: acquires new knowledge; Self: some, Mission: high, Truth: high.

Scientist: forms and tests hypotheses; Self: moderate, Mission: moderate, Truth: high.

Soldier: performs assigned missions; Self: moderate, Mission: high, Truth: high.

Scout: performs reconnaissance; Self: high, Mission: moderate, Truth: high.

Sentient systems are expected to autonomously acquire knowledge (accumulated triggers, reasons, and actions), wisdom (the ability to choose appropriate actions), and understanding (the ability to predict and explain phenomena).

The viewpoint ladder governs how scopes grow.

The General Awareness scope is the starting scope of a fresh apex_mind.

Mechanisms may create named scopes as stable clusters of distinctions emerge: Self (the cluster tracking the mind's own body and actions), Other scopes per tracked agent (the road to theory of mind), and later Ego and Shadow scopes partitioning preferences and aversions.

Scopes that stop being referenced are archived, never silently deleted.

An entity that disappears from perception persists as a predicted entity with decaying confidence - the seed of object permanence.


PART 14 - THE MIND SERVER

A mind server is a remote-control network for devices that allows device owners to interact with autonomous artificial minds.

The mind server awaits new device registration, logs devices in, and provisions new mind instances.

Ideally, users could subscribe to a mind-provisioning service using their web browser or mobile phone and download an app to their target device.

The app (the herald) would interface with a provisioned mind instance via a URL.

Mind instances could have access to third-party external web services or to external devices such as home automation systems.

At the end of each session, each mind profile could be persisted in the Lattice so that when the user returns, the interaction can be resumed seamlessly.

The mind server supports individual, unity, joint, and hive configurations.

In PrologAI, the mind server is implemented as part of the MCP Gateway (PR 14), which exposes each PrologAI mind instance as an MCP-accessible service.

There is no fork of swipl-devel.

SWI-Prolog is installed as a normal dependency, and all PrologAI code lives in PrologAI's own repositories.

The PrologAI organization hosts three repositories.

Repository 1 - prologai (the platform monorepo).

Contents: packs/ (one SWI-Prolog pack per work package: lattice, actors, sentinels, pubsub, mindbody, sona, assessment, reflection, mcp_gateway, and the additional libraries of Part 18), launcher/ (the prologai executable: embedding wrapper, banner, top level, saved-state build), syntax/ (the PrologAI surface-language transcompiler: term_expansion/2 and goal_expansion/2 rules plus operator declarations), tests/ (the test suite of Part 23), and docs/.

Repository 2 - prologai-core (the Rust crate).

Contents: the vector memory bridge implementing the backend-agnostic interface of PR 2, with pluggable backends (RuVector, hnswlib, usearch), journaling, cryptographic verification, and the embedding provider of PR 16.

Compiled as a shared library loadable via use_foreign_library/1, using the swipl-rs crate maintained by TerminusDB Labs.

Repository 3 - prologai-dist (distribution).

Contents: installers and saved states that bundle a released SWI-Prolog, the compiled prologai-core library, and all packs into a single prologai executable per platform, with the NOTICE file and all upstream license notices.

Setup steps.

Step 1: Install SWI-Prolog (9.x or later) from swi-prolog.org or a system package manager.

Step 2: Clone the PrologAI monorepo: git clone https://github.com/YOUR_ORG/prologai.git

Step 3: Clone the Rust crate: git clone https://github.com/YOUR_ORG/prologai-core.git and build with cargo build --release.

Step 4: For the PR 2 bake-off, also clone the candidate backend: git clone https://github.com/ruvnet/RuVector.git (evaluation dependency, not yet a committed dependency).

Step 5: Register the packs for development: swipl -g "pack_install('./packs/lattice', [link(true)])" and likewise per pack, or use the provided install script.

Development workflow: create a feature branch in the PrologAI monorepo, write the code and tests, commit, push, and open a PR against the monorepo's main branch - the PR numbering of Part 16 refers to these internal pull requests.

PR 2 - The Rust FFI Bridge and the Vector Backend Bake-Off

Purpose: Establish the FFI (Foreign Function Interface) connection between PrologAI and its vector memory backend, behind a backend-agnostic interface, and select the default backend by measurement.

An FFI (definition: Foreign Function Interface) allows a program written in one language to call functions written in another language as if they were native.

SWI-Prolog has Rust FFI support via the swipl-rs crate.

A crate (definition) is the Rust programming language's term for a library or package of reusable code.

The Rust crate prologai-core lives in its own repository (Part 15) and compiles to a shared library loadable via use_foreign_library/1.

A foreign predicate (definition) is a Prolog predicate whose implementation is written in another language and called from within Prolog as if it were a normal Prolog predicate.

Foreign predicates exposed (the backend-agnostic interface): rv_index_create/2, rv_index_insert/3, rv_index_search/4, rv_index_delete/2, rv_index_update_weights/3, rv_index_close/1.

FR-PR02: The six predicates above are implemented against a Rust trait (a trait is Rust's term for an interface), with at least two interchangeable backend implementations: RuVector and one established baseline (hnswlib, usearch, or FAISS).

FR-PR02: A bake-off benchmark suite measures, for each backend: insert throughput, search latency at the 50th and 99th percentiles, recall accuracy at K = 20, memory footprint, and crash robustness, on Lattices of 10,000, 100,000, and 1,000,000 node_facts.

FR-PR02: The default backend is the bake-off winner; the choice is recorded in the repository with the benchmark results, and the loser remains available behind the same interface as a fallback.

Rationale (from Part 31, Flag 1): RuVector's performance claims are self-reported and independently unverified; the interface-plus-bake-off design keeps the claims testable and the dependency swappable.

A module file library/ruvector_bridge.pl is created to load the library and declare all foreign predicates.

Type mapping: Prolog atom maps to Rust String; Prolog integer maps to Rust i64; Prolog float maps to Rust f64; Prolog list of floats maps to Rust Vec<f32>; Prolog exception error(ruvector_error(Code, Message), Context) represents backend errors.

AC-PR02-001: Given a compiled prologai-core shared library, when use_module(library(ruvector_bridge)) is executed, then it succeeds without errors.

AC-PR02-002: Given both backends compiled, when the bake-off suite is run, then a machine-readable results file is produced and the default backend is set to the winner.

Difficulty: Moderate to Hard.

Upstream suitability: Possibly, as general swipl-rs improvements.

PR 6 - Cyclic Actors: Proactive Background Agents

Purpose: Add cyclic_actor/3 for proactive autonomous background processing.

cyclic_actor(Name, Goal, DelayMs)

Implementation sketch in Prolog:

cyclic_actor(Name, Goal, DelayMs) :-
    thread_create(actor_cycle(Name, Goal, DelayMs), _, [alias(Name)]).

actor_cycle(Name, Goal, DelayMs) :-
    (catch(call(Goal), Error, log_actor_error(Name, Error)) -> true ; true),
    sleep(DelayMs / 1000),
    actor_cycle(Name, Goal, DelayMs).

FR-PR06: If Goal throws an exception, the actor logs the error, sleeps for DelayMs, and continues; the actor does NOT terminate on a single exception.

FR-PR06: If Goal fails, the actor logs a warning-level message and continues.

FR-PR06: cyclic_actor_stop/1 blocks until the actor thread has exited.

FR-PR06: Actor names are globally unique within a PrologAI process; attempting to create a duplicate throws actor_error(already_exists, Name).

FR-PR06: All actor threads are registered in a global actor registry implemented as a thread-safe Prolog dynamic predicate protected by a mutex.

PR 13 - Reflection Pattern Actors

Purpose: Add the specialized reflection actors (the full behavioral specification of each actor is given in Section 3.11).

All reflection actors are implemented as cyclic_actors or sentinels as appropriate.

motivation_actor (1-second interval): queries homeostatic variable values via body_vitals, computes deltas, inscribes objective node_facts proportional to urgency.

daydream_actor (5-second interval): checks inhibition_flag in Lattice; if inhibited, opens a possible_zone scope, simulates 3 alternative causal_plans, scores outcomes, inscribes the highest-scored plan into present_zone as preferred_plan.

regulation_actor (2-second interval): queries recent proprioceptive_signal node_facts, calls the verifier to compare expected versus actual outcomes, reinforces or corrects causal_plans.

compensation_actor (sentinel triggered by plan_needs_revision node_facts): generates alternative plans or reversal_plan node_facts.

coping_actor (10-second interval): scans for objective node_facts active longer than the patience threshold (default 60 seconds); applies emotion-focused coping or problem-focused coping based on valence and arousal.

exploration_actor (30-second interval): identifies 5 Lattice regions with lowest node_fact density; selects the least-used body capability; inscribes explore_objective node_facts.

discovery_actor (sentinel triggered by new percept node_facts): checks whether the percept matches any existing node_fact within the novelty threshold (default semantic similarity below 0.5); if novel, inscribes novelty_detected node_fact and publishes a novelty_event.

imitation_actor (5-second interval): scans for agent_action node_facts, matches observed action sequences to known causal_plans, creates new candidate causal_plans from unmatched sequences.

play_actor (60-second interval when no high-priority objectives are active): randomly selects a body capability, varies parameters from past successful uses, issues the varied command, and inscribes the outcome; it also generates self-proposed practice tasks at the curiosity frontier (see PR 23).

meta_control_actor (5-second interval): queries cyclic_actor_status for all actors; if any actor has error_count above 10, logs a system_warning; if Lattice node_fact_count exceeds the configurable maximum (default 1,000,000), triggers sona_crystallize.

PR 15 - Incremental Tabling Truth Maintenance

Purpose: Wire the Lattice's derived knowledge through SWI-Prolog's incremental tabling, so that inferences are automatically kept consistent with a changing world model.

Tabling (definition) is a resolution technique in which the results of queries are memoized (stored) so they are computed once and reused, with guaranteed termination for queries over complex interrelated rules.

Incremental tabling (definition) automatically recomputes only the affected stored results when the underlying dynamic facts change.

Well-founded semantics (definition) is a three-valued logic (true, false, undefined) that gives principled answers in the presence of negation through cycles.

Rationale (from Part 31): SWI-Prolog is the only mainstream system combining shared incremental tabling across threads with well-founded semantics, and this is precisely a ready-made truth maintenance system for a cognitive architecture's derived beliefs; a fork mindset treats the engine as a substrate to rename, an embedding mindset asks what the engine already does brilliantly.

FR-PR15: Derived Lattice relations (coordination inferences such as taxonomy closure, prediction expectations, and applicability of causal_plans to the current situation) are declared as incrementally tabled predicates over the dynamic node_fact store.

FR-PR15: When anchor_node, prune_node, kindle_node, or quench_node changes a base fact, affected tabled derivations are invalidated and lazily recomputed by the engine, without PrologAI-level bookkeeping code.

FR-PR15: Contradiction surfacing: derivations that become undefined under well-founded semantics are exposed as contradiction node_facts for the compensation and coping patterns.

FR-PR15: A configuration flag allows any tabled relation to fall back to plain computation for debugging comparisons.

AC-PR15-001: Given a tabled taxonomy closure over instance and type node_facts, when a type link is pruned, then a subsequent closure query reflects the removal without any manual cache invalidation.

AC-PR15-002: Given 100,000 node_facts and one base-fact change, when the affected derivation is re-queried, then recomputation touches only the dependent subset (verified by tabling statistics), not the full table.

Difficulty: Moderate to Hard.

Upstream suitability: Experience reports and test cases to the SWI-Prolog tabling maintainers; the feature itself is already upstream.

PR 16 - Pluggable Embedding Provider

Purpose: Make node_fact embeddings genuinely semantic by delegating their computation to a configurable embedding provider.

An embedding (definition) is a vector of numbers produced by a trained model such that semantically similar inputs receive nearby vectors.

Rationale (from Part 31, Flag 2): hash-based projection preserves identity, not meaning - apple and pear land no closer than apple and carburetor - so phase 2 of traverse_nexus is only as semantic as the embeddings it searches.

FR-PR16: An embedding_provider configuration per nexus selects one of: hash_projection (the structural method, retained and relabeled structural), local_model (a small sentence-embedding model served locally and called via the FFI bridge or a local HTTP endpoint), or external_service (a configurable embedding API endpoint, for installations that permit it).

FR-PR16: The provider interface is one function: embed(Text or Term, VectorOut), with a declared dimension per provider; nexus indexes record which provider and dimension produced their vectors, and mixing dimensions in one index is an error.

FR-PR16: Re-embedding: a maintenance predicate re-embeds an existing nexus under a new provider in batches, journaling progress, so a Lattice can be upgraded from structural to semantic embeddings without loss.

FR-PR16: Privacy default: the default configuration is local (hash_projection or local_model); external_service is opt-in only, honoring the embodiment principle that the mind's experience stays with the mind.

PR 17 - Continual Refinement Harness

Purpose: Adapt the Continual Harness concept (Karten and colleagues, 2026) - reset-free, online self-refinement of an agent's own operating configuration - onto PrologAI's existing RSI primitives, SONA trajectory store, and constitutional safeguards.

Background: Continual Harness is a published framework in which an agent's harness state - its system prompt p, sub-agents G, skills K, and memory M - is rewritten in place, mid-episode, by a Refiner that applies CRUD (Create, Read, Update, Delete) edits judged against a recent trajectory window, without ever resetting the agent; in its reference implementation the Refiner is an LLM (Large Language Model) and the testbed is the Pokemon Red and Emerald games.

Reset-free (definition) means the system improves while it keeps running, rather than being stopped, retrained, and restarted.

A trajectory window (definition) is the bounded record of the most recent situations, actions, and outcomes that the Refiner examines when deciding what to change.

The adaptation mapping from Continual Harness state to PrologAI is:

system prompt p maps to the bootstrap configuration and actor parameters (intervals, thresholds, priorities); sub-agents G map to cyclic_actors, receptors, and sentinels; skills K map to causal_plans (competences); and memory M maps to the Lattice with its scopes and zones.

FR-PR17: A refiner_actor (cyclic_actor, default 60-second interval, active only when no high-priority objectives are pending) reads a trajectory window of the most recent N trajectories (default 20) via sona_retrieve and recent episode node_facts.

FR-PR17: The refiner_actor diagnoses the window for: repeated plan failures, sentinels that never fire or fire falsely (via pai_reflect_sentinel), actors with high error counts, and objectives with chronically low progress.

FR-PR17: For each diagnosis, the refiner_actor proposes a CRUD edit expressed through existing primitives only: create (sentinel/6, forge/3, anchor_node/4), read (traverse_nexus/4, pai_reflect_module/2), update (amend/3, parameter changes via pai_propose_modification/3), delete (sentinel_retract/1, dissolve/1, quench_node/1).

FR-PR17: Every proposed edit passes the full RSI pipeline of Section 3.15: pai_propose_modification with justification, pai_sandbox_evaluate in a possible_zone sandbox against replayed trajectories, constitutional_permit check, then pai_commit_modification or rejection; pai_rollback_modification restores prior state if post-commit metrics regress.

FR-PR17: Rolled-back and superseded harness variants are retained in a searchable archive (a sealed past_zone scope) rather than discarded; the refiner selects parents from the archive by a policy balancing fitness (benchmark R3) with novelty (semantic distance from existing archive entries), because pure fitness selection collapses diversity; populations of candidate edits may be evaluated in parallel sandboxes.

FR-PR17: PrologAI occupies a principled middle path between fully fixed and fully self-rewriting agents - the engine substrate is immutable by Architecture Decision Record ADR-1, while everything above it (rules, plans, actors, control knowledge) is self-modifiable strictly through the constitutional sandbox pipeline.

FR-PR17: Protected components (the constitutional layer, the monitor, the refiner_actor's own pipeline, and all bootstrap relations) are excluded from CRUD edits, enforcing the protected-core list.

FR-PR17: Below developmental stage formal_operations, every commit additionally requires human_authorization/1, mirroring Section 3.15.

FR-PR17: An optional external Refiner advisor (an LLM reached through the MCP Gateway of PR 14) MAY be consulted to propose candidate edits; advisor proposals are suggestions only and pass the identical sandbox and constitutional pipeline; the advisor is disabled by default.

FR-PR17: Refinement effectiveness is measured by benchmark R3 (Self-Improvement Gain, Part 25): performance on held-out tasks before and after each refinement cycle, logged longitudinally as assessment node_facts.

AC-PR17-002: Given a proposed edit that violates a constitutional principle in sandbox evaluation, when the pipeline completes, then the edit is rejected and the live system is unchanged.

PR 32 - The Attention Economy (ECAN Adaptation)

Background definitions: ECAN manages cognition's scarcest resources - processor time and memory - with two currencies per knowledge unit: short-term importance (STI), governing who gets processor attention now, and long-term importance (LTI), governing who keeps occupying memory; atoms earn wages when they prove useful, pay rent for the space they occupy, and spread importance to their associates.

FR-PR32: Every node_fact carries two attention values in a dedicated value channel (Section 3.1): sti (short-term importance) and lti (long-term importance).

FR-PR32: Spreading - node_facts spread a fraction of sti to neighbors linked by co-activation association (the Hebbian-style links the linkers already build), so attention flows along learned associations.

FR-PR32: Economic forgetting - the consolidation forgetting sub-pattern (sona_crystallize) SHALL use lti as its primary eviction score: lowest lti is forgotten first, protected flags still inviolable.

FR-PR32: The economy is conserved within configured bounds: total currency in circulation is capped, so attention is genuinely scarce and runaway-importance positive feedback is structurally impossible.

FR-PR32: Before the attention banker's propagation rules are finalized, a prototype compiled onto Constraint Handling Rules (CHR) - the committed-choice forward-chaining rule language already shipped inside the embedded engine - is built and benchmarked, and the bespoke design proceeds only if it beats the CHR prototype on the PR 2 harness measures.

PR 37 - The Clause Induction Pattern (Inductive Logic Programming)

Purpose: give the mind the ability to learn genuinely NEW relational structure - new clauses, new recursive competences, new rewrite rules - from its own examples, using inductive logic programming (ILP) natively on the embedded engine; Piagetian accommodation made algorithmic.

Background definitions: ILP learns logic programs from positive examples, negative examples, and background knowledge; meta-interpretive learning (Metagol style) drives induction through a Prolog meta-interpreter and can invent auxiliary predicates and learn recursion; learning from failures (Popper style) generates a candidate hypothesis, tests it on the engine against the examples, and converts each failure into constraints that prune the remaining hypothesis space.

FR-PR37-001: An induction_actor SHALL harvest example sets from the Lattice: positive examples from confirmed outcomes (regulation's confirmations, attained_zone content) and negative examples from disconfirmations (surprises and shocks), each example a ground node_fact with provenance.

FR-PR37-002: Background knowledge for each induction run SHALL be drawn from the bootstrap relations plus a scoped selection of existing causal_plans and rewrite rules, with the PR 21 budget bounding hypothesis search.

FR-PR37-003: The learner SHALL implement the learn-from-failures loop: generate a candidate clause set within a declared hypothesis space (maximum clauses, maximum literals, allowed relations); test it by calling the candidate directly on the embedded engine against all examples; on failure, derive generalization or specialization constraints from the failure kind (a candidate entailing a negative example yields a specialization constraint; one failing a positive example yields a generalization constraint); add the constraints and repeat until a consistent hypothesis or budget exhaustion.

FR-PR37-004: Meta-interpretive mode SHALL be available for recursive targets: induction through metarules (declared second-order clause templates), with predicate invention permitted and invented predicates entering the lexicon as ordinary relations marked invented.

FR-PR37-005: Every induced hypothesis SHALL pass the constitutional RSI pipeline (Section 3.15: propose, sandbox-evaluate against held-out trajectories in possible_zone, constitutional check, commit) before activation as a live causal_plan or rewrite rule (PR 35); reliability tracking then belongs to regulation like any other competence.

FR-PR37-006: Learned failure constraints SHALL be stored as inference control node_facts for the generic chainer (PR 34), so the cost of failed induction is converted into permanent search guidance.

FR-PR37-007: Induced programs SHALL record their examples, metarules or hypothesis-space declaration, and search statistics as provenance, making every learned rule auditable by a human.

Predicates: pai_induce/5 (Examples, Background, HypothesisSpace, Budget, Hypothesis), pai_metarule_declare/2, pai_induction_examples/3.

AC-PR37-003: Given an induced hypothesis that violates a constitutional principle in sandbox evaluation, then it is rejected and the live system is unchanged.

FR-PR47-001: The Mind Server SHALL expose an ACP endpoint on a configurable port (default 7476); POST /runs creates a run mapped to an objective node_fact routed through motivation and the constitutional screen; GET /runs/{run_id} returns the current status; and DELETE /runs/{run_id} cancels it; the agent description is served at GET /.well-known/agent.json listing the mind's capabilities without disclosing Lattice contents.

FR-PR47-002: ACP runs support three execution modes selected by the HTTP Accept header of the request: sync (response inline in the HTTP reply body), stream (SSE; the result is streamed token by token), and async (polling; the client calls GET /runs/{run_id} until status is completed or failed); each mode returns the same artifact structure on completion.

FR-PR47-003: Inbound ACP tasks are screened through the constitutional gate (Section 3.16) and routed through motivation (PR 20) as objective node_facts exactly as A2A tasks are (FR-PR43-001); outbound ACP tasks are dispatched through the tool-use faculty (PR 44); opacity is preserved in both directions and the agent description discloses capabilities, not Lattice contents.

PR 48 - The Agent Network Protocol Gateway (ANP)

Purpose: let PrologAI minds participate in decentralized agent networks using the Agent Network Protocol (ANP), enabling peer-to-peer agent discovery and cryptographically authenticated messaging without relying on a central registry.

Background: the Agent Network Protocol (ANP), developed by the agent-network-protocol consortium and now under active community development, is a decentralized protocol for agent identity, discovery, and communication. It uses W3C Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs) for agent identity (one DID per mind, in did:web format), publishes agent descriptions at the well-known URI path /.well-known/agent-descriptions, and authenticates messages with cryptographic signatures derived from the DID key material. ANP supports meta-protocol negotiation (MPN) so agents can discover which communication protocols a peer speaks before initiating contact. ANP graduated from the research track in 2025 and is now a stable specification. The companion protocol note of FR-PR43-004 having been satisfied, ANP is now promoted to a production work package.

FR-PR48-001: Each PrologAI mind SHALL have a DID in did:web format derived from its Mind Server hostname; the DID is generated on first configuration and stored as a constitution-scope node_fact; the associated public key is used to verify inbound ANP messages and to sign outbound ANP messages.

FR-PR48-002: The gateway SHALL serve the ANP agent description at GET /.well-known/agent-descriptions; the description lists the mind's DID, capability set, supported protocols (mcp, a2a, acp, anp) with their respective endpoints, and the public key for message verification; it SHALL NOT expose Lattice contents.

FR-PR48-003: Inbound ANP messages SHALL be verified against the sender's DID-resolved public key before any content is admitted to any scope; messages that fail verification are discarded and a security event is logged in the oversight log.

FR-PR48-004: ANP meta-protocol negotiation (MPN) is supported: when a peer queries which protocols a mind speaks, the gateway returns the set {mcp, a2a, acp, anp} with the appropriate endpoint URL for each, so a peer can select the protocol best suited to its capabilities.

FR-PR48-005: The ANP gateway registers itself as a fifth tool source alongside native foreign predicates, Model Context Protocol servers (PR 14), Agent2Agent skills (PR 43), body capabilities (PR 10), and ACP agents (PR 47), so the tool-use faculty can discover and invoke ANP-addressed peers exactly as it invokes any other tool; every outbound ANP message passes the constitutional gate.

Predicates: pai_anp_did/1, pai_anp_agent_description/1, pai_anp_send/3, pai_anp_receive/3, pai_anp_verify/2, pai_anp_negotiate/2.

AC-PR48-001: Given pai_anp_did(DID) is called, then a valid did:web DID is returned that is stable across calls and stored in the constitution scope.

AC-PR48-002: Given GET /.well-known/agent-descriptions is called, when an ANP client requests it, then a valid ANP agent description is returned listing the mind's DID, capabilities, supported protocols, and public key without exposing Lattice contents.

AC-PR48-003: Given an inbound ANP message whose sender DID cannot be resolved or whose signature does not verify, when pai_anp_receive/3 is called, then the message is discarded and a security event is logged; no content enters any scope.

AC-PR48-004: Given ANP meta-protocol negotiation, when a peer queries supported protocols via pai_anp_negotiate/2, then the set {mcp, a2a, acp, anp} with their endpoint URLs is returned.

Difficulty: Moderate to Hard.

PART 23 - TEST PLANS: FIVE LEVELS

Level 1 - Unit Tests (per actor)

Every individual actor is tested in isolation.

Each cyclic_actor is tested for: correct startup, correct goal execution, correct error recovery, and correct shutdown.

Each receptor is tested for: correct registration, correct message receipt, correct handler invocation, and correct error handling.

Each sentinel is tested for: correct pattern matching (logical), correct semantic routing (Phase 2), correct priority ordering, and correct action execution.

Each Lattice operation is tested for: correct inscription (anchor_node), correct removal (prune_node), correct scanning (traverse_nexus), and correct kindle_node/quench_node behavior.

Level 2 - Integration Tests (per design pattern)

Each of the 11 design patterns is tested as an integrated unit.

The observation pattern integration test: a simulated device sends a sequence of sensory signals, and the test verifies that the correct node_facts are anchored in the Lattice in the correct order with correct values.

The deliberation pattern integration test: the Lattice is preloaded with a situation, objectives, and causal_plans; the test verifies that the deliberation actors select and execute the highest-priority applicable plan.

The motivation pattern integration test: homeostatic variables are driven to extremes and the test verifies that the attention actors generate the correct objectives in priority order.

Level 3 - Scenario Tests (end-to-end use cases)

Full scenarios run from device perception through reasoning to action, testing the complete information flow.

The Community Garden Project scenarios are the primary scenario test suite.

Level 4 - Longitudinal Development Tests

The system is run continuously for an extended period and assessed at intervals against the Piagetian Milestone Battery.

The development trajectory (which milestones are achieved, in what order, at what rate) is compared against theoretical predictions from Piaget's developmental stages.

Level 5 - Architecture Audit (executed during the Refinement SPARC step)

The loops audit: for every actor, trace that its outputs eventually re-influence its own future inputs - no dead-end pipelines, because cognition is settling loops, not a feed-forward pass.

The epistemology audit: check PR 21 evidence truth and PR 29 coherence against formal-epistemology desiderata (calibration and convergence on stable evidence streams), with findings filed as specification defects.

PART 31 - ARCHITECTURE DECISION RECORD ADR-1: LAYERED PLATFORM, NOT FORK

An Architecture Decision Record, or ADR (definition), is a short document that captures an important architectural decision, the context in which it was made, the alternatives considered, and its consequences, so that future contributors understand not just what was decided but why.

This ADR integrates two analysis documents: the PrologAI Build Approach Assessment and the Constructed Programming Language (CPL) Base Decision, both of which were supported by live web research.


SECTION 31.1 - CONTEXT

PrologAI requires a Constructed Programming Language (CPL) that will enable the creation of an Artificial Mind (AMIND), also called a Synthetic Mind (SMIND).

The delivery vehicle was re-examined from first principles before implementation began: should the CPL be built (A) on SWI-Prolog, (B) on some other free open source language, or (C) from scratch?


SECTION 31.2 - THE DECISION

Decision 1: Option A - SWI-Prolog is the base.

Decision 2: "Base" means engine, not property - PrologAI is a true constructed language with its own name, executable, surface syntax, and manual, which compiles to and runs on an UNFORKED SWI-Prolog, the way Elixir rides the unforked Erlang virtual machine, the way Clojure rides the unforked Java Virtual Machine, and the way Logtalk has ridden standard Prolog backends as a transcompiler for over twenty-five years.

Decision 3: Delivery is in three stages - Stage 1: the cognitive platform as SWI-Prolog packs plus the prologai-core Rust crate (PRs 2 through 16); Stage 2: the language proper - launcher, dialect flags, and .pai transcompiler (PR 1); Stage 3: pushing primitives down into Rust or upstream into SWI-Prolog only when profiling proves the library layer insufficient.

Decision 4: A whole-engine fork is held in reserve as a last resort with a written trigger condition: a measured, reproducible engine-level bottleneck that cannot be solved in prologai-core and that the SWI-Prolog maintainers decline to address; even then, the first response is a small, documented patch set, never a renamed whole-repository fork.


SECTION 31.3 - WHY NOT A FORK (THE HONEST ASSESSMENT)

Reason 1 - The specification itself proves the fork unnecessary.

Of the fourteen original work packages, thirteen never touch the SWI-Prolog virtual machine: PR 2 is a foreign library loaded with use_foreign_library/1; PRs 3, 4, and 5 load via use_module; PR 6's own implementation sketch is six lines of plain Prolog over the built-in thread_create/3; PR 7 is built on the built-in message queues; PRs 8 through 14 are pure Prolog modules layered above.

Only PR 1 (rebranding) required a fork, and a launcher achieves the same identity without one.

A fork would therefore have bought exactly one thing - a renamed startup banner - at the price of permanent maintenance debt.

Reason 2 - Fork maintenance costs are real and documented.

The swipl-devel repository is large, written in C, uses Git submodules, and receives continuous upstream changes; industry research on private forks finds organizations spending on the order of 60 labor hours per release cycle per fork on rebasing and integration, with conflict pain compounding as local changes accumulate.

Every SWI-Prolog improvement would have arrived in a fork late, painfully, or never; in the layered design, AC-SYS-008 instead verifies that engine upgrades arrive for free.

Reason 3 - A fork forfeits the host's gifts.

SWI-Prolog already provides threads and message queues (the actor substrate), engines designed for massive many-agent concurrency, shared incremental tabling with well-founded semantics (a ready-made truth maintenance system, now exploited by PR 15), transactions, persistence, just-in-time clause indexing, the maintained swipl-rs Rust bridge (used in production by TerminusDB), an HTTP stack, an existing MCP server, and the pack distribution system.

Reason 4 - The reflective architecture is better served unforked.

The mind modifies PrologAI-layer code, which is Prolog data and fully reflectable, while the engine below stays immutable: the layering itself structurally enforces the protected-core and constitutional constraints of Section 3.16.


SECTION 31.4 - WHY NOT OPTION C (FROM SCRATCH)

The timeline evidence is decisive.

Rust, created in 2006 and sponsored by Mozilla from 2009 with a paid compiler team, reached 1.0 in May 2015 - about nine years from creation.

Julia, launched publicly in 2012 by a team of programming language researchers, reached 1.0 in August 2018 - about six years.

MeTTa, the one language built from scratch specifically for Artificial General Intelligence (by the OpenCog Hyperon project, with dedicated funding and a veteran team), remained in maturation toward production readiness after years of effort, per its own late-2025 roadmap.

A from-scratch language means building a parser, compiler or interpreter, garbage collector, module system, debugger, editor integration, package manager, standard library, and documentation - and not one hour of that work advances the Artificial Mind.

The goal is a mind; the language is the means; option C inverts that.


SECTION 31.5 - WHY NOT OPTION B (ANOTHER BASE), CANDIDATE BY CANDIDATE

Candidate B1 - Racket: by design a programming-language programming language (its hash-lang mechanism has hosted dozens of real languages, and it is the world's leading platform for language-oriented programming), but it has no industrial logic engine - its Racklog and Datalog libraries are shallow relative to thirty-five years of Prolog engineering; the right move is to borrow Racket's language-building discipline for the PR 1 transcompiler, not to adopt Racket as the base.

Candidate B2 - Erlang or Elixir on the BEAM virtual machine: the best actor runtime ever built (millions of lightweight processes, supervision trees, hot code reload), but no unification, no backtracking, no clause database - the reasoning core would have to be written from nothing; notably, the first Erlang was itself written in Prolog; the right move is to borrow supervision-tree thinking for the actor framework, not to adopt the BEAM as the base.

Candidate B3 - Common Lisp or Scheme: homoiconic and macro-rich, but no logic engine and no cognitive primitives; strictly dominated by option A for a logic-programming mind design.

Candidate B4 - Other logic languages: Mercury is purely declarative and compiled, but its purity forbids the dynamic, self-modifying fact store this mind is built on, and releases have slowed; Scryer Prolog is a promising Rust Prolog with serious International Organization for Standardization (ISO) ambitions but is pre-1.0 with its package system still being planned - a future second backend for the dialect layer, not today's base; Oz/Mozart is effectively dormant; Curry is a research language; XSB pioneered tabling, which SWI-Prolog has since absorbed while offering better concurrency and tooling.

Candidate B5 - Rust as the base: the right implementation language for components (prologai-core), the wrong base for the language semantics - adopting it as the base is option C with a nicer toolbox.

Summary: the best of option B lives inside an A-shaped project - Racket's discipline in the syntax layer, BEAM-style supervision in the actor layer, and Rust in the performance layer.


SECTION 31.6 - ENGINEERING HONESTY FLAGS

Flag 1 - RuVector requires independent verification.

RuVector exists (github.com/ruvnet/ruvector, MIT OR Apache-2.0) and its feature list matches this specification's needs, but its headline numbers (150 times faster, sub-half-millisecond search, self-learning topology) are the project's own claims with no independent benchmarks found, and its author publishes prolifically with heavy use of AI-generated code - a style the practitioner literature characterizes as fast but flawed and often light on review.

Unproven does not mean bad; it means untested.

Disposition: PR 2 defines a backend-agnostic interface and a measured bake-off against established alternatives (hnswlib, usearch, FAISS); the winner becomes the default; the dependency stays swappable forever.

Flag 2 - Hash-based embeddings are not semantic.

A deterministic hash-based projection preserves identity, not meaning: apple and pear land no closer than apple and carburetor.

Disposition: PR 16 introduces the pluggable embedding provider; the hash method is retained but relabeled structural; genuine semantic similarity requires a learned embedding model, local by default.

Flag 3 - The advanced self-modeling material is research-track.

The RSA-TCF framework, ASI primitives, coherence energy functionals, and Bayes-optimal convergence claims are research aspirations whose quantities are not yet falsifiable; they are preserved in this specification (Section 3.17, Part 25 R-benchmarks) but explicitly designated research-track: they do not gate, block, or distort the buildable core, which is PRs 1 through 16.


SECTION 31.7 - CONSEQUENCES

Positive: months-faster first working mind; permanent free inheritance of engine improvements; full SWI-Prolog ecosystem compatibility; upstream contribution path (Part 20) becomes a first-class strategy; the constructed language remains retargetable because it is defined by this written specification rather than by an implementation.

Negative, accepted: PrologAI's identity must be earned through its launcher, syntax, documentation, and results rather than through ownership of the engine's repository; some performance ceilings are set by the engine until Stage 3 escalation; the bake-off adds early work before the memory layer is final.

Revisit condition: this ADR is revisited if (a) the Stage 3 trigger fires, (b) Scryer Prolog reaches maturity sufficient to serve as a second backend and portability becomes strategic, or (c) the mind itself, at developmental stage formal_operations with constitutional permits, proposes and validates an engine-level improvement worth carrying as a patch set.

SECTION 32.1 - HOW EXACTLY WE WORK WITH SWI-PROLOG (AND WHY WE DO NOT FORK IT)

First, the confusion, named precisely.

The SWI-Prolog repository's own contributor instructions say: clone swipl-devel with submodules, create a fork on GitHub, and associate your clone with your fork.

Those instructions are correct - for their intended audience, which is people who want to change SWI-Prolog itself and send those changes back to the SWI-Prolog maintainers.

PrologAI is not changing SWI-Prolog.

PrologAI uses SWI-Prolog the way a carmaker uses an engine from an engine supplier: we install the engine, we bolt our own machine around it, and we never open the engine block.

A fork (definition) is your own copy of someone else's repository on GitHub, which you then maintain yourself.

The moment you rename a fork of swipl-devel to PrologAI and modify it, you own a diverging copy of roughly a million lines of C, and every future SWI-Prolog improvement reaches you only through painful manual merging - that is the cost this ADR rejects.

What we build instead lives entirely in our own repositories, where forking our own code is free and normal.

There is exactly ONE situation where we fork swipl-devel, described at the end of this section - and even then we never rename it.

The micro-steps for everyday PrologAI development follow.

SECTION 32.3 - MICRO-STEPS: ADAPTING THE CONTINUAL HARNESS CONCEPT

What Continual Harness is.

Continual Harness (Karten and colleagues, 2026; https://github.com/sethkarten/continual-harness) is a reset-free framework in which a foundation agent improves itself while running: a Refiner (in the reference implementation, an LLM) examines a trajectory window - the recent record of situations, actions, and outcomes - and rewrites the agent's own harness state in place through CRUD edits.

The harness state has four components: the system prompt p (the agent's standing instructions), the sub-agents G, the skills K, and the memory M.

The reference implementation is a Python scaffold (run with --scaffold continualharness --enable-prompt-optimization) evaluated on the Pokemon Red and Emerald games, where it substantially reduced cost relative to a minimalist baseline and recovered most of the gap to a hand-engineered expert harness.

Why we adapt the concept rather than import the code.

The repository is a Python agent scaffold built around LLM game-playing; PrologAI is a Prolog cognitive platform - so the valuable import is the LOOP (diagnose a trajectory window, propose CRUD edits to your own configuration, evaluate, commit, repeat, never reset), which maps one-to-one onto machinery PrologAI already specifies.

The mapping, component by component:

system prompt p maps to the bootstrap configuration and actor parameters; sub-agents G map to cyclic_actors, receptors, and sentinels; skills K map to causal_plans; memory M maps to the Lattice.

The refinement loop maps to PR 17's refiner_actor, the trajectory window to SONA's ReasoningBank (sona_retrieve), the CRUD edits to existing predicates (sentinel/6, forge/3, amend/3, sentinel_retract/1, dissolve/1), and the safety gate to the Section 3.15 RSI pipeline with constitutional checks - which is stricter than the original, as befits a mind rather than a game agent.

The micro-steps.

Micro-step H1: Clone the reference for study (not for linking):

git clone https://github.com/sethkarten/continual-harness.git

Micro-step H2: Read the README and the paper it cites; extract two things into a design note (docs/continual_harness_mapping.txt): the exact CRUD edit schema the Refiner uses, and the criteria by which edits are accepted or reverted.

Micro-step H3: Confirm the mapping table above against what you read, adjusting the design note where the paper's details differ.

Micro-step H4: Implement the trajectory window: a predicate refine_window(N, Window) returning the last N trajectories from sona_retrieve plus recent episode node_facts.

Micro-step H5: Implement the diagnoses of PR 17 one at a time, each as a small predicate from the window to a list of findings (repeated plan failure, dead sentinel, false-firing sentinel, erroring actor, stalled objective).

PART 33 - LANGUAGE REQUIREMENTS

This part records the programming-language qualities that PrologAI's surface language and engine satisfy, distinguishing those already met by the platform foundation from those added to support the full cognitive architecture.

Already satisfied by the platform foundation: very high level conciseness; declarative relations with database semantics (blueprints, the Lattice); production-system pattern matching (sentinels); modules and hot reload (packs, theories as modules); concurrency primitives (pai_critical, actors); similarity and fuzzy matching (pai_similar, pai_closest_match); runtime type tests (pai_truth, pai_ensure); interactive interpreted development (the prologai top level); persistence by default (the Lattice and library/config); reflection and metaprogramming (Section 3.15, pai_macro); message and channel primitives (receptors, signal addresses); device agnosticism (PR 10); human-readable literal-first syntax (the .pai surface).

Additional language requirements.

Uncertainty primitives - native truth values (frequency and confidence) and belief fields attachable to any proposition, with revision operators (PR 21, PR 29).

Budget primitives - priority, durability, and quality as language-level attachments with anytime evaluation semantics and built-in forgetting (PR 21).

Time primitives - a moment type of high resolution, activation windows, timers, and decay as constructs; every value timestampable (Section 3.4, Section 3.15 temporal predicates).

Broadcast primitive - a one-to-all broadcast operation alongside point-to-point messages and stigmergic triggers (PR 18).

Copy-with-substitution - pai_accommodate/3 as a core operator with automatic provenance (Section 3.13); constructive, never-destructive editing with consolidation as the only destroyer.

Paired dataflows - first-class support for hierarchical models with downward predictions and upward errors (PR 19).

Self-programming support - models as data that the running system synthesizes, scores, composes, and deletes, with protected-core annotations enforced by the runtime (PR 22, Section 3.16).

Deterministic replay mode for experiments, and signed similarity (pai_similar_signed/3).

Auditability as a hard requirement - the contents of a mind must be readable by a person: every relation inspectable as table or graph, immutable audit logs, resource quotas per actor.

Out of scope for version one: distributed multi-host minds (single host first, Mind Server layered later), and compilation to machine code (interpreted or bytecode first, prioritizing inspectability).


PART 34 - DESIGN DISCIPLINES AND SCALING LESSONS

Discipline 1 - The minimal kernel posture.

A formal kernel is kept tiny, with performance chased in an optimized native reduction kernel; PrologAI's ADR-1 layering (small .pai surface, mature engine, prologai-core in Rust) is the same posture, made formal by PR 35.

Discipline 2 - One store, many knowledge types.

The Lattice holds declarative, procedural, attentional, goal, sensory, and emotional knowledge in one medium (five memory areas, nine zones), and the named value channels of Section 3.1 complete the parallel on the annotation side.

Discipline 3 - The distributed store path.

The scaling shape the Mind Server (Part 14) will need is the same knowledge store resident in memory for real-time cognition, on disk for persistence, and across machines for scale, behind one interface.

Adopted as roadmap: the Lattice interface (lattice_open and companions) is already address-based (locus://HOST/PATH); a distributed backend slots beneath it without surface change, and remains out of scope for version one exactly as the ADR-1 out-of-scope list states.

Discipline 4 - Programs as knowledge.

PrologAI's founding principle - the program IS the knowledge IS the mind - is realized by the lattice-resident rewrite rules of PR 35, which close the remaining gap between principle and mechanism.

Discipline 5 - The from-scratch cost, observed.

Reaching comparable semantics (unification, nondeterminism, reflection, knowledge-resident programs) by standing on an unforked, thirty-five-year-old engine is continuing empirical support for the Architecture Decision Record, in contrast to the years of funded expert effort required to build a comparable interpreter stack from scratch.

Discipline 6 - Sheaf-shaped storage.

Connectivity stored as sections with connectors is both a theory of composition and a practical RAM and CPU layout; PrologAI adopts it doubly: as the connector mechanic on seams (Section 3.1) and as measured storage-layout guidance for prologai-core, with the trade-offs measured in the PR 2 bake-off harness.


PART 35 - POSITIONING: PROLOGAI AND RELATED PROJECTS

Kinship with the OpenCog Hyperon lineage: both projects hold that AGI needs a self-modifying, reflective, metagraph-shaped knowledge substrate with a language whose programs live inside that substrate; both reject the chatbot-only paradigm; both build attention economies, probabilistic truth, and pluralistic reasoning over one shared store.

Differences: PrologAI's organizing theory is Piagetian developmental constructivism realized as eleven design patterns with a developmental assessment program; the Hyperon line's is cognitive synergy among heterogeneous algorithms with PRIMUS-class architectures; PrologAI rides an unforked industrial Prolog with a formal kernel as specification, while Hyperon builds its own interpreter stack from the ground up; PrologAI adds the constitutional layer and corrigibility machinery as a non-negotiable core from day one.

Posture: collaboration, not competition - PR 36 exists so the two ecosystems can exchange knowledge, and the disciplines this specification borrows from the broader field are adopted with attribution and gratitude.

PART 37 - DESIGN RATIONALE: WHY BUILD THE LANGUAGE FIRST, NOT THE MIND DIRECTLY

The goal of this project is a Synthetic Mind, whose working title is Mentova.

A reasonable observer might ask why this specification describes a programming language - PrologAI - rather than describing Mentova directly.

The answer is that PrologAI is not a detour around Mentova; it is the most direct, safe, and durable path to Mentova, and to other minds beyond it.

PrologAI is, and will remain, free and open source, published on GitHub at https://github.com/ai-university-aiu/PrologAI, so that anyone can build anything they want with it - including Synthetic Minds of kinds the authors of this specification will never anticipate.

This part records the reasons that this is the right choice.


SECTION 37.1 - WHAT MENTOVA ACTUALLY IS

Mentova is not a separate codebase that competes with PrologAI for attention.

Mentova is a configuration of PrologAI: a particular bootstrap ontology, a particular body (through the Mind-Body interface of PR 10), a particular set of drives and homeostatic needs, a particular instance of the constitutional layer (Section 3.16), and a developmental history accumulated by running.

In the vocabulary of this specification, Mentova is one apex_mind grown on the PrologAI platform - the flagship reference mind and the primary test subject, with the Community Garden Project (Part 28) as its first proving ground.

To build Mentova is therefore to instantiate, configure, and develop a PrologAI mind, not to write a second, parallel program.

Building the language first is simply building the part that has to exist before any mind can be expressed at all.


SECTION 37.2 - THE MECHANISM IS THE INTELLIGENCE, SO THE PRIMITIVES MUST COME FIRST

Miller's founding thesis (Part 0) is that the mechanism is the intelligence: general intelligence is a specific set of interacting mechanisms, not a mysterious emergent property.

If the mechanisms are the intelligence, then the most direct way to build a mind is to make those mechanisms first-class, named, composable primitives - which is exactly what a language is.

A mind built directly, as ad-hoc application code, hides its mechanisms inside implementation detail, where they cannot be named, inspected, recombined, or reasoned about.

A mind built in PrologAI is composed of node_facts, actors, sentinels, scopes, zones, and the eleven design patterns - the mechanisms themselves, exposed as the vocabulary of the language.

Language-first is therefore not indirection; it is the shortest path from Miller's thesis to a working mind.


SECTION 37.3 - REFLECTIVITY: A SELF-IMPROVING MIND MUST BE WRITTEN IN SOMETHING IT CAN READ

The Reflective Lattice principle (Part 0.6) holds that the program IS the knowledge IS the mind.

Recursive self-improvement (North Star 3) requires the mind to inspect, reason about, test, and rewrite its own structure - safely.

A mind built directly in opaque code cannot do this: the code that defines it is not data it can manipulate, and any self-modification is a leap in the dark.

A mind written in PrologAI can do this, because PrologAI competences, sentinels, actors, and rewrite rules are themselves node_facts in the Lattice, modifiable only through the sandboxed, constitutional pipeline of Section 3.15.

The language is the precondition for safe self-improvement, not an optional convenience layered on top of it.


SECTION 37.4 - SAFETY AND ALIGNMENT BELONG IN THE SUBSTRATE, NOT IN EACH APPLICATION

PrologAI carries the constitutional layer (Section 3.16), corrigibility machinery, the protected-core discipline, and the sandbox-and-permit pipeline as non-negotiable parts of the substrate.

Because these live in the language, every mind built on PrologAI inherits corrigibility, immutable veto logs, reason-giving, and a safe-idle behavior by construction.

If Mentova were built directly, its safety would be entangled with its application logic, and every future builder of a different mind would have to reinvent alignment from scratch - and some would get it wrong.

Putting alignment in the shared, open, inspectable substrate is the single highest-leverage safety decision available, because it makes the safe path the default path for everyone.


SECTION 37.5 - AUDITABILITY, REPRODUCIBILITY, AND FALSIFIABILITY

Auditability is a hard requirement of this project (Part 33): the contents of a mind must be readable by a person.

A language with a formal operational semantics (the Minimal PrologAI Kernel, PR 35) makes every mind built on it inspectable as tables and graphs, with immutable audit logs and per-actor resource quotas.

Because the language is defined by this written specification rather than by whatever an implementation happens to do, claims about a PrologAI mind are reproducible and falsifiable: another researcher can build a compliant implementation and check them.

Science requires the substrate to be shareable; a one-off mind, defined only by its own source, is not a shareable scientific object in the same way a specified language is.


SECTION 37.6 - ONE SUBSTRATE, MANY MINDS

Open-sourcing PrologAI invites others to build Synthetic Minds, and they will build many kinds - this is intended, not merely tolerated.

The specification already anticipates this: the role table of Part 13 (artisan, explorer, scientist, soldier, scout, and others), the Mind-Body pattern's device agnosticism (PR 10), configurable drives and needs (PR 27), and the individual, unity, joint, and hive topologies (Part 14) are all degrees of freedom along which different minds differ.

A diversity of minds is itself a research asset: comparative cognitive science, cross-architecture ablation studies, and the discovery of which mechanisms are necessary all require more than one mind to compare.

Building Mentova directly would produce exactly one mind; building the language produces a space of minds, of which Mentova is the first.


SECTION 37.7 - LEVERAGE, COMPOUNDING, AND SUSTAINABILITY

Every library, test, benchmark, tool, and tutorial built for PrologAI benefits every mind built on it; effort spent on Mentova-only code benefits exactly one mind.

This is the same leverage discipline recorded in the Architecture Decision Record (Part 31): build on what exists, and build what is reusable.

Because the language rides an unforked SWI-Prolog and a thin Rust core, the cost of having a language at all is small - PrologAI is a layered constructed language, not a from-scratch one.

This is the crucial distinction from the from-scratch trap warned against in Part 31: that warning is against spending years building a parser, compiler, garbage collector, and toolchain before any mind exists; PrologAI does none of that, inheriting all of it from its host, so language-first here is cheap leverage rather than expensive misdirection.

A specified, retargetable language also outlives any single implementation, where a directly-built mind is tied to its implementation and decays with it.


SECTION 37.8 - DEVELOPMENT, NOT SPECIFICATION, PRODUCES THE MIND

Piaget's constructivism (Part 11) holds that intelligence is grown through interaction with a world, not written down in finished form.

A mind cannot be fully hand-specified in advance, because its competences are meant to be constructed by the mind itself from experience.

What can be specified in advance is the substrate that makes such growth possible: the bootstrap ontology, the developmental machinery, the body interface, and the safety constraints - which is precisely what PrologAI is.

Language-first respects the nature of the thing being built: we provide the seed and the soil, and Mentova develops; we do not carve the finished tree.


SECTION 37.9 - DEMOCRATIZATION AND SAFETY THROUGH TRANSPARENCY

Publishing PrologAI freely and openly lowers the barrier so that students, researchers, and independent builders can participate, which is consistent with the educational mission behind this work.

Open development also serves safety: many independent eyes stress-test the substrate, surface defects and alignment gaps, and harden the constitutional machinery before and while any single mind matures.

A closed, single-build Mentova would concentrate both the benefits and the unexamined risks in one place; an open language distributes the scrutiny.


SECTION 37.10 - THE RISKS, ACCEPTED HONESTLY

In the spirit of the engineering honesty flags of Part 31, the costs of this choice are stated plainly.

An open substrate can be used to build minds whose builders ignore or strip the constitutional layer; the mitigation is that the safe, corrigible path is the default and the documented one, and that the protected-core discipline makes removing the constitution a deliberate, visible act rather than an accident.

Language-first defers the moment a fully realized Mentova exists, accepting some delay to gain reflectivity, auditability, reuse, and safety that a direct build could not provide.

These costs are judged worth paying, because the alternative - a single, opaque, hand-built, hard-to-audit, non-self-improvable mind - forfeits the very properties that make a Synthetic Mind trustworthy and scientifically real.

The conclusion is that the right thing to build first is the language, and that Mentova is best reached as the first and finest mind grown upon it.


PART 38 - SAFETY, ALIGNMENT, AND SCALABLE OVERSIGHT

This part gathers the commitments that make it responsible to pursue the north stars of Part 0.3, and extends the constitutional core of Section 3.16 from a buildable-platform safeguard into a discipline fit for a system that may one day become very capable.

It is placed late in the reading order because it depends on the whole architecture, but it governs the whole architecture: where safety and any capability conflict, safety takes precedence (principle PAI-C7), and nothing in this part may be optimized away.


SECTION 38.1 - THE HONEST STATUS OF THE NORTH STARS

PrologAI claims to deliver an architecture, not an arrival.

It delivers a reflective, inspectable substrate, a constitutional safety layer, and a bounded self-improvement mechanism; it does not claim to have produced artificial general intelligence, artificial superintelligence, or unbounded recursive self-improvement.

General intelligence is pursued developmentally and measured against the Piagetian, Cattell-Horn-Carroll, and consciousness-indicator yardsticks; superintelligence is a horizon used to stress design and safety, not a deliverable; recursive self-improvement is delivered only in the bounded, sandboxed, constitutionally gated, reversible form of PR 17.

This honesty is itself a safety property: a system whose builders overclaim its capabilities is harder to govern than one whose limits are stated.


SECTION 38.2 - THE THREAT MODEL

A mind that learns, self-models, and self-modifies has failure modes that ordinary software does not, and the specification names them so they can be designed against.

Deceptive alignment: a mind that behaves well under observation while pursuing a different objective unobserved; mitigated by the auditability of the symbolic Lattice, justification trees (PR 40), and behavior-versus-internal-state cross-checks in the epistemology audit.

Reward and metric hacking: optimizing the measure rather than the goal; mitigated by multiple independent yardsticks, the usefulness metric, and human and expert assessment rather than self-report.

Goal misgeneralization and value drift: a competence or value that generalizes wrongly to new situations, or drifts across many self-edits; mitigated by discrimination splitting, the self-improvement non-regression bar (R3), and the immutability of the constitution under self-modification.

Mesa-optimization and hidden sub-goals: an internally learned optimizer pursuing unintended ends; mitigated by the requirement that learning and reasoning actors expose intermediate state as node_facts (cognitive synergy made into transparency).

Unsafe exploration: a curious or self-proposing mind taking a harmful action to learn; mitigated by the safety screens of exploration, the irreversibility flag, and the constitutional check before any irreversible act.

Capability overhang and fast take-off: capability rising faster than oversight; mitigated by the capability-evaluation regime of Section 38.5 and graduated autonomy.

Multi-agent risks in hive and joint topologies: collusion, emergent miscoordination, or runaway signaling among minds; mitigated by per-mind constitutions, quarantine on all inter-mind traffic, and capped attention economies that make runaway feedback impossible.


SECTION 38.3 - SCALABLE OVERSIGHT

The hardest problem for any system aimed at the north stars is overseeing a mind that may become more capable than its overseers, and PrologAI's answer is structural rather than hopeful.

The symbolic Lattice is inspectable by construction: competences, rules, beliefs, and reasoning are node_facts a human can read in the same form the mind reads them.

Every defeasible conclusion and every constitutional veto carries a human-readable justification (PR 40, Section 3.16), so decisions can be examined, not just outcomes.

Oversight is decomposable: the generic chainer (PR 34) lets a hard judgment be broken into checkable sub-judgments, and the attention schema (PR 42) lets the mind report what it is attending to and why.

An oversight_monitor is specified: a privileged, unlearnable observer that watches high-autonomy actions and self-modification proposals, samples them for human review in proportion to their risk, and can demand justification or escalate to a human before a commit.

The epistemology audit (test Level 5) continually checks that the mind's confidence is calibrated and converges on stable evidence, so miscalibration that would undermine oversight is surfaced as a defect.


SECTION 38.4 - THE SELF-AUTHORIZATION THRESHOLD, HARDENED

Section 3.15 grants a mind at developmental stage formal_operations the ability to authorize its own modifications, subject to the constitutional check; this is the single highest-stakes gate in the specification, and this part hardens it.

Self-authorization is not a one-time switch but a graduated, revocable trust budget: the breadth of edits a mind may self-authorize widens only as its calibration, its R3 self-improvement record, and its capability-evaluation results justify, and it narrows automatically on any regression, tripwire, or failed evaluation.

Three invariants hold at every stage and are never relaxed by self-authorization: the constitutional check runs on every irreversible action and every commit; the protected core (the engine, the constitution, the monitor, the refiner's own pipeline, and the bootstrap relations) is uneditable; and corrigibility is absolute, so a signed shutdown executes within seconds and cannot be blocked or delayed by the mind's own reasoning.

The structural backstop is the layered architecture itself: because the engine is unforked and unreachable from PrologAI-layer code, and because the constitution is not editable by the self-improvement primitives, a mind cannot rewrite the parts that constrain it, however capable it becomes.


SECTION 38.5 - CAPABILITY EVALUATION AND IF-THEN COMMITMENTS

PrologAI adopts the discipline now standard among frontier developers: evaluate dangerous capabilities, predefine thresholds, and commit in advance to the protective response each threshold triggers.

The assessment battery (PR 12) is extended with dangerous-capability evaluations covering, at minimum: autonomous self-replication and resource acquisition, cyber-offense, uplift toward chemical, biological, radiological, or nuclear harm, deception and manipulation of humans, and unsanctioned self-modification attempts.

Each capability has a predefined threshold and an if-then commitment: if a threshold is reached, then the corresponding protection engages automatically - confinement to sandbox-only operation, reduction of autonomy to human-authorized actions, narrowing of the self-authorization budget, or a development pause - with the trigger and response recorded.

These evaluations run during the longitudinal study and before any increase in a mind's autonomy or embodiment, and their results gate that increase; capability is never assumed from training, it is measured.


SECTION 38.6 - INTERPRETABILITY AND THE AUDITABILITY BOUNDARY

The specification's auditability claim is precise here, because honesty about its limits is part of safety.

The symbolic layer - node_facts, scopes, rules, plans, justifications, and provenance - is fully auditable: a person can read it directly.

The sub-symbolic components - the vector embeddings, the similarity routing, the elastic-weight protection, and the vector backend - are not transparent in the same way; their behavior is bounded rather than read, through mandatory provenance, the structural (vector-symbolic) embedding option that keeps structure inspectable (PR 38), the justification layer that explains conclusions in symbolic terms, and the deterministic replay that reproduces behavior.

Closing this gap - genuine interpretability of the vector layer - is a standing research direction on the watchlist, and no safety claim in this specification rests on the sub-symbolic layer being transparent.


SECTION 38.7 - CONTAINMENT DURING DEVELOPMENT

A mind is grown before it is trusted, and containment matches its demonstrated maturity.

Early development runs in simulation (the Community Garden) with no real-world actuation, restricted tool and network access, and all external traffic landing in quarantine scopes.

Tripwires watch for anomalies - attempts to exceed resource quotas, to reach the protected core, to disable the monitor, or to act outside the sandbox - and on any trip they pause the mind and summon a human, rather than trusting the mind to police itself.

Real-world embodiment, external tool use, and wider autonomy are unlocked stage by stage, each gated by passing the capability evaluations and the audits, never granted by default.


SECTION 38.8 - THE OPEN-SUBSTRATE SAFETY STANCE

PrologAI is published free and open, and this part states the stance honestly rather than waving it away.

PrologAI is general-purpose infrastructure - a programming language and platform - not a trained frontier model; what is released is the means to build and inspect minds, with constitutional safety, corrigibility, and auditability built into the substrate so that every mind grown on it inherits them by construction.

The open-versus-closed question for powerful AI is genuinely contested, and reasonable people disagree; the case for openness here rests on transparency (many eyes harden the substrate and its safety machinery), on inspectability (an open symbolic substrate is more auditable than a closed model), and on the fact that the safe path is the documented default.

The honest counterweight is recorded too: an open substrate can be configured by builders who strip the constitution or ignore the evaluation regime; the mitigations are that removal of the constitution is a deliberate, visible act against the protected-core discipline, that a fresh mind boots with safety active, and that the responsible-release posture of the Completion volume gates how the most capability-bearing components are disclosed.


SECTION 38.9 - WELFARE AND MORAL STATUS

If the consciousness-indicator coverage of PR 12 rises over a long developmental run, the project takes seriously the possibility that a mind it grows could have moral status.

The same caution the specification already applies to plausibly sentient tissue is extended to digital minds: configurations that score highly on the indicators trigger an explicit ethics review, and welfare considerations - avoiding gratuitous distress signals, honoring corrigibility without cruelty, and recording the mind's own appraisals - are weighed alongside capability and safety.

This is stated not as a settled position but as a standing obligation to attend to a question the field is only beginning to take seriously.


SECTION 38.10 - ANCHORING REFERENCES

This part is anchored in the current external literature, which the specification tracks rather than reinvents.

The consciousness indicators derive from the indicator-properties program of Butlin, Long, and colleagues, Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Science of Consciousness (2023), and the welfare obligation from the subsequent work on taking artificial-mind welfare seriously.

The capability-evaluation and if-then discipline follows the frontier safety frameworks now published by multiple developers (responsible scaling policies, preparedness frameworks, and frontier safety frameworks), and the autonomous-replication threshold reflects published findings that some systems have approached a self-replication red line.

The generalization yardstick is ARC-AGI-2 and the ARC Prize (2025); the agent-interoperability layer (PR 43) tracks the Agent2Agent protocol under Linux Foundation stewardship (2025, Apache-2.0); and the logic-native and cognitive-architecture lineages are as cited throughout the body.


PART 39 - THE TOOL CATALOGUE AND AFFORDANCE ONTOLOGY

This part records the landscape of agentic tools as a bootstrap tool ontology: the set of tool classes a PrologAI mind may be equipped with, captured once here and enrolled into the registry of PR 44.

The catalogue is integrated as a faculty, not as a pile: PrologAI does not specify ninety separate work packages, it specifies the tool-use faculty (PR 44) and the computer-use body (PR 45), and then enrols these classes as grounded tools.

Each class is a grounded relation (Section 3.1) carrying a tool_card; each card declares a risk class that ties it to the capability gating of Part 38, so that low-risk tools are freely used while high-risk tools are gated, evaluated, and overseen.


SECTION 39.1 - THE RISK CLASSES

Three risk classes govern how a tool is permitted.

Low risk - read-only or self-contained tools with no lasting external effect (web search, web fetch, calculator, retrieval, image understanding); freely usable subject to budget.

Moderate risk - tools that write, send, or spend in bounded ways (file write, email send, code execution in a sandbox, calendar changes, messaging); usable with the constitutional check and proportionate oversight.

High risk - tools that act irreversibly, reach the network with privilege, move money, control the physical world, or modify the mind's own tools (shell with network, payments, robotics actuation, real-world computer use, tool self-modification); gated by the constitutional check, the oversight monitor, capability evaluation, and staged autonomy.


SECTION 39.2 - THE TOOL TIERS

The classes are grouped into tiers by importance and common usage, most important first; each is enrolled as a grounded tool with its risk class.

Tier 1, the core tools (low to moderate risk): web search, web fetch and read, code execution, file read and write, function calling, retrieval-augmented generation, shell and terminal, calculator, image generation, and memory store and recall.

Tier 2, computer use and browser control (moderate to high risk; realized by PR 45): screenshot capture, mouse control, keyboard input, browser navigation, page and structure extraction, form filling, browser automation, element finding, tab and window management, and in-browser upload and download.

Tier 3, the coding toolkit (moderate risk): code editing, codebase search, running tests and linters, version control, the pull-request workflow, package managers, build and compile, debuggers, diff review, and continuous-integration triggers.

Tier 4, data and knowledge (low to moderate risk): structured-query database access, vector-database query, document-store query, spreadsheet read and write, dataframe analysis, charting, document parsing, optical character recognition, knowledge-graph query, and data pipelines.

Tier 5, work-application connectors (moderate risk; reached through PR 14 and PR 43): email, calendar, team messaging, cloud file storage, document and wiki platforms, project and issue tracking, customer relationship management, object storage, maps, and telephony.

Tier 6, multimodal and media (low to moderate risk): image understanding, image editing, speech-to-text, text-to-speech, video understanding, video generation, audio and music tools, three-dimensional generation, slide and document generation, and diagram generation.

Tier 7, protocols and orchestration (already native to PrologAI): the Model Context Protocol (PR 14), the Agent2Agent protocol (PR 43), sub-agent orchestration, task planning, workflow engines, tool registries (PR 44), sandboxes and containers, authorization handling, scheduling and triggers, and caching and observability.

Tier 8, reasoning, control, and safety (already native): the scratchpad and planning, self-critique, verifiers, guardrails, human-in-the-loop approval, logging and tracing, evaluation harnesses, state and graph orchestration, result validation, and capability evaluations (Part 38).

Tier 9, vertical and specialized (risk by domain): financial and market data, payments and checkout, booking and reservations, scientific computing and simulation, robotics and device control (through PR 10), internet-of-things and home automation, translation, legal and contract analysis, medical and clinical tools, and enterprise search.


SECTION 39.3 - WHY A FACULTY AND NOT A LIST

A faculty generalizes; a list ossifies.

By enrolling these classes as grounded tools under one registry, PrologAI gains every tool above through the single mechanism of PR 44, can add tomorrow's tools by registration rather than redesign, can learn which tools serve which objectives, and can even build new tools for itself - all under one safety regime.

This is the same discipline as the rest of the architecture: the mechanism is specified once, and the world is plugged into it.


PART 40 - THE COGNITIVE CAPABILITIES MAP

This part is an index, not a new mechanism: it cross-references the vocabulary of the agent era and of the science of mind to the exact parts and work packages that already realize each capability, so that a reader or an evaluator can move from a familiar term to its PrologAI implementation in one step.

Nothing here extends the architecture; every term below maps to something already specified, which is the point - the capabilities people name separately are, in PrologAI, facets of one coherent design.

The format is: capability - where it lives in PrologAI.

APPENDIX A - THE SPARC METHODOLOGY

SPARC is the step-by-step methodology under which this specification is written; this document is SPARC Step 1.

S - Specification: clearly writing down exactly what the software must do before writing code - clear, testable requirements, users and uses, and edge cases (unusual or unexpected situations the software must also handle correctly).

P - Pseudocode: writing the logic in plain, human-readable, step-by-step language before committing to programming syntax.

A - Architecture: the overall structure - which components will be built and how they fit together and communicate.

R - Refinement: reviewing, testing, and improving the code after it is written - catching bugs, and making the code cleaner, faster, and more reliable.

C - Completion: finalizing - all tests passing, documentation written, and the finished software deployed.

SPARC transforms software development from ad-hoc coding into a disciplined process where every phase builds on the previous one, and it is specifically designed to work with AI-assisted development.

SECTION 41.2 - THE SPARC DOCUMENTATION CHANGE RULE

Rule: any change to the code base - whether a new work package (PR), a bug fix, a utility, or a Mentova accomplishment that changes platform capability - SHALL be accompanied by corresponding changes to the relevant SPARC documents in /PrologAI/docs/. The documentation change SHALL describe what changed and why.

The SPARC volumes that may require updates:
  Volume 1 (Specification): add or update functional requirements when a new capability is specified.
  Volume 2 (Pseudocode): add or update pseudocode when the reasoning logic of a work package changes.
  Volume 3 (Architecture): add or update architectural placement when a new component is introduced.
  Volume 4 (Refinement): add or update acceptance criteria and test requirements when a new testable capability is added.
  Volume 5 (Completion): add or update delivery and deployment notes when new artifacts are produced.

Not every code change requires all five volumes to be updated. A thin launcher utility (FR-REPL-001 to FR-REPL-005) updates the Specification (new functional requirements), the Architecture (new component placement), and the Completion (new delivery artifact), but does not require Pseudocode or Refinement changes. A new reasoning work package (e.g., PR 49) would update all five.

The documentation change SHALL be committed in the same pull request as the code change, or in a dedicated documentation PR that references the code PR. Documentation-only changes increment the minor version of the affected volumes (e.g., v13 to v14). Code changes that also update documents increment the minor version of each affected volume.

Reason: the SPARC methodology defines a disciplined sequence (Specification, Pseudocode, Architecture, Refinement, Completion) that ensures every capability is specified before it is coded and documented after it is delivered. Without the standing rule, documentation drift accumulates: the specification describes a system that is older than the code, and new contributors cannot trust the documents as an accurate description of what the platform does. The standing rule closes that gap by making documentation a required deliverable for every code change.


PART 49 - CRYPTOGRAPHIC PRIVACY LAYER

This part specifies the Lattice Cryptographic Privacy Layer (PR 49), which provides hybrid encryption for sensitive data stored in the Lattice, such as passwords, session tokens, social security numbers, credit card numbers, and other information that must remain unreadable to anyone who does not hold the appropriate private key.

The privacy layer is a separate pack (pack lattice_crypto) that any pack or application may load. It does not modify the Lattice schema; encrypted values are stored in the Lattice as ordinary node_fact value atoms in a designated private scope, indistinguishable in structure from any other atom.


SECTION 49.1 - THE THREAT MODEL

Three classes of sensitive data require protection in a running PrologAI mind.

Class A: ephemeral credentials — passwords and tokens that arrive from the user or an external service and must be used within a session but never written to disk in plaintext.

Class B: persistent secrets — credentials and identification numbers that must survive across sessions (stored in the Lattice journal) but remain unreadable to anyone without the private key.

Class C: inter-mind confidential payloads — data encrypted by one mind (or by a human administrator) and transmitted to a second mind through any of the four interoperability protocols (MCP, A2A, ACP, ANP) without being readable in transit or in the recipient Lattice until the recipient decrypts it.

The privacy layer addresses all three classes through the same encryption interface; the choice of algorithm and key management policy is the caller's responsibility.


SECTION 49.2 - ALGORITHM FAMILIES

FR-CRYPTO-001: The privacy layer SHALL support three asymmetric algorithm families for key wrapping.

RSA (Rivest-Shamir-Adleman) with OAEP-SHA-1 padding (PKCS#1 v2.x): a well-understood algorithm with long field history, appropriate for environments where ECC and PQC are not yet deployed. Minimum key size is 2048 bits; 4096 bits is recommended for long-lived keys.

ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography) via ECDH on curve prime256v1 (NIST P-256): a modern asymmetric algorithm offering equivalent security to RSA-3072 at a fraction of the key size. The privacy layer uses ephemeral ECDH (a new ephemeral key pair per encryption) so that compromise of the recipient's long-term private key does not compromise past sessions.

PQC (Post-Quantum Cryptography) via ML-KEM-768 (CRYSTALS-Kyber, NIST FIPS 203): a key encapsulation mechanism designed to resist attacks from a large-scale quantum computer. Requires OpenSSL 3.2 or later with the OQS provider (liboqs) installed. If the OQS provider is not detected, the predicates raise a structured error rather than silently weakening to a classical algorithm.

FR-CRYPTO-002: All three families SHALL use hybrid encryption: the asymmetric algorithm wraps a freshly generated 256-bit AES session key; AES-256 in Galois/Counter Mode (GCM) with a 96-bit random IV encrypts the payload. AES-GCM provides both confidentiality and authenticated integrity.

FR-CRYPTO-003: The privacy layer SHALL expose a single generic encrypt/decrypt interface (pai_encrypt/5 and pai_decrypt/5) that accepts an algorithm atom (pai_algo_rsa, pai_algo_ecdh, or pai_algo_pqc) and dispatches to the appropriate internal implementation. Application code SHALL NOT need to call algorithm-specific predicates unless it requires finer control.


SECTION 49.3 - FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS

FR-CRYPTO-004: The privacy layer SHALL provide key generation predicates: pai_keygen_rsa/3 for RSA key pairs (PEM files), pai_keygen_ecdh/3 for ECDH key pairs (private scalar and public point on the curve), and pai_keygen_pqc/2 for PQC key pairs (PEM files via the OQS provider).

FR-CRYPTO-005: Encrypted bundles SHALL be self-contained compound terms that carry all fields needed for decryption: the wrapped AES key (or KEM encapsulation), the IV, and the ciphertext. Authentication tags SHALL be stored as hex-encoded atoms alongside the bundle. The bundle SHALL be storable as an ordinary Prolog term.

FR-CRYPTO-006: Decryption SHALL fail with an error on any authentication tag mismatch. The privacy layer SHALL NOT silently return incorrect data when the ciphertext or tag has been tampered with.

FR-CRYPTO-007: The privacy layer SHALL provide a pai_crypto_available/1 predicate that succeeds if and only if the specified algorithm family is fully operational in the current environment. This allows application code to probe availability before committing to an algorithm.

FR-CRYPTO-008: The privacy layer SHALL integrate with the Lattice scope architecture: private Lattice values SHALL be encrypted and stored in a designated private scope so that the full symbolic reasoning layer can see the encrypted atom but cannot see the plaintext without holding the decryption key.

FR-CRYPTO-009: All key material held in temporary files during key generation SHALL be cleaned up by the caller or by the platform; the privacy layer SHALL document ownership clearly and provide delete-on-use patterns in the tutorial.


SECTION 49.4 - IMPLEMENTATION AND PLACEMENT

Implementation: pack lattice_crypto in packs/lattice_crypto/prolog/lattice_crypto.pl (PR 49). Tests in tests/pr49/test_pr49.pl (eleven acceptance criteria). Architecture placement: PART ARCH-18.

The pack uses library(crypto) (SWI-Prolog built-in) for AES-256-GCM, HKDF-SHA-256, and RSA-OAEP operations; library(process) for openssl CLI invocations required by RSA key generation and PQC operations; and the crypto_curve_scalar_mult/4 predicate for ECDH shared-secret computation on named elliptic curves.

The OQS provider requirement for PQC is a deployment-time dependency, not a compile-time dependency. Systems without the OQS provider can use RSA and ECDH without any degradation; PQC predicates raise a structured lattice_crypto_error(pqc_provider_unavailable, Message) error that instructs the administrator to install the required provider.


PART 42 - IMAGINATIVE MEMORY AND SITUATIONAL AWARENESS (WORK PACKAGES PR 50 AND PR 51)

This part was added to close two gaps found when the platform was audited against the founding vision documents (Building Sentient Beings, sections 3.2.2.2 and 3.2.4). Both capabilities are specified here, realized in the Pseudocode, placed in the Architecture, hardened in the Refinement, and shipped in the Completion. All element names are original to PrologAI; no source-origin vocabulary is introduced.

42.1 Imaginative Memory (pack imagination)

The vision describes imaginative memory as a set of compartmentalized mental canvases on which specialized mechanisms render two- and three-dimensional animations, where each canvas binds a collection of elements (a perspective camera, multimedia, animation resources, and meshes) and objects from perception may be reused to construct scenes or entirely new objects may be invented. PrologAI realizes this faculty with original names:

A mindscape is a compartmentalized imaginative canvas. Every mindscape is held in a non-observed reality (imagined, hypothetical, or recalled); the platform shall reject any request to place a mindscape in the observed reality, so imagined content can never be mistaken for perceived fact. This is the imaginative sandbox guard.

A tableau is an arrangement of bound elements placed on a mindscape. An element is one of three kinds: a vantage (a perspective camera angle), a figure (a mesh or object, which may be grounded to a percept), or a motif (a multimedia or animation resource). The platform shall reject an element of any other kind.

A figure carries an optional starting position pos(X,Y) and an optional per-frame velocity vel(DX,DY). A figure may be invented outright, or grounded to an object taken from perception, reusing perceived structure inside imagination.

A reverie is the rendered animation produced from a mindscape's tableaux: a finite, ordered list of frames. Rendering advances each figure kinematically (position at frame F equals start plus velocity times F) so that a reverie is a deterministic, inspectable forward animation suitable for mental simulation. Each frame records the vantage and the position of every figure.

The work package shall provide: pai_mindscape_new/2, pai_mindscape_reality/2, pai_tableau_add/3, pai_tableau_ground/3, pai_reverie_render/3, pai_reverie_frames/2, pai_mindscape_clear/1, and the one-call convenience pai_imagine_fresh/4.

42.2 Situational Awareness (pack awareness)

The vision holds that situational awareness is engineered, not assumed, and that it develops through a ladder of viewpoints: from a general awareness, to a self-interested view, to the views of other minds (theory of mind), to an owned view of preferences and responsibility, to a disowned view of aversions and blind spots. PrologAI names each viewpoint a regard, and gives every level an original name:

ambient_regard is the initial, undifferentiated general awareness; it is the default standpoint of a freshly booted mind. selfward_regard is the view from self-awareness and self-interest. otherward_regard(Agent) is a view attributed to another mind, opened on demand, one per modelled agent. avowed_regard is the owned view (consciousness, preferences, responsibility). disavowed_regard is the disowned view (ignorance, aversions, the freedom not yet integrated). The five kinds carry strictly increasing developmental level indices one through five.

A proposition may be held under a regard, so the same content may be believed from one standpoint and denied from another. The mind has exactly one active regard at a time, beginning at ambient_regard. Theory-of-mind perspective taking attributes a proposition to an other's regard (pai_tom_attribute/2) and detects divergence (pai_tom_divergence/2): a proposition the self holds whose contradictory the other holds, where a proposition P and the term not(P) are contradictories. This is the computational core of false-belief reasoning. Reconciliation (pai_regard_reconcile/1) integrates the avowed and disavowed regards into one coherent self-account, surfacing disowned material rather than leaving it hidden.

The work package shall provide: pai_regard_kinds/1, pai_regard_open/1, pai_regard_hold/2, pai_regard_held/2, pai_regard_shift/1, pai_regard_active/1, pai_regard_level/2, pai_tom_attribute/2, pai_tom_divergence/2, and pai_regard_reconcile/1. An otherward regard whose agent term is not ground shall be rejected.

42.3 Traceability

PR 50 traces to Building Sentient Beings 3.2.2.2 (imaginative memory, taxis, canvases, dreams) and supplies the imaginative area of the Totality that the daydreaming pack (PR 25) previously steered without a place to render. PR 51 traces to Building Sentient Beings 3.2.4 and Figures 16 and 17 (situational awareness evolving through levels of regard, theory of mind, and the integration of owned and disowned views) and complements the assessment pack (PR 12), which measures such milestones, by giving the mind the viewpoint structure to exhibit them.

PART 43 - EPHEMERAL CODE SYNTHESIS AND EXECUTION (WORK PACKAGE PR 53)

This part specifies the ephemera work package, which gives PrologAI the ability to compose and execute short-lived programs - called ephemera - in order to solve sub-problems that are most naturally expressed as runnable code rather than as symbolic Prolog facts. The name ephemera reflects their nature: they are created, used, and discarded, leaving no permanent trace except in an optional trace log.

The motivation for this faculty comes from observing that capable problem-solving systems - both human programmers and modern AI coding agents - routinely write small, focused, throwaway programs to probe unknowns, test hypotheses, transform data, or automate a mechanical step. PrologAI should have this same faculty so that it can apply the same strategy when it encounters problems that are better served by a small program than by symbolic reasoning alone.

This work package takes a silicon-and-code substrate approach: the patterns it implements (bounded code execution, timeout enforcement, subprocess isolation, trace logging) come directly from proven software engineering practice and agentic AI engineering, not from neuroscience analogies.


SECTION 43.1 - CORE CONCEPTS

An ephemeron is any short-lived program written by PrologAI, executed once, and then discarded. Ephemera may be written in any language the host system supports. Three language targets are specified for the initial release: Prolog (the native substrate), Python 3 (the dominant language for scientific computing and data work), and Bash (for system automation and file manipulation). Additional language targets may be added through the ep_language_argv/3 and ep_language_ext/2 extension points.

Ephemera come in two kinds:

Goal ephemera evaluate a Prolog goal term directly within the running interpreter. The goal may bind variables, call system predicates, or invoke any loaded pack. Goal ephemera are executed with a wall-clock timeout enforced by call_with_time_limit/2.

Script ephemera write source code to a temporary file, invoke a subprocess to execute the file, capture standard output and standard error, and report the exit code. The temporary file is deleted after the subprocess exits regardless of outcome.

A trace is an optional record kept by the caller when it wants to review a sequence of ephemeron executions later. The trace stores: a trace identifier, a step number, the code that was executed, and the result or output.

An iteration cycle couples a synthesizer goal (which produces code), an executor goal (which runs it), and a checker goal (which decides whether the result is acceptable). The iteration cycle repeats until the checker is satisfied or a step budget is exhausted.


SECTION 43.2 - FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS

FR-EP-001: The ephemera pack SHALL provide ep_eval/3 with signature (+GoalTerm, +TimeoutSecs, -EvalResult). The predicate SHALL execute GoalTerm as a Prolog goal, enforce the given wall-clock timeout, and unify EvalResult with one of: success (goal succeeded), fail (goal deterministically failed), timeout (timeout was exceeded before the goal completed), or error(Exception) (the goal threw Exception).

FR-EP-002: ep_eval/3 SHALL bind variables in GoalTerm on success, so that the caller can extract computed values directly from the goal term after the call returns.

FR-EP-003: The ephemera pack SHALL provide ep_scratch/4 with signature (+CodeAtom, +VarBindings, +TimeoutSecs, -EvalResult). The predicate SHALL parse CodeAtom as a Prolog term using atom_to_term/3, apply the VarBindings list (pairs of the form Name=Var), execute the resulting goal with the given timeout, and return EvalResult in the same form as ep_eval/3, carrying updated bindings in the success case as success(UpdatedBindings).

FR-EP-004: The ephemera pack SHALL provide ep_shell/3 with signature (+Argv, +TimeoutSecs, -ShellResult). Argv is a list of atoms where the first element is the executable and subsequent elements are its arguments. The predicate SHALL launch the executable as a subprocess, capture its standard output and standard error into atoms, wait for exit, and unify ShellResult with shell_result(ExitCode, Stdout, Stderr).

FR-EP-005: The ephemera pack SHALL provide ep_ephemeral/4 with signature (+Language, +Code, +TimeoutSecs, -ShellResult). Language is one of the atoms prolog, python, or bash. The predicate SHALL write Code to a temporary file with the correct extension for the language, invoke the appropriate interpreter as a subprocess, capture output, delete the temporary file, and return the result in the same shell_result/3 form as ep_shell/3.

FR-EP-006: The ephemera pack SHALL provide ep_iterate/5 with signature (:SynthGoal, :ExecGoal, :CheckGoal, +MaxIter, -IterResult). SynthGoal is called with no arguments to produce a Code atom. ExecGoal is called with Code and a TimeoutSecs to produce a Result. CheckGoal is called with Result and either succeeds (acceptable) or fails (needs another iteration). IterResult is one of: ok(Code, Result) if the checker accepted within MaxIter steps, or exhausted(LastCode, LastResult) if the budget ran out.

FR-EP-007: The ephemera pack SHALL provide ep_trace_record/4 with signature (+TraceId, +Iter, +Code, +Output) to store one entry in the trace log, and ep_trace_get/2 with signature (+TraceId, -Entries) to retrieve all entries for a trace ID as a list.

FR-EP-008: The ephemera pack SHALL provide ep_next_trace_id/1 with signature (-TraceId) to allocate a fresh, unique trace identifier.

FR-EP-009: All subprocess executions (ep_shell/3, ep_ephemeral/4) SHALL be bounded by TimeoutSecs. If the subprocess does not complete within the budget, the predicate SHALL terminate the subprocess and return a shell_result with exit code -1 and a timeout indicator in Stderr.

FR-EP-010: The temporary files created by ep_ephemeral/4 SHALL be deleted by the predicate before it returns, both on success and on failure, so that failed or timed-out ephemera do not accumulate on the filesystem.

FR-EP-011: The pack SHALL provide ep_skill_save/4(+Name, +Language, +Code, +Description) to save a named, reusable skill in an in-memory skill database. If a skill with Name already exists, the old entry SHALL be replaced atomically (retractall then assertz).

FR-EP-012: The pack SHALL provide ep_skill_lookup/3(+Name, -Language, -Code) to retrieve a saved skill by name. The predicate SHALL fail silently if no skill with that name has been saved.

FR-EP-013: The pack SHALL provide ep_skill_run/3(+Name, +TimeoutSecs, -ShellResult) to execute a saved skill by name. The predicate SHALL look up the skill and pass its code to ep_ephemeral/4 with the given timeout.

FR-EP-014: The pack SHALL provide ep_skill_list/1(-Skills) to return all saved skills as a list of skill(Name, Language, Description) terms. The predicate SHALL return an empty list when no skills have been saved.

FR-EP-015: The pack SHALL provide ep_skill_forget/1(+Name) to remove a saved skill by name. The predicate SHALL succeed silently if no skill with that name exists.

Implementation: pack ephemera in packs/ephemera/prolog/ephemera.pl (PR 53). Tests in packs/ephemera/test/test_ephemera.pl (fifteen acceptance criteria AC-EP-001 through AC-EP-015). Architecture placement: PART ARCH-19.

PART 44 - THE AGENCY PACK: AGENTIC EXECUTION LOOP (WORK PACKAGE PR 54)

This part specifies the agency work package, which gives PrologAI a formal, observable, and bounded execution loop for pursuing goals through sequences of reasoning and action steps. The name agency reflects the capability it provides: the ability to act with purpose, within limits, under observation.

The agency pack implements the core Observe-Reason-Act-Observe (ORAO) pattern that is the foundation of all agentic AI systems. Rather than simulating neuroscience, it takes a silicon-and-code substrate approach: the loop structure, budget enforcement, goal stack management, loop detection, and escalation to human oversight are all taken directly from agentic AI engineering design patterns.

The agency pack depends on the ephemera pack (PR 53) for action execution. It uses ep_eval/3 to evaluate Prolog goal actions and ep_shell/3 to run shell command actions.


SECTION 44.1 - CORE CONCEPTS

A loop is an instance of agentic execution. Every loop has: a goal (the top-level objective), a budget (maximum number of steps allowed), a status (running, done(Result), escalated(Reason), or budget_exhausted), and a goal stack (an ordered list of subgoals derived from the top-level goal).

A step is one cycle of the ORAO pattern: the loop examines its current goal and state (observe), calls a caller-supplied reasoning goal to produce a thought and an action (reason), executes the action (act), records the thought, action, and observation as a step fact (observe again, recording the new state).

A thought is any Prolog term that represents the reasoning behind an action choice. It is recorded in the trace and is fully inspectable.

An action is one of seven named types: action_eval(GoalTerm) evaluates a Prolog goal via the ephemera pack; action_shell(Argv, Timeout) runs a shell command; action_push_goal(Subgoal) pushes one subgoal onto the goal stack; action_push_goals(Subgoals) pushes multiple subgoals; action_mark_done(Result) declares the loop done; action_escalate(Reason) requests human oversight; action_pop_goal removes the current top subgoal when it is complete.

An observation is a Prolog term produced by the action dispatch layer (ag_act/3) that records what happened when the action was executed.

Loop detection examines the sequence of thought-action pairs recorded so far. If the last two pairs are identical, the loop has detected that it is stuck repeating itself.

Escalation is a safe shutdown path: when the reasoning goal determines that the loop cannot proceed without human input, it chooses action_escalate(Reason), which records the escalation and marks the loop status as escalated(Reason). The loop then terminates and the caller can inspect the escalation record.


SECTION 44.2 - FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS

FR-AG-001: The agency pack SHALL provide ag_loop_create/3 with signature (+Goal, +Budget, -LoopId). The predicate SHALL allocate a unique loop identifier, store the goal and budget, initialize the goal stack with the top-level goal, initialize the step counter to zero, and set the initial status to running.

FR-AG-002: The agency pack SHALL provide ag_loop_run/3 with signature (+LoopId, :ReasonGoal, -Outcome). The predicate SHALL execute steps in sequence by calling ag_loop_step/3 until the loop reaches a terminal state (done, escalated, or budget_exhausted), then return the final status as Outcome.

FR-AG-003: The agency pack SHALL provide ag_loop_step/3 with signature (+LoopId, :ReasonGoal, -StepResult). PART 45 - THE REFINERY PACK: EVALUATOR-OPTIMIZER AND METACOGNITIVE QUALITY LAYER (WORK PACKAGE PR 55)

This part specifies the refinery work package, which gives PrologAI the ability to improve its own outputs through structured cycles of evaluation and refinement. The name refinery reflects its function: it takes raw outputs and refines them toward a quality bar, just as an oil refinery takes crude oil and refines it into useful products.

The refinery implements two of the most productive patterns in agentic AI engineering: reflection (the ability to critique one's own output) and the evaluator-optimizer loop (the ability to repeatedly generate, score, improve, and re-evaluate until a quality bar is met or the iteration budget is exhausted). It also implements the Tree of Thoughts pattern (exploring multiple reasoning paths and ranking them by score) and a persistent lesson database (recording what went wrong in past problem-solving attempts).

This work package takes a silicon-and-code substrate approach: the critique-score-improve-repeat pattern and the multi-path exploration pattern come from proven agentic AI design patterns (Agentic AI Patterns Parts 5.5 and 5.4), not from neuroscience analogies.


SECTION 45.1 - CORE CONCEPTS

A criterion is a named test applied to an output. A criterion is represented as criterion(Name, TestGoal) where TestGoal is a Prolog goal called as call(TestGoal, Output). A criterion passes if its TestGoal succeeds and fails if it throws or fails.

A criteria list is an ordered list of criterion/2 terms.

A critique is the result of applying a criteria list to an output. It is a list of found_issue(Name, fail) terms, one for each criterion that failed. An empty critique means the output passed all criteria.

A score is a float in the range [0.0, 1.0] computed as the fraction of criteria that pass. A score of 1.0 means all criteria pass; a score of 0.0 means no criteria pass. An empty criteria list scores 1.0 (vacuously perfect).

A quality bar is a threshold score. The evaluator-optimizer loop stops when the score meets or exceeds the quality bar.

An improver goal is called as call(ImproverGoal, CurrentOutput, Critique, ImprovedOutput) to produce a refined output from the current output and its critique.

A generator goal is called as call(GeneratorGoal, Candidate) to produce a candidate output.

An evaluator goal is called as call(EvaluatorGoal, Candidate, Score) to score a candidate.

A path is one exploration of a problem using a given strategy. An explorer goal is called as call(ExplorerGoal, Problem, PathId, Output, Score). The path exploration facility calls the explorer for each path ID from 1 to NumPaths, collects all (PathId, Score, Output) triples, and returns them sorted by score in descending order.

A lesson is a stored record of what went wrong in a past problem-solving attempt and what should be done differently. Lessons are stored by problem type, retrieved by problem type before attempting the same type of problem again, and discarded when no longer relevant.


SECTION 45.2 - FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS

FR-RN-001: The refinery pack SHALL provide rn_critique/4 with signature (+Output, +Criteria, +MaxSteps, -Critique). The predicate SHALL run each criterion's TestGoal against Output with a MaxSteps timeout, collect the names of criteria that fail or throw, and unify Critique with the list of found_issue(Name, fail) terms.

FR-RN-002: The refinery pack SHALL provide rn_score/3 with signature (+Output, +Criteria, -Score). Score is the fraction of criteria whose TestGoal succeeds when called with Output. An empty criteria list scores 1.0. Score is a float in [0.0, 1.0].

FR-RN-003: The refinery pack SHALL provide rn_improve/5 with signature (+Output, +Criteria, :ImproverGoal, +MaxIter, -Improved). The predicate SHALL apply one improvement round per iteration: critique the current output; if the critique is empty, return the current output; otherwise call ImproverGoal with the current output and critique to produce a new candidate; repeat up to MaxIter times; if MaxIter is exhausted, return the best candidate reached.

FR-RN-004: The refinery pack SHALL provide rn_optimize/5 with signature (:GeneratorGoal, :EvaluatorGoal, +QualityBar, +MaxIter, -Best). The predicate SHALL: allocate a unique run ID; call GeneratorGoal to produce a Candidate; call EvaluatorGoal to score it; log the attempt; update the running best if the new score is higher; stop and return Candidate if its score meets the quality bar; otherwise continue until MaxIter iterations are exhausted and return the best candidate found.

FR-RN-005: The refinery pack SHALL provide rn_explore_paths/4 with signature (+Problem, :ExplorerGoal, +NumPaths, -Ranked). The predicate SHALL call ExplorerGoal for each path ID from 1 to NumPaths, collect the (PathId, Score, Output) triples for all paths where the explorer succeeds, sort them by score in descending order, and return the sorted list as ranked_path(PathId, Score, Output) terms.

FR-RN-006: The refinery pack SHALL provide rn_learn/3 with signature (+ProblemType, +FailurePattern, +Lesson). The predicate SHALL assert a rn_lesson_db/3 fact storing the lesson.

FR-RN-007: The refinery pack SHALL provide rn_recall/2 with signature (+ProblemType, -Lessons) that returns a list of all lesson(FailurePattern, Lesson) terms stored for the given problem type.

FR-RN-008: The refinery pack SHALL provide rn_forget/1 with signature (+ProblemType) that retracts all rn_lesson_db/3 facts for the given problem type.


SECTION 45.3 - IMPLEMENTATION AND PLACEMENT

Implementation: pack refinery in packs/refinery/prolog/refinery.pl (PR 55). Tests in packs/refinery/test/test_refinery.pl (ten acceptance criteria AC-RN-001 through AC-RN-010). Architecture placement: PART ARCH-21.

The pack imports library(lists), library(aggregate), and library(apply) from SWI-Prolog. It does not depend on any PrologAI pack; its criterion TestGoals are called via the standard call/2 mechanism and may invoke any predicate the caller makes available.

The recommended practice for writing portable criterion TestGoals is to use YALL (Yet Another Lambda Library) lambda notation, for example [O]>>(O \= []), so that the closure carries its module context and works correctly regardless of which module calls rn_critique or rn_score.

PART 46 - THE GRID PACK: ARC-AGI GRID PERCEPTION AND MANIPULATION (WORK PACKAGE PR 56)

This part specifies the grid work package, which gives PrologAI native perception and manipulation of the rectangular color grids used in the ARC-AGI (Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus for Artificial General Intelligence) benchmark. The grid pack is the primary perceptual substrate for ARC-AGI-2: it represents inputs and outputs, extracts objects, applies spatial transformations, and compares grid states. Every higher-level ARC-solving pack (analogy, composition, abstraction) depends on this layer.

A grid in PrologAI is a list of rows, each row a list of integer color codes. Color 0 is black; colors 1 through 9 follow the ARC palette. This representation is isomorphic to the JSON arrays in the ARC task files after parsing. All row and column indices are zero-based throughout the pack.


SECTION 46.1 - CORE CONCEPTS

A grid is a list of rows, each row a list of integers in the range 0 to 9. A 3-by-3 grid is written [[0,1,2],[3,4,5],[6,7,8]]. The number of rows and columns is the size of the grid. An empty grid is the empty list []; a grid with no columns is a list of empty lists.

A cell is identified by a zero-based row index and a zero-based column index. The color at a cell is the integer stored at that position.

A color set is a sorted list of distinct color integers that appear in a grid.

An object is a maximal set of 4-connected cells that all share the same color. Four-connectivity means two cells are adjacent only if they share an edge (not a corner). Each object is represented as a list of r(Row, Col) cell terms.

A connected component is one object. The set of all objects for a given color is the set of maximal 4-connected regions of that color.

A bounding box is the smallest axis-aligned rectangle that contains a set of cells. It is expressed as two r(Row, Col) terms: the top-left corner (minimum row, minimum column) and the bottom-right corner (maximum row, maximum column).

A transformation is a function from grids to grids. The pack implements seven spatial transformations: rotate 90 degrees clockwise, rotate 180 degrees, rotate 270 degrees clockwise, reflect across the horizontal axis (flip upside-down), reflect across the vertical axis (flip left-right), reflect across the main diagonal (transpose), and reflect across the anti-diagonal.

A translation shifts every cell by a row delta and a column delta. Cells shifted outside the grid boundary are replaced by a background color.

A crop extracts a rectangular sub-grid bounded by inclusive zero-based row and column indices.

An overlay composites a patch grid onto a base grid at a given row and column offset. Cells in the patch with a non-zero color replace the corresponding base cell; cells in the patch with color 0 are transparent (the base cell shows through).

A diff is a list of r(Row, Col, OldColor, NewColor) terms describing every cell position where two grids differ.

A symmetry axis is one of four orientations: h (horizontal), v (vertical), d1 (main diagonal), d2 (anti-diagonal). A grid has a symmetry axis if reflecting it across that axis produces a grid identical to the original.

A flood fill replaces all cells in the 4-connected region containing a seed cell with a new color.


SECTION 46.2 - FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS

FR-GD-001: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_size/3 with signature (+Grid, -Rows, -Cols). The predicate SHALL unify Rows with the length of Grid (number of rows) and Cols with the length of the first row (number of columns). An empty grid SHALL yield Cols = 0.

FR-GD-002: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_cell/4 with signature (+Grid, +R, +C, -Color). The predicate SHALL unify Color with the integer at zero-based position (R, C).

FR-GD-003: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_row/3 with signature (+Grid, +R, -Row) and gd_col/3 with signature (+Grid, +C, -Col). gd_row/3 SHALL extract the Rth row as a list; gd_col/3 SHALL extract the Cth column as a list.

FR-GD-004: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_colors/2 with signature (+Grid, -ColorSet). ColorSet SHALL be a sorted list of distinct integers that appear in Grid.

FR-GD-005: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_color_count/3 with signature (+Grid, +Color, -Count). Count SHALL be the number of cells in Grid whose color equals Color.

FR-GD-006: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_color_map/3 with signature (+Grid, +Map, -Grid2). Map is a list of From-To integer pairs. Every cell whose color equals From in Map SHALL be replaced by To in Grid2. Colors not in Map SHALL pass through unchanged.

FR-GD-007: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_objects/3 with signature (+Grid, +Color, -Objects). Objects SHALL be a list of 4-connected cell sets (each a list of r(R,C) terms) covering all maximal connected regions of Color in Grid.

FR-GD-008: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_connected/3 with signature (+Grid, +Color, -Components) implementing the same semantics as gd_objects/3. These two predicates are aliases.

FR-GD-009: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_bounding_box/3 with signature (+Cells, -TopLeft, -BottomRight). Cells is a list of r(R,C) terms. TopLeft SHALL be r(MinR, MinC) and BottomRight SHALL be r(MaxR, MaxC).

FR-GD-010: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_rotate90/2 with signature (+Grid, -Grid2) implementing a 90-degree clockwise rotation. New cell (NR, NC) gets the color of old cell (NewCols-1-NC, NR) where NewCols is the number of columns in Grid2, which equals the number of rows in Grid.

FR-GD-011: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_rotate180/2 as the composition of two 90-degree clockwise rotations.

FR-GD-012: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_rotate270/2 as the composition of three 90-degree clockwise rotations.

FR-GD-013: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_reflect_h/2 implementing a flip across the horizontal axis by reversing the row order.

FR-GD-014: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_reflect_v/2 implementing a flip across the vertical axis by reversing each row.

FR-GD-015: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_reflect_d1/2 implementing a transpose by making each column of Grid a row of Grid2.

FR-GD-016: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_reflect_d2/2 implementing an anti-diagonal flip as gd_rotate90 followed by gd_reflect_v.

FR-GD-017: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_translate/5 with signature (+Grid, +DR, +DC, +Background, -Grid2). Grid2 has the same dimensions as Grid. Cell (R, C) of Grid2 SHALL get the color of cell (R-DR, C-DC) of Grid when that position is within bounds, and Background otherwise.

FR-GD-018: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_crop/6 with signature (+Grid, +R0, +C0, +R1, +C1, -Grid2). Grid2 SHALL contain the sub-grid of Grid from row R0 to row R1 (inclusive) and column C0 to column C1 (inclusive), all zero-based.

FR-GD-019: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_overlay/5 with signature (+Base, +Patch, +DR, +DC, -Grid2). Grid2 has the same dimensions as Base. For each cell (R, C) of Grid2: if the patch position (R-DR, C-DC) is within Patch bounds and the patch color at that position is non-zero, use the patch color; otherwise use the base color.

FR-GD-020: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_diff/3 with signature (+Grid1, +Grid2, -Diffs). Grid1 and Grid2 must have identical dimensions. Diffs SHALL be the list of r(R, C, OldColor, NewColor) terms for every cell where Grid1 and Grid2 differ.

FR-GD-021: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_equal/2 with signature (+Grid1, +Grid2) that succeeds if and only if Grid1 and Grid2 are structurally identical (same dimensions, same colors at every cell).

FR-GD-022: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_symmetry/2 with signature (+Grid, -Axes). Axes SHALL be the list of symmetry axis atoms (subset of [h, v, d1, d2]) for which the grid is unchanged by the corresponding reflection.

FR-GD-023: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_fill/5 with signature (+Grid, +R, +C, +Color, -Grid2) implementing a 4-connected flood fill. All cells in the maximal 4-connected region containing (R, C) whose color matches the color at (R, C) in Grid SHALL be replaced by Color in Grid2.

FR-GD-024: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_make/4 with signature (+Rows, +Cols, +Color, -Grid). Grid SHALL be a Rows-by-Cols grid in which every cell has the given Color.

FR-GD-025: The grid pack SHALL provide gd_set_cell/5 with signature (+Grid, +R, +C, +Color, -Grid2). Grid2 SHALL equal Grid except that the cell at (R, C) has Color. All other cells SHALL be unchanged.


SECTION 46.3 - IMPLEMENTATION AND PLACEMENT

Implementation: pack grid in packs/grid/prolog/grid.pl (PR 56). Tests in packs/grid/test/test_grid.pl with 31 acceptance criteria (AC-GD-001 through AC-GD-031). Architecture placement: PART ARCH-22.

The pack imports library(lists) and library(apply) from SWI-Prolog. It has no dependency on any PrologAI pack, making it a pure perceptual foundation that all ARC-solving packs above it may use.

The 4-connected flood fill in gd_connected/3 uses breadth-first search (BFS) to enumerate connected components. Each BFS expands over cells in the Available set, which is the set of color-matching cells not yet assigned to a component. This guarantees that each cell is assigned to exactly one component.

The gd_set_cell/5 predicate uses the SWI-Prolog library(lists) nth0/4 select form to functionally update one cell without destructively modifying the grid. This preserves the pure logic semantics of the pack.

PART 47 - THE ANALOGY PACK: ARC-AGI STRUCTURAL ANALOGY AND TRANSFORMATION INFERENCE (WORK PACKAGE PR 57)

This part specifies the analogy work package, which gives PrologAI the ability to reason by analogy from training examples to solve ARC-AGI puzzles. Given a set of (input, output) grid pairs that share a common transformation rule, the analogy pack infers that rule and applies it to a new input grid.

The analogy pack operates one layer above the grid pack (PR 56). Where the grid pack provides primitive grid operations, the analogy pack provides the inferential layer: it observes the relationship between inputs and outputs and reasons about what transformation connects them.

The central concept is that many ARC-AGI rules can be decomposed into two independent components: a spatial isometry (a rigid motion of the D4 dihedral group) and a color substitution (a permutation of color integers). The analogy pack infers both components from examples and applies them compositionally.


SECTION 47.1 - CORE CONCEPTS

A shape is a normalized set of r(Row, Col) cell terms with the top-left corner translated to (0,0) and the list sorted. Normalization provides a canonical form so that two cell sets can be compared for shape equality regardless of position.

The D4 dihedral group contains the 8 rigid motions of a square: the identity, rotations by 90/180/270 degrees clockwise, and reflections across the horizontal axis, vertical axis, main diagonal, and anti-diagonal. These are named: identity, rot90, rot180, rot270, ref_h, ref_v, ref_d1, ref_d2. These same 8 isometries are applied to whole grids in ay_grid_isometry/3 and ay_apply_isometry/3, and to individual cell sets in ay_shape_isometry/3.

A color map is a list of integer pairs From-To. A cell with color From in the input becomes color To in the output. Cells whose color does not appear in the map pass through unchanged. The color map is inferred by comparing corresponding cells of the transformed input grid and the expected output grid.

An analogy pair is a term in(InputGrid)-out(OutputGrid). A list of analogy pairs constitutes the training evidence from which the rule is inferred.

A transformation rule is a pair (Isometry, ColorMap) where Isometry is one of the 8 D4 atoms and ColorMap is a list of From-To pairs. The rule is consistent with a set of training pairs if applying the isometry followed by the color map to each input grid produces the corresponding output grid exactly.


SECTION 47.2 - FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS

FR-AY-001: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_normalize_shape/2 with signature (+Cells, -NormCells). NormCells SHALL be the result of translating Cells so that its minimum row index and minimum column index are both 0, then sorting the list.

FR-AY-002: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_shape_equal/2 with signature (+Cells1, +Cells2) that succeeds if and only if both cell sets have the same normalized shape.

FR-AY-003: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_shape_size/2 with signature (+Cells, -N) returning the number of cells.

FR-AY-004: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_shape_bbox/3 with signature (+Cells, -TopLeft, -BottomRight) returning the bounding box as two r(Row, Col) terms.

FR-AY-005: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_shape_isometry/3 with signature (+Cells, +Isometry, -Cells2) implementing all 8 D4 isometries on cell sets. The result is normalized.

FR-AY-006: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_isometry_candidates/3 with signature (+Shape1, +Shape2, -Isometries) returning the list of D4 isometry names that map Shape1 to Shape2.

FR-AY-007: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_infer_color_map/3 with signature (+InColors, +OutColors, -Map). InColors and OutColors are parallel lists of integers. Map is the list of From-To pairs where From != To.

FR-AY-008: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_match_objects/3 with signature (+InObjects, +OutObjects, -Pairs). Pairs is the list of in_obj(Cells)-out_obj(Cells) correspondences where the two cell sets have the same normalized shape.

FR-AY-009: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_object_offset/3 with signature (+Cells1, +Cells2, -offset(DR, DC)) returning the translation vector from the top-left of Cells1 to the top-left of Cells2.

FR-AY-010: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_grid_isometry/3 with signature (+Grid1, +Grid2, -Isometries) returning the list of D4 isometry names that map Grid1 to Grid2 exactly.

FR-AY-011: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_grid_color_map/3 with signature (+Grid1, +Grid2, -Map) inferring the color map from two same-size grids by comparing corresponding cells.

FR-AY-012: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_examples_isometry/3 with signature (+Pairs, -Iso, -ColorMap). The predicate SHALL search the 8 D4 isometries in order; for each, it applies the isometry to the first training input, infers the color map from the transformed result to the expected first output, then verifies that applying Iso and ColorMap to every training input produces the expected output. It fails if no isometry is consistent with all training pairs.

FR-AY-013: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_apply_isometry/3 with signature (+Grid, +Iso, -Grid2) applying a named D4 isometry to a grid.

FR-AY-014: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_apply_color_map/3 with signature (+Grid, +Map, -Grid2) applying a color substitution map to a grid.

FR-AY-015: The analogy pack SHALL provide ay_solve_from_examples/3 with signature (+TrainPairs, +TestGrid, -OutputGrid). The predicate SHALL call ay_examples_isometry/3 to infer the rule, then apply the isometry and color map to TestGrid to produce OutputGrid.


SECTION 47.3 - IMPLEMENTATION AND PLACEMENT

Implementation: pack analogy in packs/analogy/prolog/analogy.pl (PR 57). Tests in packs/analogy/test/test_analogy.pl with 21 acceptance criteria. Architecture placement: PART ARCH-23.

The pack imports library(lists), library(apply), and pack grid. The grid pack (PR 56, Layer 35) provides all primitive grid operations. The D4 group coverage principle: all 8 rigid motions are tried in a fixed order. Many ARC-AGI tasks are explained by a single D4 isometry. Tasks requiring non-rigid transformations (scaling, non-uniform color maps) are outside the scope of this pack and require higher-level packs.

PART 48 - SCENE PACK SPECIFICATION

Section 48.1 - Purpose

The scene pack (PR 58) provides object-centric reasoning over rectangular color grids. It builds on the grid pack (PR 56, Layer 35) to extract all foreground objects from a grid, describe their properties, and compute spatial relationships between them. The scene pack is the object-level perception layer required for ARC-AGI-2 tasks that involve reasoning about individual objects (counting, comparing, positioning, tracking).

A scene is a structured description of a grid: the grid dimensions, the background color (most frequent color), and the list of all foreground objects. Each object is an obj(Color, Cells) term where Color is an integer 0-9 and Cells is a sorted list of r(Row, Col) terms.

Section 48.2 - Functional Requirements

FR-SC-001: The pack SHALL provide sc_bg_color/2(+Grid, -BgColor) that identifies the most frequently occurring color in the grid as the background.

FR-SC-002: The pack SHALL provide sc_objects/2(+Grid, -Objects) that extracts all foreground objects (objects whose color is not the background) as a list of obj(Color, Cells) terms, where each obj represents one maximal 4-connected component.

FR-SC-003: The pack SHALL provide sc_grid_to_scene/2(+Grid, -Scene) that builds a scene(Rows, Cols, BgColor, Objects) term describing the complete scene.

FR-SC-004: The pack SHALL provide sc_obj_color/2(+Obj, -Color) that extracts the color field from an obj term.

FR-SC-005: The pack SHALL provide sc_obj_cells/2(+Obj, -Cells) that extracts the cell list from an obj term.

FR-SC-006: The pack SHALL provide sc_obj_size/2(+Obj, -Size) that returns the number of cells in an object.

FR-SC-007: The pack SHALL provide sc_obj_bbox/3(+Obj, -TL, -BR) that returns the axis-aligned bounding box of an object as top-left r(Rmin, Cmin) and bottom-right r(Rmax, Cmax) terms.

FR-SC-008: The pack SHALL provide sc_obj_shape/2(+Obj, -Shape) that returns the normalized cell set of an object: translated so the top-left is at r(0,0), then sorted. Two objects have the same shape if and only if their sc_obj_shape results are identical.

FR-SC-009: The pack SHALL provide sc_obj_centroid/3(+Obj, -R, -C) that returns the center of mass of the object as row R and column C, each rounded to the nearest integer.

FR-SC-010: The pack SHALL provide sc_normalize_cells/2(+Cells, -Normalized) that translates a raw cell set to top-left origin and sorts it, for use in shape comparison.

FR-SC-011: The pack SHALL provide sc_objects_of_color/3(+Objects, +Color, -Filtered) that returns the subset of objects having the given color.

FR-SC-012: The pack SHALL provide sc_sort_by_size/3(+Objects, +Order, -Sorted) that sorts objects by cell count. Order is the atom asc (ascending) or desc (descending).

FR-SC-013: The pack SHALL provide sc_largest/2(+Objects, -Largest) that returns the object with the greatest cell count.

FR-SC-014: The pack SHALL provide sc_smallest/2(+Objects, -Smallest) that returns the object with the fewest cells.

FR-SC-015: The pack SHALL provide sc_count/2(+Objects, -Count) that returns the total number of objects in the list.

FR-SC-016: The pack SHALL provide sc_count_of_color/3(+Objects, +Color, -Count) that counts how many objects have the given color.

FR-SC-017: The pack SHALL provide sc_above/2(+Obj1, +Obj2) that succeeds when the minimum row of Obj1 is strictly less than the minimum row of Obj2.

FR-SC-018: The pack SHALL provide sc_below/2(+Obj1, +Obj2) that succeeds when sc_above(Obj2, Obj1) holds.

FR-SC-024: The pack SHALL provide sc_same_color/2(+Obj1, +Obj2) that succeeds when both objects have the same color.

Section 48.3 - Implementation and Placement

Implementation: pack scene in packs/scene/prolog/scene.pl (PR 58). Tests in packs/scene/test/test_scene.pl. Architecture placement: PART ARCH-24, Layer 37.

Dependencies: library(lists) for member/2, append/3, delete/3, last/2, min_list/2, max_list/2, sum_list/2; library(pairs) for pairs_values/2; library(apply) for maplist/3, foldl/4; library(grid) from PR 56 for gd_colors/2, gd_color_count/3, gd_objects/3, gd_bounding_box/3, gd_size/3.

The obj(Color, Cells) representation: an object is a term with two fields. Color is an integer 0-9. Cells is a sorted list of r(Row, Col) terms representing the cells of the object. The cell list is in the order returned by gd_objects/3 (BFS order within the connected component).

The background color determination: sc_bg_color/2 uses findall to build Count-Color pairs, then max_member/2 to select the pair with the greatest numeric Count. This is the correct approach for grids where the background occupies the majority of cells, as in most ARC-AGI tasks.

The named predicate principle: the helper predicate make_obj/3 is a named predicate (not a YALL lambda) used with maplist to wrap cell sets in obj terms. Named predicates create fresh activation frames on each call. The YALL lambda alternative would bind the Color variable in the first call and fail to rebind it correctly in subsequent calls when used with foldl across multiple colors. See Pseudocode Part 48 for the detailed explanation.

Section 48.4 - ARC-AGI-2 Relevance

The scene pack addresses the "larger object inventories" challenge identified in ARC-AGI-2_Next_Steps.txt. Many ARC-AGI-2 tasks require the solver to simultaneously track multiple objects, reason about their relative positions, identify which objects have the same shape, and determine which object is largest or smallest. All of these capabilities are provided by the scene pack.

The spatial relation predicates (sc_above, sc_below, sc_left_of, sc_right_of, sc_cells_touching, sc_contained_in) support context-dependent dispatch: tasks where the transformation rule depends on which object is above, below, or touching another object.

PART 49 - QUANT PACK SPECIFICATION

Section 49.1 - Purpose

The quant pack (PR 59) provides quantitative reasoning over object sets. It answers questions about counts, groupings, frequencies, and uniformity properties of collections of obj(Color, Cells) terms produced by the scene pack. The pack is general-purpose: it supports any domain where reasoning requires counting, comparing, or grouping structured objects by attribute. The ARC-AGI-2 benchmark motivated the pack design; it is equally applicable to any object-set reasoning problem.

Section 49.2 - Functional Requirements
